# Chapter 14 — Pitching and Fundraising Mechanics

Y Combinator ran a mock pitch exercise once, live, in front of a class. A founder played
himself. A partner played an investor. The founder described his company as "a
communication platform that will allow businesses and consumers to collaborate on one
single platform." Asked what the product actually does, he circled the same sentence
again, slightly reworded. Asked about traction, he said he didn't want to "open the
kimono." Asked about the business model, he said they hadn't precisely figured it out
yet, but were thinking maybe a monthly fee, they weren't sure how much. The investor
ended the meeting politely and moved on.

Then they ran it again, with a second, better-prepared founder pitching a similar kind
of idea. This one opened with something concrete: "when you walk into a Crate and
Barrel, you can send the manager a message like, 'there's puke in the hallway.'" Pressed
on traction, he gave real numbers without flinching: 350 locations, growing 11% a week,
three months in. Pressed on what he'd learned, he offered something the investor didn't
already know: more than half the messages sent through the product weren't feedback at
all — they were simple questions, like "when do you open?" Asked about the business
model, he said plainly that they didn't have a fully settled answer yet, and explained
the two paths they were actually testing. At the end, he made a specific, real ask: a
defined amount, on defined terms, with part of the round already committed by named
investors, closing on a specific date.

Same format, same amount of time, two completely different outcomes. This chapter is
about the difference between those two pitches, and about the mechanics of fundraising
that surround the pitch itself — when to start, how to get the meeting, and what happens
after.

## The Core Idea: Thirty Seconds, Then Two Minutes, Nothing Longer

Michael Seibel, a YC partner who has founded two companies himself, argues that a
founder needs exactly two versions of their pitch memorized: one that takes thirty
seconds, and one that takes two minutes. Nothing longer is actually necessary, and
longer pitches tend to hurt more than they help — every additional minute of talking is
another chance to say something an investor doesn't like, with no offsetting benefit.

The **thirty-second pitch** is three plain sentences: what the company does, how big the
market is, and how much traction exists so far. The **two-minute pitch** adds four more
components on top of that foundation: a specific insight nobody else has, how the
company makes money, who the team is, and — only in this longer version — the actual
ask. Everything in this chapter is really just an elaboration of these seven pieces, and
the discipline of keeping each one to a sentence or two is not a stylistic preference. It
forces a founder to know, with total clarity, what actually matters about their own
company.

## Why It's True: What Each Sentence Has to Do, and Why the Mechanics Matter as Much as the Words

### The thirty-second pitch: what, how big, how fast

The first sentence has to describe the product in language so simple that a
non-technical relative would understand it — Seibel's own name for this is the "mom
test," a direct echo of the one-sentence clarity rule Chapter 2 already established for
evaluating ideas in the first place. If the honest, simple version of the sentence sounds
underwhelming, that's still better than a longer, vaguer sentence that sounds impressive
but doesn't actually explain anything — Ron Conway's complaint in Chapter 13, that a
quarter of founders can't clear this bar, is describing exactly this failure.

The second sentence states the market size, aiming at a number in the billions where a
credible one exists — not because the number itself proves anything, but because it
tells an investor whether the company could ever become one of the outliers Chapter 13
described. The third sentence states traction: real growth numbers if they exist, or, if
the company hasn't launched yet, evidence of how fast the team is moving — a launched
beta within weeks of starting, for instance — since speed is the best available
substitute for traction when traction doesn't exist yet.

### The two-minute pitch: insight, money, team, ask

The two-minute version adds four things, each disciplined down to a sentence or two.

**Unique insight** is the specific thing the founder understands that the biggest
players in the space either don't understand or haven't acted on — the "aha" moment an
investor should feel on hearing it. If the insight produces a shrug of recognition
rather than genuine surprise, it hasn't been sharpened enough yet.

**How you make money** should be stated plainly, using the actual, unhedged mechanism —
advertising, direct sales, subscription, whatever it genuinely is — rather than a list of
several possible models offered as if to hedge against being wrong. Naming several
possible business models at once doesn't read as flexible. It reads as not yet having
figured out the business, which is a different and worse impression to leave.

**Team** should mention truly impressive past accomplishments if they exist (having
built something that made real money for people, for instance) and otherwise stick to
concrete, checkable facts: how many founders, how many are technical, how long the team
has known each other, whether everyone is committed full-time, and how the team actually
met. Padding this section with unrelated academic credentials or generic descriptors
tends to draw attention to what's missing rather than what's there.

**The ask** is the one place in the entire pitch where using precise financial jargon is
correct instead of a warning sign. Everywhere else in this chapter, plain language is the
goal — but stating the ask vaguely ("we're raising some money") signals a founder who
hasn't done the homework, in a section where the homework is exactly what an investor is
checking for. The ask needs the exact instrument (a convertible note or a SAFE), the
valuation cap, the total amount being raised, and the minimum check size an investor
could write. A founder who doesn't know these numbers cold isn't ready to be having this
conversation yet — Chapter 15 covers what each of these terms actually means in enough
depth to use them confidently.

### The two live pitches, side by side

The contrast from this chapter's opening is worth breaking into its specific,
transferable lessons, because each failure in the first pitch maps directly onto one of
the seven components above. The weak pitch never landed a clear one-sentence
description, even after several attempts — a direct failure of the "mom test." It
avoided giving real numbers when asked about traction, using the phrase "don't want to
open the kimono" instead of either real data or an honest "we're pre-launch." Its
business model answer hedged across several unconsidered options rather than committing
to an actual mechanism. Its team description was vague and unconvincing rather than
concrete. And it never arrived at an actual ask at all — the meeting simply ran out of
time.

The stronger pitch did the opposite at every single point. Its one-sentence description
used a specific, vivid, concrete example (the Crate and Barrel "there's puke in the
hallway" message) instead of an abstract category description. Its traction numbers were
exact and unhesitating. Its unique insight was genuinely surprising — that most messages
sent weren't complaints at all, but simple logistical questions, an insight the company
had only discovered by building the product and watching real behavior, not by guessing
in advance. Its business-model answer was honest about genuine uncertainty while still
demonstrating that two specific paths were actively being tested, rather than a vague
shrug. Its team answer included a past failure, disclosed plainly rather than hidden, and
concrete facts about who the founders were and how they'd worked together. And it closed
with a fully specified ask — dollar amount, instrument, valuation cap, already-committed
capital from named investors, and a specific closing date — that gave the investor
everything needed to actually say yes on the spot.

### When to actually start, and how to set up the meetings

Investors, unsurprisingly, prefer to invest based on real traction — so the honest
strategic question isn't just "am I ready," it's "how do I put myself in the strongest
possible position before I start." If traction doesn't exist yet, two substitutes carry
real weight: whether investors are already proactively asking to be involved (a strong
signal you're already talking about the company effectively), and whether the team has
already minimized how much money is actually needed to get to market — Michael Seibel's
estimate is that the large majority of startups can get an initial version of a product
built and launched on very little capital. The goal in either case is the same: never
put yourself in a position where the pitch implicitly says "we can't do anything until
you fund us." The stronger, and usually true, position is "this is moving already, with
or without you" — which flips the power dynamic in the room in a way no amount of polish
on the pitch itself can substitute for.

Once you're ready to start real conversations, the practical mechanics matter almost as
much as the pitch content. A warm introduction — from another founder, or from a
previous investor — is close to mandatory; a cold approach with no connection starts
from a real disadvantage. Fundraising should run as a sprint, not a marathon: schedule as
many meetings as possible in the same tight window, rather than spreading them out over
months, so that offers and momentum can compound instead of going stale one at a time. A
useful, slightly counterintuitive tactic for creating that clustering: when investors
respond with interest, ask to meet in two or three weeks rather than immediately, citing
real work you're heads-down on — this naturally bunches meetings into the same period
across every investor you're talking to, and it signals, correctly, that you're not
desperate for the money right now. Finally, one person on the founding team — not the
whole company — should own the fundraising process, so the rest of the team can keep
building instead of getting pulled into a process that, per Chapter 13, is supposed to be
one of the easier and smaller parts of the company's life.

## Common Mistakes

- **Failing the "mom test" on the very first sentence.** If a non-technical relative
  couldn't repeat back what the company does, the sentence needs more work before
  anything else in the pitch matters.
- **Hedging the business model across several possible options instead of stating the
  real one.** This reads as not having figured out the business yet, not as
  admirable flexibility.
- **Being evasive about traction instead of giving real numbers, or an honest
  "pre-launch."** "I don't want to open the kimono" tells an investor there's something
  to hide, whether or not that's true.
- **Vague deal terms in "the ask."** This is the one part of the pitch where precise
  jargon is required, not optional — an imprecise ask signals a founder who isn't
  actually ready to close a deal.
- **Spreading investor meetings out over months instead of clustering them into a
  tight window.** Fundraising momentum compounds when meetings happen close together and
  fades when they're spread thin.
- **Pitching from a position of needing the money to do anything at all**, instead of
  demonstrating the company is already moving regardless of whether this particular
  investor says yes.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Doesn't "use precise jargon in the ask" contradict this book's general preference for
plain language?** No, and it's worth being explicit about why the two don't conflict.
Chapters 1 and 2 argued for plain, jargon-free language when *describing an idea*,
because jargon there usually hides a lack of clarity. The ask is a different kind of
statement — a specific, checkable financial term, not a description of what a product
does — and precision there signals competence rather than obscuring anything. The rule
across this whole book is really one rule, not two: use the simplest language that is
still fully accurate for what you're actually saying.

**What if you genuinely have no traction and no way to fake speed either?** This is a
real, common position for a first-time, pre-launch founder, and it's worth being honest
that the "how much traction" sentence is weakest exactly here. The available substitutes
— team commitment (everyone full-time), evidence of speed (what's shipped in the last
few weeks), and a sharp, specific "why now" (Chapter 2) — won't fully replace real
traction, but they're the honest, available alternative, and overstating traction that
doesn't exist is a worse option than admitting its absence plainly.

**Is it ever fine to skip a formal ask and just build relationships first?** Yes — plenty
of real fundraising conversations start as relationship-building meetings with no
immediate ask attached, particularly with investors a founder expects to approach again
later. The seven-part structure in this chapter describes what a meeting where you *are*
asking for money should contain, not a requirement for every single investor
conversation a founder ever has.

## Applying the Test

Write out your own thirty-second and two-minute pitches, using the exact structure from
this chapter — one sentence per component, nothing longer. Then read the thirty-second
version out loud to someone with no context on your company or industry. If they can't
repeat back what your company does afterward, in their own words, the first sentence
isn't done yet, no matter how good the rest of the pitch is.

## Chapter Summary

- Memorize exactly two pitches: thirty seconds (what, market size, traction) and two
  minutes (adds unique insight, business model, team, and the ask).
- Use the "mom test" for the one-sentence description — the same clarity standard
  Chapter 2 established for evaluating ideas in the first place.
- State the business model plainly, using the real mechanism, rather than hedging
  across several possible options.
- The ask is the one place precise financial jargon belongs — vague deal terms signal a
  founder who isn't actually ready to close.
- Never pitch from a position that implies the company can't move without this specific
  investor's money — demonstrate it's already moving regardless.
- Get warm introductions, cluster meetings into a tight window rather than spreading them
  out, and have one person own the fundraising process rather than distracting the whole
  team.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** List the seven components of the full two-minute pitch, and explain why
   the ask is the one place where precise jargon is appropriate rather than a warning
   sign.
2. **Apply:** Write your own thirty-second and two-minute pitches for a real or
   hypothetical company, holding strictly to one or two sentences per component.
3. **Apply:** Test your one-sentence description on someone unfamiliar with your
   industry, using the "mom test." Revise it based on what they couldn't repeat back
   correctly.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** The strong pitch in this chapter's cold open disclosed
   a past failed startup honestly rather than hiding it. Are there situations where
   disclosing a past failure this openly would hurt a founder's credibility rather than
   help it, and how would you tell the difference in advance?
