# Chapter 3 — Monopoly, Not Competition

In 2012, U.S. airlines brought in about $195 billion in domestic revenue. Google brought
in a little over $50 billion worldwide. By revenue alone, airlines look nearly four
times more important than Google. But look at profit instead of revenue, and the picture
flips completely. Airlines have been flying passengers for a hundred years, and across
that entire century, their cumulative profit is close to zero. Companies in the industry
make money for a while, go bankrupt, get bought or restructured, and the cycle repeats.
Google, over a much shorter life, has piled up enormous profit margins. The airline
industry's total stock market value is roughly a quarter of Google's alone — one
company, not even one industry.

Peter Thiel, who co-founded PayPal and Palantir and built one of Silicon Valley's most
influential venture firms, uses this comparison to make a single point that this chapter
is built around: **being big is not the same as being valuable.** What separates
airlines from Google isn't the size of the market they're in. It's whether they have a
monopoly. This chapter is about what that word actually means, why nearly every
successful startup is trying — quietly — to build one, and why the businesses that admit
this out loud are rare.

## The Core Idea: Two Numbers, and Only Two Kinds of Business

Thiel starts with a simple formula for how valuable a company is. Two things have to be
true:

> A company creates **X** dollars of value for the world, and captures **Y** percent of
> that value for itself.

The critical point is that X and Y are **independent**. A company can create an enormous
amount of value (a large X) and still capture almost none of it (a small Y) — that's the
airline industry, and as this chapter will get to later, it's also most of science. Or a
company can create a comparatively modest amount of value and still capture a large slice
of it, which is enough to make it worth more than an industry many times its size. Google
does both: it created a lot of value, and it captured an unusually large share of it.
Value creation alone tells you almost nothing about a company's worth. Capture is the
other half of the equation, and it's the half most people forget to ask about.

Thiel's own title for this argument, delivered with a straight face to a room of
ambitious Stanford students, was blunter than anything in this chapter so far:
**"competition is for losers."**

Thiel's next claim is more provocative: there are really only two kinds of businesses.
There's **perfect competition**, where many companies sell similar things, nobody can
charge much above cost, and — this is the part people miss — almost nobody makes money.
And there's **monopoly**, a business with no serious competitor doing what it does,
which is stable, can plan years ahead, and usually makes real money. "There is shockingly
little that is in between," in Thiel's words. Most businesses aren't a blend of the two.
They're much closer to one end of the spectrum than people assume, and the reason that's
hard to see is the subject of the next section.

> **Terminology note:** the qualities that make a monopoly durable — the things that
> make it hard for a competitor to attack — are often bundled today under the word
> "moat," a term popularized by Warren Buffett and widely used across Silicon Valley.
> This chapter will get to what actually builds a moat, but it's worth knowing the word,
> since you'll see it used everywhere from here on in the book.

## Why It's True: The Lies, the Small Start, and the Characteristics That Last

### Everyone lies about which one they are, in opposite directions

If monopoly and competition were easy to tell apart, this chapter would be much shorter.
They're hard to tell apart because of a specific, structural reason: **companies have an
incentive to lie about which one they are, and the lie runs in opposite directions
depending on which one they actually are.**

A real monopoly has every reason to hide it. Admitting you dominate a market invites
regulators, journalists, and antitrust lawyers. So a monopoly describes its market as
enormous and crowded with competition — the opposite of the truth.

A company stuck in real competition has the opposite incentive. Nobody wants to invest
in a business with no way to make money, so a struggling, undifferentiated company
describes its market as narrow and unique — again, the opposite of the truth.

Thiel gives a blunt example of the second kind of lie. Restaurants are famously bad
businesses — too much competition, thin margins, nobody keeps their capital for long — so
a new restaurant owner looking for investors won't say "I'm opening a restaurant." They
say something like, "We're the only British restaurant in Palo Alto." That sounds like a
real, defensible niche. It's actually a meaningless one: nothing stops a customer from
driving to Mountain View, and there probably isn't a large population of people who eat
only British food. Thiel's phrase for this kind of manufactured niche: "the something of
somewhere is really mostly just the nothing of nowhere — it's like the Stanford of North
Dakota." One of a kind, maybe. Not actually Stanford.

The same move happens with startup pitches: sharing, mobile, and social, mashed together
into a single business, describing the *intersection* of several huge categories as if
that intersection were both real and valuable. Sometimes it is. Often it's a warning sign
disguised as a differentiator.

Now look at the opposite lie, from an actual monopoly. Google, which had roughly
two-thirds of the search market at the time Thiel gave this lecture, almost never
describes itself as a search engine. Depending on which regulator or reporter is asking,
it describes itself as:

- an **advertising company** — in which case search ads are a small fraction (roughly
  3.5%) of the ~$500 billion global advertising market
- a **technology company** — in which case it's "competing" with car companies on
  self-driving, Apple on phones, Facebook on social, Microsoft on office software, and
  Amazon on cloud computing, all at once

Both descriptions describe the *union* of many huge markets rather than the actual
market Google dominates. The real number — a company with roughly two-thirds share of
search — sounds like exactly what it is: a much bigger, more durable monopoly than
Microsoft ever had on operating systems in the 1990s. That's precisely why Google avoids
saying it.

The pattern to remember: **a non-monopoly describes its market as an intersection, to
sound uniquely differentiated. A monopoly describes its market as a union, to sound
like just one competitor among many.** Once you know to look for this, you'll notice it
in almost every pitch and every regulatory filing you read.

One piece of outside evidence for how real this pattern is: Apple, Google, Microsoft, and
Amazon have spent years accumulating cash faster than they know what to do with it. That
kind of profit margin, sustained over years, is close to impossible in real competition.
It's a signature of monopoly-like economics, whatever the company's public description
of its market says.

### Build small, take over completely, then expand

If monopolies are valuable and competition mostly isn't, the obvious next question is how
a startup — which starts with nothing — ever gets to be one. Thiel's answer is the same
one Sam Altman reaches independently in Chapter 2, which is worth pausing on: two people
teaching different parts of the same course, coming from different backgrounds
(Altman as an investor and founder, Thiel as an investor and founder from an earlier
generation of companies), land on an identical structure without citing each other.
That's a decent sign the structure is actually true rather than just a fashionable
opinion.

The structure: start in a market small enough that you can take almost all of it, then
expand outward in the same concentric rings from Figure 2.1. Thiel's examples put real
numbers on this pattern:

- **Amazon** started as an online bookstore — a better bookstore than any physical
  competitor, since it could offer far more titles — before expanding into every other
  category of e-commerce.
- **eBay** started with people trading Pez dispensers, moved to Beanie Babies, and only
  later became the platform for practically any category of good sold at auction.
- **PayPal** started with eBay's "power sellers," a group of only about 20,000 people.
  Thiel and his cofounders reached roughly 25–30% penetration of that tiny market within
  two to three months of launch, and used that foothold to expand from there.
- **Facebook** started with about 10,000 people at Harvard, and reached roughly 60%
  of that market within ten days.

In every case, a business-school-style market-size analysis, done on day one, would have
concluded the company was worthless: a market of 20,000 eBay sellers, or 10,000 students
at a single university, looks too small to matter. What that analysis misses is the
concentric expansion — the fact that dominating a tiny market completely is what makes
the next, larger ring reachable at all.

The clean tech boom of 2005–2008 is Thiel's cautionary example of doing the opposite.
Nearly every clean tech pitch deck from that period opened with the total size of the
global energy market — trillions of dollars. Starting there put every one of those
companies in the position of "a minnow in a vast ocean," competing against more rivals
than they could even count, with no way to carve out the kind of dominant foothold that
Amazon or PayPal built early on. Bigger, in this case, was worse, not better — it's the
same mistake Chapter 2 already warned against (starting at the outer ring of Figure 2.1
instead of the center), just at a larger scale and with more money burned proving it.

### What actually makes a monopoly last

Getting small and dominating it is only half the problem. The other half is durability —
staying dominant once you've gotten there. Thiel names four characteristics that build a
durable monopoly, and it's worth being honest that no single one of them is required; the
best companies usually have more than one.

| Characteristic | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Proprietary technology | Something meaningfully better than the next-best alternative — Thiel's rough rule of thumb is at least 10 times better on some important dimension | PayPal moved money roughly 10x faster than the checks it replaced; the iPhone worked as a smartphone in a way nothing before it really did |
| Network effects | The product gets more valuable to each user as more people join | Hard to start — there has to be a reason the first users show up before the network exists — but once it takes off, it tends to get stronger, not weaker, over time |
| Economies of scale | High fixed costs, very low costs to serve each additional customer | Software is unusually good at this: the marginal cost of one more user is close to zero, so a company that gets ahead can pull away fast |
| Branding | A perception that gets lodged in people's minds | Real, but Thiel is candid that he doesn't fully understand how it works, and avoids investing in a company on branding alone |

Two of these deserve a further point. **Proprietary technology** needs to keep winning,
not just win once — disk-drive manufacturers in the 1980s kept out-innovating each other
by wide margins for over a decade, which was fantastic for consumers and terrible for the
manufacturers, since none of them ever got to keep the advantage long enough to become
durably profitable. **Economies of scale** is where software has a genuine structural
edge over almost every other kind of business: zero marginal cost combined with the
ability to reach users instantly, worldwide, means a software company that gets a real
lead can turn that lead into a durable monopoly faster than almost any other kind of
company in history.

### Last mover, not first mover

Here's the part of Thiel's argument that inverts a piece of standard startup wisdom.
Everyone talks about the value of being a "first mover." Thiel argues you should be
aiming to be the **last** mover instead — the final company to enter a category and the
one that's still standing at the end of it. Microsoft was the last operating system
that mattered, for decades. Google was the last search engine that mattered. Whether
Facebook turns out to be the last major social network is, in Thiel's framing, the real
test of whether it was ever a monopoly at all.

There's a chess analogy Thiel likes for this: the player with white pieces (the first
mover) gets a real but small edge — something like a third of a pawn's worth of
advantage. It's real, but it's not decisive. World chess champion José Raúl Capablanca's
advice was the opposite of "move first and don't look back": "You must begin by studying
the endgame." The question that matters for a monopoly isn't who got there first. It's
who's still there in fifteen years.

This connects to something concrete and checkable: where a company's *value* actually
comes from. Thiel did this exercise for PayPal in March 2001, when the company was 27
months old, growing at 100% a year, and using a 30% discount rate on future cash flows.
The result: roughly three-quarters of PayPal's value at that moment came from cash flows
the model expected in 2011 and beyond — a full decade out. Run the same kind of analysis
on almost any fast-growing tech company, Thiel argues, and you get a similar answer: the
overwhelming majority of the value sits far in the future, not in this year's growth
number. That has an uncomfortable implication for how outsiders judge these companies:
**growth is the thing everyone can measure right now, so it gets over-weighted. Whether
the company will still be around and still be winning in ten years is what actually
determines the value, and it's much harder to measure — which is exactly why it gets
under-weighted.**

### The history of value that got captured, and value that didn't

Zoom out further, and the X/Y framing from the start of this chapter explains a genuine
pattern across roughly 250 years of technological and scientific progress: most of it
created enormous value and captured almost none.

Science, as an institution, is close to a hundred-percent example of this. The
smartest physicist of the twentieth century works out special and general relativity —
foundational to enormous amounts of value created afterward — and doesn't become a
millionaire, let alone a billionaire, from either discovery. The railroads created
enormous value for the societies they connected and mostly went bankrupt doing it, because
there were too many competing railroads for any one of them to keep the profit. The
Wright brothers flew the first airplane and made essentially no money from having done
it. Even the first Industrial Revolution's textile mills, which delivered something like
5–7% efficiency gains *every single year* for sixty or seventy years — a staggering,
compounding run of real technological progress — didn't make the manufacturers who ran
them rich either. There were too many of them, competing the gains away as fast as they
appeared. Even the landed aristocracy, not the industrialists, still held most of
Britain's wealth by 1850.

By Thiel's account, there are really only two categories in that long stretch of history
where people captured serious value from genuine innovation, rather than just creating
it for everyone else. The first is **complex, vertically integrated monopolies** — Ford,
Standard Oil — where the advantage came not from one single breakthrough but from
coordinating an enormous number of moving pieces better than anyone else could. Thiel's
modern example is Tesla and SpaceX: neither company's edge, in his account, comes from
one single 10x technical breakthrough. It comes from vertical integration done more
thoroughly than competitors — Tesla controlling its own distribution instead of ceding
margin to dealers the way the rest of the auto industry does, or SpaceX bringing
subcontracting in-house instead of leaving itself exposed to suppliers who could
otherwise charge monopoly prices on critical parts.

The second category is **software**, for the structural reasons already covered above:
extreme economies of scale, and the ability to reach a global market almost instantly,
which lets a small starting market get dominated and expanded from before competitors
can catch up. This is, in Thiel's telling, the actual explanation for why Silicon Valley
has been so financially successful: not that software people are smarter, but that
software as a category is unusually well-suited to building the kind of monopoly this
whole chapter has been describing.

### Why we're drawn to competition anyway

If avoiding competition is clearly the better outcome, why do so many founders — and
whole industries — end up competing instead of monopolizing? Thiel's answer isn't just
practical. It's psychological, and he argues it runs deeper than most people are willing
to admit.

The word "ape" has meant both "primate" and "to imitate" since at least Shakespeare's
time. That's not a coincidence of English vocabulary. It's a hint about human nature: we
are, at some level, deeply imitative, and we tend to treat other people wanting the same
thing as validation rather than as a warning sign. Thiel's example: roughly twenty
thousand people move to Los Angeles every year hoping to become a movie star. About
twenty of them make it. Ask any one of those twenty thousand whether so many others
chasing the identical goal makes the goal feel more attractive or less, and most say more
— proof, in their minds, that the goal must be worth having, since so many people agree.

This gets built into people well before they ever start a company, through an education
system that runs almost entirely as a tournament: competing for grades, for college
admission, for the right internship, for the right first job. Henry Kissinger's
description of his own Harvard faculty is the sharpest version of the same point Thiel
has found: "The battles were so ferocious because the stakes were so small." That sounds,
on the surface, like a description of people behaving irrationally. Thiel's reading is
more specific — when the real differences between competitors are genuinely small,
people fight *harder*, not less hard, to manufacture a difference that feels real.

Thiel offers his own life as the personal version of the same trap. He describes himself
as "hypertracked" from early adolescence: a classmate wrote in his eighth-grade yearbook
that she knew he'd get into Stanford. Four years later, he did. From there he went to
Stanford Law School, and from Stanford Law into a top law firm in New York — exactly the
job everyone on the outside of it wanted, and exactly the job everyone already inside it
wanted out of. He left after seven months and three days. A colleague told him on his
way out, "I had no idea it was possible to escape from Alcatraz" — and then pointed out
that the door had been unlocked the whole time. All it had ever taken was walking out the
front door and not coming back. So much of that colleague's own identity, and the
identities of the people who stayed, had gotten wrapped up in winning a competition that
nobody had stopped to ask whether the prize was actually worth having.

None of this means competition doesn't make you better at whatever you're competing
at — Thiel is explicit that it does. Comparing yourself against the people around you is
a real way to improve. The cost is that it also crowds out a harder, more important
question: whether the thing you're getting better at is worth getting better at in the
first place. Thiel's closing image is worth carrying past this chapter: don't always go
through the tiny door that everyone else is rushing to get through. Go around the
corner, and look for the vast gate that nobody else is taking.

## Common Mistakes

- **Sizing your market by its current, obvious total instead of the smallest slice you
  can dominate first.** This is the same mistake as Chapter 2's "starting at the outer
  ring," restated here with the clean tech bubble as its most expensive historical
  example.
- **Describing your market as an "intersection" to sound unique, when it's actually
  small and fake.** ("The only British restaurant in Palo Alto.") If your market
  description only makes sense with several specific adjectives stacked together, be
  suspicious of it.
- **Chasing "first mover" status instead of asking whether you'll be the last one
  standing.** Being first is a small, real advantage. Being durable is the one that
  actually determines value.
- **Treating growth and durability as the same thing.** A company can be growing fast
  and still be in a business that's fundamentally competitive, with no lasting
  advantage — which means the growth won't hold once competitors catch up.
- **Investing in — or building toward — a single 10x technical breakthrough as the whole
  strategy, with no plan for what happens once someone else matches it.** Disk-drive
  manufacturers in the 1980s did this repeatedly, improved the world enormously, and
  made almost no money doing it.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Doesn't "last mover" contradict itself — Google and Facebook weren't literally first,
so how are they "last"?** This is worth resolving directly, because it's a real
tension in Thiel's own language. Google wasn't the first search engine — there were
several before it. Facebook wasn't the first social network; Reid Hoffman had already
started one, called SocialNet, back in 1997. What made each of them "last" wasn't
literal chronological order. It was being the first to nail one specific, important
dimension well enough that no one after them displaced it: Google was the first to use
an automated, algorithmic approach (PageRank) that was an order of magnitude better than
manually-curated search. Facebook was the first social network built around real
identity rather than invented personas. "Last mover" describes durability along the
dimension that actually mattered, not who launched a website first.

**Does this chapter's skepticism of "lean startup" methodology contradict the rest of
this book's emphasis on talking to users?** It looks that way, and it's worth resolving
directly rather than leaving the contradiction sitting unaddressed, since Chapters 7, 8,
and 16 all lean heavily on user interviews and rapid iteration as core practices. Thiel's
actual claim is narrower than "don't talk to users." His own words on this, from the same
lecture: founders should spend more effort trying to have "a virtual ESP link with the
public" — a direct, almost intuitive read on what people need — and less effort treating
iteration itself as the answer. His argument is that the *degree* of iteration and
customer-survey-driven development some founders rely on can become a substitute for
genuine insight and differentiation, rather than a source of it. Notice
that PayPal and Palantir — his own companies — both started in a deliberately narrow
market and both had a specific technical or strategic insight going in; they didn't
arrive at their strategy by surveying users into it. The reconciliation: talking to users
(the subject of Part Three of this book) is how you refine and validate an idea you
already have real conviction about. It's not a substitute for having that conviction in
the first place. A founder with no differentiated insight who iterates rapidly on user
feedback can still end up with a well-executed, undifferentiated product — competing, not
monopolizing.

**Is Thiel's four-characteristics model the only way to build a durable business?**
No — Thiel is explicit that there's no single formula, and that the vertically-integrated
model (Ford, Tesla, SpaceX) doesn't fit neatly into "proprietary technology, network
effects, economies of scale, branding" at all. It's really a fifth path: winning through
superior coordination of many ordinary pieces, rather than through one exceptional piece.
This matters if your business involves physical operations, logistics, or manufacturing,
where the software-style economies of scale don't automatically apply.

## Applying the Test: Is This a Monopoly, or a Competitive Business Wearing a Costume?

Take an idea, and try describing its market honestly in both directions.

First, describe it the way a struggling competitor would describe theirs: as narrowly and
specifically as you can, stacking on adjectives until it sounds unique. If that
description only sounds meaningful because of how many qualifiers you had to add, you've
probably found an "intersection" lie rather than a real, defensible position.

Second, describe it the way an actual monopoly downplays itself: as the union of several
much larger categories, so the real dominant position gets lost in a crowd of imagined
competitors. If your honest internal sense of the business — the one you'd only say out
loud to a cofounder — sounds a lot closer to the second description than the first, you
may actually be closer to a real monopoly than your public pitch admits.

The test that matters, per Thiel's own answer to this exact question in a Q&A after his
lecture: ignore the narrative in both directions, and ask what the *actual* market is —
not the story that makes you sound uniquely interesting, and not the story that makes you
sound reassuringly unthreatening. People, in his words, "have incentives to powerfully
distort these things," in both directions at once, and the only way through is to keep
asking what's real underneath the story.

## Chapter Summary

- A company's value depends on two independent things: how much value it creates (X) and
  what percentage of that value it captures (Y). Being big (a large X) doesn't
  automatically mean being valuable (a large X times Y).
- There are really only two kinds of businesses: monopolies, which are rare, stable, and
  profitable, and competitive businesses, where almost nobody makes money. There is very
  little in between.
- Monopolies downplay themselves to avoid regulators; competitive businesses inflate
  themselves to attract investors. The lies run in opposite directions, which is why the
  difference is hard to see from the outside.
- The path to monopoly starts small — a market so tiny it looks worthless on a
  spreadsheet — and expands outward in concentric rings, the same structure Chapter 2
  introduced from a completely different lecture.
- Durable monopolies usually have at least one of: proprietary technology roughly 10x
  better than the alternative, network effects, extreme economies of scale, or branding
  — and software is unusually well-suited to several of these at once.
- Aim to be the *last* mover in a category, not the first — durability, not speed to
  market, is what determines whether most of a company's value (often concentrated a
  decade or more in the future) actually gets realized.
- Across 250 years of innovation, most value created was never captured by the people
  who created it — science, railroads, and early industrial manufacturing all show this.
  The rare exceptions are complex vertically-integrated businesses and software.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** In your own words, explain why a monopoly and a struggling competitive
   business would describe their markets in exactly opposite ways, even though both are
   lying.
2. **Apply:** Take an idea you're evaluating. Write its market description twice — once
   the way a monopoly would understate it, and once the way a weak competitor would
   overstate it. Which one is closer to true?
3. **Apply:** Using the characteristics table in this chapter, identify which of
   proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, or branding your idea
   could plausibly have. If the honest answer is "none yet," what would need to be true
   in a year for that to change?
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues that heavy reliance on user
   surveys and rapid iteration can substitute for genuine differentiated insight rather
   than produce it. But Part Three of this book (particularly Chapters 7 and 8) treats
   direct user feedback as close to essential. Reconcile these two claims in your own
   words — what is each one actually arguing, and where exactly is the line between them?
