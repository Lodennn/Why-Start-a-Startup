# Chapter 10 — Retention Is the Root of Growth

When Alex Schultz joined Facebook's advertising team, the product he was hired to grow
was three days old. Within 90 days of that product launching — not a year, ninety
days — his team used early cohort data to predict what an advertiser's value would be to
the company over their entire first year. When the real number came in twelve months
later, the prediction had been accurate to within 97%.

That kind of forecast sounds like it should require years of data. It doesn't, because
of a specific technique this chapter is built around: instead of waiting to see what
happens to users over a year, you look at how *every* user behaves in their first days
and weeks, and let that early curve tell you where things are headed. This chapter is
about that technique, and about the deeper claim underneath it — that retention, not
acquisition, is the actual foundation everything else in growth gets built on.

## The Core Idea: Product, Then Customers, Then Retention — In That Order

Ask a room full of founders what matters most for growth, and most eventually arrive at
some version of the right answer, just not in the right order. A great product gets you
customers. But what determines whether those customers turn into real growth is whether
they **stay** — and whether, by staying, they tell other people. Schultz's own summary:
retention is the single most important thing for growth, full stop.

The practical tool for measuring this is a **retention curve**: on one axis, the number
of days since a user first showed up; on the other, what percentage of users from that
starting cohort are still active. Every product's curve starts at 100% on day one and
declines from there — the question that matters is *how* it declines. A curve that keeps
sloping down toward zero, no matter how long you wait, describes a product without real
product-market fit. A curve that bends and flattens out — becoming roughly parallel to
the x-axis instead of continuing toward zero — describes a product that has found a
real, durable core of users who aren't leaving. Adora Cheung, building Homejoy in a
completely different industry, arrived at the identical tool from the opposite
direction, using it to track cohort-by-cohort retention rather than growth-team
forecasting. Two speakers, two industries, one identical curve — which is itself decent
evidence the underlying pattern is real rather than a growth-team fashion.

The instruction that follows directly from this: if your own curve hasn't flattened
yet, do not hire a growth specialist, do not invest in virality mechanics, and do not
worry about acquisition channels. Go back to Chapter 2 and Chapter 3's foundations —
product-market fit has to exist before growth tactics have anything to amplify.

## Why It's True: What Good Retention Looks Like, North Stars, and the Marginal User

### Estimating what "good" even means, with almost no data

A common, reasonable-sounding question founders ask is some version of: is 5% monthly
retention good? Schultz's answer is that you can usually figure this out yourself with
publicly available numbers, using the same kind of rough, order-of-magnitude reasoning
that let physicists estimate the yield of a nuclear test from a single published
photograph, using nothing but the visible dimensions of the blast and some basic physics.
You don't need precision. You need a ballpark.

Applied to a real product: if there are roughly 2 billion people online worldwide, and
Facebook has around 1.3 billion active users, that ratio alone tells you Facebook has
captured a very large share of everyone who could plausibly use it — a signal, on its
own, that its retention must be extraordinarily strong. The same trick works for
WhatsApp, which had around 600 million active users, against the global smartphone
population, or Amazon against the population of its home market. None of these give you
an exact figure. All of them tell you whether you're in the right neighborhood.

The right neighborhood, importantly, is different for every category. Retaining 20–30%
of users on a monthly basis is a genuinely strong result for an e-commerce business. That
same number would be a catastrophic result for a social network, where the earliest
cohort of users typically needs to stay around 80% retained for the product to have any
real chance of becoming huge. Benchmarking your own retention against the wrong
category's expectations — comparing a social product's numbers to an e-commerce
company's, or the reverse — will make a genuinely fine result look like a failure, or a
genuinely bad one look acceptable.

### The North Star metric, and why it matters more once you're not in the room

Once retention shows a real, flattening curve, the next problem is coordinating an
entire company's day-to-day decisions around growing it. Schultz's advice here is
deliberately contrarian: a startup shouldn't build a separate "growth team" walled off
from the rest of the company. The whole company should function as the growth team, and
the CEO — not a specialized hire — should personally be the one setting the target
everyone else optimizes toward.

That target is what Schultz calls a **North Star metric**: a single number, chosen
deliberately, that the entire company holds itself accountable to. Facebook's version was
monthly active users, not total registrations — a specific decision by Mark Zuckerberg to
measure and publish the number that actually reflected engagement, rather than the
larger, more flattering number of everyone who'd ever signed up. WhatsApp measured
messages sent. Airbnb measured nights booked. eBay measured Gross Merchandise
Volume — the total value of goods moving through the platform — rather than eBay's own
revenue, since GMV was the number that actually captured how much commerce the platform
was enabling: eBay's GMV consistently ran at roughly **10 times** its reported revenue,
so a metric anchored to revenue alone would have badly understated the business. eBay's
own leaders — Pierre Omidyar, Meg Whitman, and John Donahoe, across their respective
tenures — consistently pointed back to that GMV figure rather than letting revenue stand
in for it.

The reason a single, clearly chosen metric matters this much connects back to an idea
this book already covered from a completely different angle: Patrick Collison's
observation, in Chapter 6, that culture is a solution to a bandwidth problem — the thing
that keeps decisions aligned once a founder can't personally be in every room. A North
Star metric does the identical job for growth decisions specifically. You cannot control
what a hundred different employees each decide to prioritize on a given afternoon. What
you can do is make sure they're all optimizing toward the same specific number, so that
even a decision made without you in the room tends to push in the right direction.

eBay's own affiliate program is a clean, concrete illustration of how much the *choice*
of metric shapes behavior. The company originally paid affiliates for every **confirmed
registered user** (CRU) they brought in — someone who signed up and verified their
account. Switching the payment model to reward **activated** confirmed registered users
(ACRUs) — people who went on to actually bid on or list an item — caused total CRUs to
drop by about 20% overnight. But ACRUs, the number that actually mattered, dropped by
only about 5%, and then began growing faster than before. The mechanism: paying for raw
registrations sent affiliates traffic straight to a generic sign-up page, with no
compelling reason to complete it. Paying for activation sent that same traffic to an
actual search results page — showing someone searching for a specific item exactly what
they wanted — which is what Schultz calls the **magic moment**: the specific point where
a new user genuinely understands, viscerally, what your product is for.

Every product has its own magic moment, and identifying it precisely is worth real
effort. Facebook's is seeing a friend's face in your feed for the first time — which is
why the company measured and optimized hard for getting new users to ten friends within
their first fourteen days, treating that specific number as a leading indicator of
whether the magic moment had landed. Airbnb's is finding the one listing you actually
want to stay in. eBay's is finding the exact collectible you've been looking for. None of
these are abstract — they're the specific instant a new user stops evaluating your
product and starts using it because they want to.

### Optimize for the user who's about to leave, not the user you already have

A subtle, easy-to-miss mistake Schultz calls out directly: when a company thinks about
its own product, it naturally thinks from the perspective of its most engaged users —
the people already using it constantly, the people most like the founders themselves.
Growth decisions made from that perspective are frequently wrong, because the most
engaged users are not the ones whose behavior determines whether the company keeps
growing.

His example is notifications. Power users complain about getting too many. The
founder's instinct, hearing that complaint, is to reduce notification volume across the
board. But the growth-relevant question isn't "are my best users annoyed" — it's "is the
person who's about to churn getting the one notification that would have brought them
back." Schultz's own growth-accounting framework at Facebook broke users into three
buckets: new users, resurrected users (people who'd been gone for 30+ days and came
back), and churned users. Once a product has been around for a few years, resurrected
and churned users dominate the numbers far more than new signups do — and the users in
both of those categories reliably had low friend counts and had never really been
connected to the product's magic moment in the first place. The fix wasn't reducing
notifications for everyone. It was increasing the *specific* notifications that helped
low-connection users reach the magic moment, while leaving already-engaged power users
alone.

## Common Mistakes

- **Investing in growth tactics before the retention curve has actually flattened.**
  Virality, paid acquisition, and dedicated growth hires all amplify a product that
  already works. None of them fix a product that doesn't.
- **Building a separate "growth team" instead of making growth the whole company's job,
  led personally by the CEO.**
- **Benchmarking retention against the wrong category.** A perfectly healthy e-commerce
  retention rate looks catastrophic by social-network standards, and the reverse is just
  as misleading.
- **Choosing a North Star metric that flatters the company rather than one that reflects
  real engagement** — total registrations instead of monthly active users, revenue
  instead of the volume of value actually flowing through the platform.
- **Designing growth decisions around your own experience, or your most engaged users',
  instead of the marginal user who is about to leave.**

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**What if the retention curve looks ambiguous — not clearly flattening, but not clearly
failing either?** This is a real, common case, and Chapter 7 already covered the
practical answer from a different angle: Cheung's advice was to set a specific,
honest growth plan in advance and treat three or four consecutive weeks of no
improvement as the signal to reconsider, rather than waiting for total clarity that may
never arrive. The retention curve and the growth-plan check are two views of the same
underlying question, and it's worth using both together rather than either alone.

**Does every company need exactly one North Star metric, forever?** Not necessarily
forever — a company's North Star can and sometimes should evolve as the business
matures. What matters more than permanence is clarity at any given time: everyone in the
company, at any moment, should be able to name the same single metric if asked, the way
eBay's leadership consistently pointed to Gross Merchandise Volume rather than letting
different teams quietly optimize for revenue, registrations, or listings instead.

**Isn't optimizing for the "marginal user" in tension with building a great product for
your best users?** Not in practice, though it can feel that way. Chapter 8 already
established that a great product requires deep, direct engagement with real users —
that's still true, and still necessary for product quality. The distinction Schultz is
making is narrower: product *quality* decisions can reasonably lean on your most engaged
users' feedback, but *growth* decisions specifically — the ones about who to notify,
re-engage, or push toward the magic moment — need to be evaluated from the perspective
of users who haven't gotten there yet, not the ones who already have.

## Applying the Test

Using only public information, make a rough estimate of your own product's plausible
retention ceiling the way Schultz estimated Facebook's: what's the total realistic
population who could ever use what you're building, and how does your current active
user count compare to it? Then identify your product's magic moment as specifically as
you can — the exact instant a new user would say "oh, *that's* what this is for" — and
check honestly whether your onboarding gets a new user there quickly, or buries it
behind several steps that have nothing to do with it.

## Chapter Summary

- Retention is the actual foundation of growth — a great product produces customers, and
  whether those customers stay is what determines whether the company actually grows.
- A retention curve that flattens (asymptotic) signals real product-market fit. A curve
  that keeps declining toward zero signals its absence — Cheung's cohort-analysis method
  from Chapter 7 and Schultz's growth-forecasting method arrive at the identical tool
  independently.
- If your retention curve hasn't flattened, don't invest in growth tactics yet — fix the
  product first.
- What counts as "good" retention differs by category — 20–30% monthly is strong for
  e-commerce; a social product typically needs closer to 80% in its earliest cohort.
- A single, clearly chosen North Star metric — not a separate growth team — keeps a whole
  company's decisions aligned even when the founder isn't in the room, the same role
  culture plays in Chapter 6.
- What you choose to measure and pay for shapes real behavior — eBay's shift from paying
  for registrations to paying for activated users changed what its affiliates actually
  did.
- Every product has a specific "magic moment" worth identifying precisely and driving
  new users toward as fast as possible.
- Growth decisions should be optimized for the marginal user who's about to leave, not
  for your own experience or your most engaged users.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain why a retention curve that keeps sloping toward zero is a signal
   to stop investing in growth tactics rather than a signal to invest harder in them.
2. **Apply:** Identify your own product's (or a product idea's) magic moment as
   specifically as you can. What does your current onboarding flow actually do to get a
   new user there quickly, and what's standing in the way?
3. **Apply:** Choose a North Star metric for a real or hypothetical product, and explain
   why it's a better choice than the two or three other metrics you considered and
   rejected.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues against having a dedicated growth
   team. But many well-known, successful companies do have dedicated growth teams and
   growth hires. Under what conditions does a dedicated growth team make sense, and how
   would you tell whether your company has actually earned the right to have one yet?
