# Chapter 12 — Getting Press

PayPal was in the news for freezing merchant accounts without much explanation, and
developers were furious about it. A YC company called WePay saw the opportunity, froze a
block of ice with real cash inside it, and dropped it outside a PayPal developer
conference — a stunt with essentially no production budget, tied directly to a story
already in the news, timed to a specific event where the right reporters would already
be standing around with nothing else to write about. It worked. WePay, a company nobody
had covered before, got attached to a story everybody was already covering.

That's the pattern this chapter is about: press isn't a meritocracy where the most
important company automatically gets covered. It's closer to a specific, learnable skill
— knowing what counts as a story, who to tell it to, and how to get a reporter's
attention without a budget most startups don't have anyway.

## The Core Idea: Goal and Audience Come Before Anything Else

The instinct many first-time founders have is to want press simply because press feels
like validation — a sign the company is real and important. Justin Kan's first, blunt
correction: if you don't have an actual goal for a piece of coverage, getting it
accomplishes nothing for the business, whatever it does for your own feeling of
momentum.

The goal determines the audience, and the audience determines which outlets are worth
targeting at all. Kan's own examples make the point concrete: Socialcam wanted to be
positioned as "video Instagram" for Silicon Valley investors and influencers, so tech
press was the target. Exec, a local cleaning service, only needed San Francisco
residents who could actually use the service — national coverage would have reached
almost entirely the wrong readers, so local outlets like the San Francisco Chronicle were
the real target. TwitchTV needed the gaming industry itself — developers and
advertisers — to see it as a serious, important place, so the target was gaming industry
trade publications and developer blogs, not general consumer press. Three completely
different audiences, three completely different outlets, because three completely
different business goals sat underneath each campaign.

## Why It's True: What Counts as a Story, and How the Mechanics Actually Work

### Not everything you do is a story

The single most useful filter Kan offers: before pitching anything, ask honestly whether
you'd want to read this story if you weren't the company's founder. Founders reliably
overestimate how interesting their own routine progress is — a minor feature update or a
small version bump rarely clears this bar, however meaningful it feels internally.

There are a handful of story types that reliably do clear it: product launches,
fundraising announcements (which the press covers readily even though, as Kan notes,
they're rarely genuinely interesting), real milestones and metrics, business-strategy
stories (usually reserved for companies already established enough to be worth
profiling), creative stunts tied to a live news moment (WePay's ice block), hiring
announcements for a sufficiently senior or newsworthy hire, and contributor or opinion
pieces written by the founder for an outlet.

A story also doesn't need to be original in an absolute sense — it needs to be, in Kan's
phrase, "original enough." Being the first company in a specific, narrow category to hit
a milestone gets covered; being the second gets ignored, even if the second company is
otherwise just as interesting. The first video game console to raise a large amount of
money on Kickstarter was genuine news, despite many other projects having raised
comparable amounts on Kickstarter before it in different categories — the specific
combination was new enough to count as a real story.

### Treat it like a sales funnel, and get the introduction from someone else's success

Most pitches to most reporters don't convert into coverage, and that's the normal,
expected outcome — not a sign the story or the company is bad. The practical mechanics
that improve the odds are worth following in order. The single highest-leverage move is
getting introduced to a reporter by someone that reporter has already covered
successfully, rather than cold-emailing. This benefits everyone involved: the founder
making the introduction loses nothing by connecting a reporter to another interesting
company, and the reporter, receiving a referral from someone they already decided was
worth covering, extends some of that trust to the new introduction by association.

Once in touch, give a reporter real lead time — roughly a week or more before any news
needs to run, not a same-day request. Reporters have their own schedules and existing
commitments; showing up with no notice reads as a lack of professionalism rather than
urgency. Where possible, get an actual phone call or in-person meeting rather than
relying only on email exchanges. There's a real, human mechanism behind why this works:
the more time and attention a reporter has already invested in a conversation, the more
likely they are to follow through and actually write the story, simply because that
invested time now feels wasted if they don't.

The pitch itself should be prepared in advance, as a specific set of bullet points
memorized well enough to guide the conversation naturally, so the reporter's own notes
end up capturing what the founder actually wanted covered — the cofounder's name spelled
right, the specific feature or metric that matters, the framing that fits the goal set
at the very start. A short follow-up close to the actual publication date, with
logistics and any supporting material (photos, video, correct spellings), closes the
loop.

### PR firms can't tell you what's interesting about your own company

Firms exist to manage contacts and logistics at scale, and that's a real, if limited,
value. What they generally cannot do is figure out what's actually newsworthy about a
specific company — that judgment has to come from the founder, who understands the
business, the product, and the goal well enough to make the call themselves. Kan's own
experience with PR firms, across several companies, is that they've never once told him
something genuinely interesting about his own company that he hadn't already figured out
— only handed him a list of reporters to contact. Combined with real cost (commonly
somewhere between five and twenty thousand dollars a month), the case for hiring a firm
early is weak. Learning the mechanics in this chapter yourself, first, is both cheaper
and more effective — and it's a skill that transfers to every future round of press the
company will ever need, in a way that outsourcing the whole function from day one
doesn't build.

### Press is a vanity metric, and it doesn't scale

It's worth stating plainly, because the temptation to conflate the two is strong: getting
covered feels like succeeding, but it isn't the same thing as succeeding. A company can
generate a steady stream of press with a flat usage graph and no revenue, and a company
can be genuinely thriving with almost no coverage at all. Press correlates loosely with
real success because successful companies tend to attract more of it over time — but the
correlation runs in that direction, not the reverse, and chasing coverage as a substitute
for the metrics covered in Chapters 10 and 11 (retention, growth, revenue) mistakes a
symptom for a cause.

It's also worth being explicit about a limit this chapter's advice runs into: press works
well as a bootstrapping tool for a company's first hundred, or few hundred, customers —
exactly the audience a single well-placed story can plausibly reach. It does not scale
into an ongoing acquisition channel the way the tactics in Chapter 11 can, because there
is no way to generate an unlimited supply of stories a reporter will find genuinely new.
Once a company needs sustained, large-volume growth, this chapter's tools stop being the
right tool for that specific job, even though they remain useful for the goals — investor
perception, industry credibility, hiring — press is actually good at achieving.

### Maintaining relationships pays off later, in both directions

Once a reporter has covered a company, staying in touch — without an immediate ask
attached — makes it more likely that reporter covers the company again later, simply
because there's now a real relationship rather than a one-time transaction. This matters
in both good times and bad: if a company ever faces negative coverage, having an existing
relationship with at least a few reporters makes it meaningfully easier to get a fair
hearing and get the company's own side of the story included.

The same logic extends outward, not just backward: recommending other founders' stories
to reporters, even when there's no direct benefit to you, tends to come back around,
because reporters remember who reliably sends them good leads, and other founders
remember who helped connect them.

Twitch's own biggest viral moment, "Twitch Plays Pokémon," illustrates how this all fits
together honestly. The company didn't invent the idea — a segment of its own community
did, entirely organically. Twitch's actual contribution was making sure the groundwork
was already in place: enough prior, smaller press coverage that when a major outlet like
the BBC went looking for context on this strange new internet phenomenon, there was
already a trail of earlier stories to draw on, and a company ready and available to talk
to reporters and suggest natural follow-up angles as the story kept developing. The
company didn't manufacture the moment. It had done the unglamorous relationship-building
work in advance that let it capitalize on a moment it didn't create.

## Common Mistakes

- **Pursuing press without a specific goal or target audience.** Coverage that reaches
  the wrong readers doesn't accomplish anything, however good it feels.
- **Pitching a story that isn't actually a story** — a minor update that wouldn't
  interest anyone who isn't already invested in the company's success.
- **Cold-emailing reporters instead of getting a warm introduction from someone they've
  already covered.**
- **Giving a reporter no lead time**, then being surprised when a same-day request goes
  nowhere.
- **Hiring a PR firm early, expecting it to identify what's interesting about the
  company.** That judgment has to come from the founder.
- **Treating press coverage as a proxy for real business success**, or as a scalable
  growth channel once a company needs volume rather than an initial hundred customers.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Doesn't calling press "a vanity metric" contradict recommending it as a tool for the
first hundred customers?** Not really — the two claims describe different uses of the
same tool. Press is a legitimate, useful bootstrapping mechanism at very small scale,
precisely because a single story genuinely can reach a company's first cohort of users.
The warning is against mistaking that early, real usefulness for a signal of the
company's overall health, or expecting the same mechanism to keep working at the volume
Chapter 11's growth channels are built for.

**Is there ever a good time to hire a PR firm?** Yes, once a company has a genuine,
ongoing cadence of newsworthy events and the volume of contact management becomes a real
logistical burden rather than a judgment problem — at that point, a firm's actual
strength (managing relationships and logistics at scale) becomes relevant. The mistake
this chapter warns against is hiring one early, when the actual bottleneck is deciding
what's interesting, not managing contact lists.

**What if a company can't identify a "goal" for press beyond general visibility?**
That's a signal worth taking seriously rather than working around — if there's no
specific business objective a piece of coverage would advance, that's a sign this isn't
the right time to be pursuing press at all, not a sign to lower the bar for what counts
as a goal.

## Applying the Test

Before pitching any story, write down two things separately: the specific business goal
this coverage is meant to advance, and the specific audience that goal implies. Then
apply the honesty test directly — if you had no connection to this company, would you
actually want to read the story you're about to pitch? If the honest answer is no, the
fix isn't better writing. It's a different story, or waiting until there's a real one to
tell.

## Chapter Summary

- Press is not a meritocracy — define your specific business goal and target audience
  before pitching anything, since different goals point to completely different outlets.
- A story has to clear a real bar: would you want to read it if you weren't the founder?
  Most routine updates don't clear it.
- A story doesn't need to be original — it needs to be original enough, usually meaning
  first in some specific, narrow category.
- Get warm introductions from founders a reporter has already covered, rather than cold
  emailing; give real lead time; prepare a memorized bullet-point pitch in advance.
- PR firms manage contacts and logistics well but can't tell you what's actually
  interesting about your company — that judgment has to be the founder's.
- Press correlates with looking successful, but it isn't the same as being successful,
  and it doesn't scale past a company's first few hundred customers the way real growth
  channels do.
- Maintain reporter relationships even without an immediate ask, and help other founders
  get coverage — both tend to come back around later.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain why "original enough" is a more useful standard than "completely
   original" when deciding whether something is a pitchable story.
2. **Apply:** Pick a real or hypothetical company. Define its specific press goal and
   target audience, the way Kan did for Socialcam, Exec, and TwitchTV. What outlets
   would actually reach that audience?
3. **Apply:** Draft the bullet-point pitch you'd bring into a reporter meeting for a
   real or hypothetical story, structured so a reporter's own notes would capture what
   you actually wanted covered.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues press doesn't scale as a growth
   channel past a company's first few hundred customers. Are there business models or
   company types where ongoing press coverage genuinely does function as a durable,
   scalable growth channel? What would have to be true about that business for it to
   work?
