# Chapter 20 — How to Manage

A genuinely good employee walks into a CEO's office and asks for a raise. Every instinct
in the room points toward saying yes: this person is talented, you want to keep them,
saying no feels petty, and saying yes costs you nothing you weren't already prepared to
eventually pay. Ben Horowitz, who has run companies and sat on the other side of this
exact conversation many times, argues that the instinct to just say yes is almost always
wrong — not because the raise isn't deserved, but because of a question nobody in the
room is asking: what happens to the equally good employee, sitting two desks away, doing
equally strong work, who never thought to ask?

That second employee, once they eventually notice the difference, draws a rational and
corrosive conclusion: performance isn't actually what determines pay here. Asking is. And
once that conclusion spreads, every good employee in the company now has, in Horowitz's
phrase, "a fiduciary responsibility to their family" to start asking constantly, whether
or not they have a real case — because the alternative is quietly falling behind people
no more deserving than they are.

This chapter is about that specific management skill: seeing a decision through the eyes
of everyone it touches, not just the person sitting across from you, and not just your
own instinct in the moment.

## The Core Idea: You Are Never Deciding for Just One Person

Horowitz's central claim, stated plainly: the single management mistake he sees most
consistently, from first-time founders all the way up to experienced CEOs, is failing to
see a decision through the eyes of the whole company. Not the person asking for
something. Not your own gut reaction, formed under whatever pressure you're feeling in
the moment. Every employee who will eventually hear about the decision, directly or
through the grapevine, and draw their own conclusion about what it means for them.

This is hard for a specific, structural reason: you're making the decision while looking
at exactly one person, under real time pressure, and the people who will actually be
affected by the precedent aren't in the room to advocate for themselves. Learning to
imagine them anyway — consistently, especially under pressure — is what this chapter is
actually teaching.

## Why It's True: Three Decisions, and One Historical Case Where Getting This Right Changed a Country

### The raise nobody sees is the one that actually matters

Return to the raise request that opened this chapter. The fix isn't refusing every
raise — it's removing the *timing* of the ask from the equation entirely. A formal,
periodic review process — evaluated on a fixed schedule (every quarter, or every six
months), gathering input systematically, applied to everyone at once rather than
whenever someone happens to lobby for it — protects exactly the thing an ad hoc "whoever
asks, gets" system destroys. It also gives every employee something valuable: the
psychological security of knowing they don't have to constantly campaign for what they've
earned, because there's a real process that will surface it on a predictable schedule
regardless of who's most comfortable asking.

### A demotion changes what everyone believes it takes to keep their equity

Horowitz's second case: an executive is working extremely hard, well-liked, and
genuinely trying — and still failing at the job, because the role has outgrown their
actual skills. Firing feels harsh, given the visible effort. Demoting — hiring someone
above them, letting them stay in a smaller role — feels like the kinder, more humane
choice, and it's the one most first-time founders default to.

Horowitz's question, the one that reframes the whole decision: what percentage of the
company does this person actually own? In the real conversation this chapter draws from,
the answer was 1.5% — an amount that dwarfs what a strong individual engineer at the same
company might hold (a tenth or two-tenths of a percent). Left untouched, a demotion
without any change to that equity stake sends an unavoidable message to every other
employee watching: real effort, even without real results, is enough to keep a
disproportionate ownership stake. That's a company-wide statement about what your equity
actually requires — genuine results, or merely genuine effort — made through a single,
seemingly private personnel decision. There's a cost on the other side too, worth being
honest about: the demoted executive, now reporting to someone hired above them, has to
work alongside people who remember them as the boss, which is its own difficult,
often unworkable dynamic. Neither option here is comfortable. The point isn't that
demotion is always wrong — it's that the equity question has to be part of the decision
explicitly, not skipped because the personnel conversation already feels hard enough.

### Should a departing employee get 90 days or 10 years to exercise their stock options?

The most detailed case in this chapter concerns a real policy debate: most startup stock
option agreements require an employee, once they leave, to exercise their vested options
— paying the strike price and often a real tax bill — within 90 days, or forfeit them
entirely. At a company whose valuation has grown substantially, this can require far more
cash than a typical employee has sitting around, which means someone can leave a company
having earned real equity on paper and walk away with nothing, simply because they
couldn't afford to buy it. Sam Altman, teaching elsewhere in this same course, proposed
extending that window to a full ten years instead, an idea borrowed from Quora's Adam
D'Angelo.

Horowitz's analysis is worth following in full, because it's a clean example of this
chapter's whole method. First, the historical context: the 90-day standard exists partly
because of an old accounting rule (APB Opinion 25) that made longer exercise windows
create an unpredictable, stock-price-linked expense a company would have had to report —
a rule that no longer applies today, which means the original reason many companies
adopted the 90-day default has quietly become outdated even though the default itself
persisted. Second, the perspectives actually in play: the departing employee wants
fairness and enough time to come up with the cash. The employees who *stay* care about
two different things — whether unclaimed, forfeited equity returns to the option pool
(benefiting everyone who stayed, since typical annual dilution runs around 10% and
returned shares offset some of that), and whether watching a departing colleague get
what feels like a fair deal changes their own read on whether leaving might have been the
smarter move. A 10-year window is also, quietly, a real financial asset in itself — an
option on a volatile stock is worth more the longer it has to run — which means an
employee who stays and simply holds ordinary vested stock isn't getting that same
optionality value at all.

Horowitz doesn't declare a single correct answer, and that refusal is itself the lesson.
He offers two honest, opposite cultural stances a company could actually take, both
defensible: **"we are straightforwardly fair — you keep the full ten years no matter
what happens"**, or **"your equity is only meaningful if you vest, stay until an actual
exit, and the company ends up being worth something — we're telling you that up front so
you know what you're actually signing up for."** Neither is a trick. Each is a genuine,
different bet about what a company wants its equity to communicate. Altman's own live
response, in the same conversation, is worth noting for a different reason: he revised
his position on the spot, acknowledging the proposal needed more built-in incentive to
stay, since a departing employee can still end up "screwed" depending on whether they
personally have the cash to exercise. That kind of visible, immediate willingness to
update a public position, in front of an audience, having just heard a good counter-
argument, is itself a small demonstration of the skill this whole chapter is about.

### Toussaint Louverture: seeing a decision through the eyes of people you have every reason to hate

Horowitz's most extreme illustration of this chapter's core skill comes from far outside
business. Toussaint Louverture was born into slavery in the French colony of
Saint-Domingue — a system whose brutality, by the numeric record Horowitz cites, was
markedly worse even than slavery in the United States. He went on to lead the only fully
successful slave revolution in recorded history, ending slavery in the colony, defeating
the forces Napoleon sent to retake it, and building what briefly became one of the most
economically productive colonies in the world — one point in its history exporting more
value than the United States did at the time.

What makes Louverture's story a management case study, in Horowitz's telling, is how he
treated the two groups he had the most reason to treat as pure enemies. When his army
defeated opposing soldiers — Spanish, British, French — his own troops expected the
normal spoils of war. Louverture refused to allow pillaging, and beyond that, actively
recruited the most capable officers from the armies he'd just defeated into his own
command, reasoning that the culture and expertise he wanted to build required the best
available talent, regardless of which side that talent had just been fighting for. When
he ultimately controlled the former plantations, his own soldiers — former slaves —
understandably wanted the land and the owners' wealth seized outright. Louverture instead
ended slavery, let plantation owners keep their land, required them to pay real wages to
the workers who had once been enslaved on it, and lowered the owners' taxes to help the
economy actually survive the transition. He didn't do this because he'd forgotten what
had been done to him. He did it because he'd thought through the decision from the
economic perspective of people he had every reason to despise, and concluded that a
country capable of enduring needed their productive capacity intact, not just their
punishment.

> **Sidebar: The Full Weight of What Louverture Overcame**
>
> The brutality Louverture survived deserves to be stated honestly, not softened.
> Horowitz's own account, drawn from historical record, describes routine torture and
> mutilation used to control enslaved people in Saint-Domingue — practices markedly more
> severe, by the numbers he cites, than plantation slavery in the American South. Of
> roughly two million people brought to the Caribbean's sugar colonies over the full
> span of the slave trade, only around seven hundred thousand remained alive by
> slavery's end there — a survival rate dramatically worse than the comparable figures
> in the United States over the same period. This is the system Louverture was born
> into, escaped, and eventually defeated militarily and politically. The reason his
> later decisions — refusing his own soldiers' demand for vengeance, keeping plantation
> owners' land intact — carry real moral and strategic weight is precisely because they
> came from someone who had absolutely no natural inclination toward mercy for the
> people he chose to treat fairly anyway.

The management lesson Horowitz draws from this isn't really about slavery, war, or
national economics — it's that the discipline of imagining a decision through the eyes
of people you have the least natural sympathy for, at the moment you have the most
power over them, is both the hardest and the most consequential version of the skill
this entire chapter describes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Granting a raise only because someone asked, without a formal process evaluating
  everyone.** This teaches an entire company that performance isn't what's actually
  rewarded — asking is.
- **Demoting a struggling executive without addressing what their equity stake now
  signals to the rest of the company.** The personnel decision and the equity question
  are the same decision, not two separate ones.
- **Assuming an old policy (like a 90-day option exercise window) still makes sense
  without checking whether the original reason for it — in this case, an accounting rule
  — still applies.**
- **Making a real decision quickly under pressure, without pausing to think it through
  from every stakeholder's perspective first.** Horowitz's own advice: it's better to
  say "let me think this through properly and get back to you" than to answer instantly
  and get it wrong — burying a real problem instead of addressing it directly tends to
  make it worse the longer it's left alone, not easier.
- **Assuming there's always one universally correct answer to a genuine values tradeoff**
  (like the stock-option window debate), rather than choosing a defensible position
  deliberately and communicating it honestly in advance.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Is there a single right answer to the 90-day-versus-10-year option question?** No, and
this chapter deliberately doesn't pretend there is one. Both of Horowitz's proposed
cultural stances — full fairness regardless of outcome, or an honest, upfront statement
that equity requires staying until an exit — are legitimate choices a company can make.
What matters is making the choice deliberately, understanding the tradeoffs on both
sides, and communicating whichever one you pick clearly, rather than defaulting to
whatever the standard template happens to say without examining it.

**Does "see it through everyone's eyes" mean every decision requires infinite
deliberation?** No — Horowitz's own practical advice is a pause, not paralysis: when a
decision is clearly consequential, say so directly ("I'm taking this seriously and need
to think it through") rather than either rushing to a bad answer or freezing entirely.
Most day-to-day decisions don't require this level of consideration; the skill is
recognizing which ones genuinely do.

**How does this chapter's approach to firing relate to Chapter 5's "fire fast"
advice?** They're complementary, not in tension. Chapter 5 argues for acting quickly once
it's clear someone isn't working out. This chapter is about *how* to communicate that
decision once you've made it — honestly, without either cruelty or excessive apology, and
in a way that preserves the departing person's dignity in front of the rest of the
company, since how a firing is handled publicly shapes what everyone who stays believes
about how they'd be treated in the same position.

## Applying the Test

Take a real or hypothetical management decision you're currently facing — a raise, a
promotion, a difficult personnel call, anything with real stakes. Before deciding, write
down every group of people who will eventually learn about this decision, even indirectly:
the person directly involved, their peers, anyone with a similar role, and anyone who
might reasonably wonder what the decision implies about their own situation. For each
group, write one sentence describing what they would conclude about "what it takes to
succeed here" based on this decision alone. If any of those conclusions would surprise or
alarm you, that's the part of the decision you hadn't actually finished thinking through.

## Chapter Summary

- The core management skill this chapter teaches: see every real decision through the
  eyes of the whole company, not just your own instinct or the person in front of you.
- A raise given only because someone asked teaches everyone else that performance isn't
  what's actually rewarded — use a formal, scheduled process instead.
- A demotion instead of a firing isn't just about the individual — it's a statement,
  visible to the whole company, about whether effort alone is enough to keep a
  disproportionate equity stake.
- The 90-day versus 10-year stock option debate has no single correct answer — pick a
  defensible cultural stance deliberately, and communicate it honestly in advance.
- Toussaint Louverture's decision to treat defeated enemies and former slave owners
  fairly, despite having every reason not to, is the most extreme version of this
  chapter's core skill: imagining a decision through the eyes of people you have the
  least natural sympathy for.
- Pause before deciding on genuinely consequential questions — it's better to say "let
  me think this through" than to answer quickly and get it wrong, since unresolved
  people problems tend to get worse, not better, the longer they're left buried.

   in this chapter as corrosive to a company's culture, even though the raise itself may
   be fully deserved.
2. **Apply:** Using the "Applying the Test" exercise above, work through a real or
   hypothetical management decision you're facing, listing every group who will
   eventually learn about it and what each group would conclude.
3. **Apply:** Draft your own company's policy on stock option exercise windows,
   explicitly choosing one of this chapter's two cultural stances (or a third of your
   own) and writing the one-paragraph explanation you'd give a new hire about it.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues Louverture's decision to treat
   defeated enemies and slave owners fairly was strategically and morally sound. Are
   there situations where extending this kind of consideration to people who have wronged
   you would be the wrong call rather than the right one? What would distinguish those
   cases from Louverture's?
