# Glossary

Terms are listed alphabetically. Each entry names the chapter where the term is first
introduced and explained in full.

**83(b) election** — an IRS form that must be filed within 30 days of receiving
restricted stock, locking in a founder's tax basis while the company's value is still
low. (Ch. 15)

**Accredited investor** — an investor who meets specific income or net worth thresholds,
presumed sophisticated enough to bear the risk of an early-stage investment. (Ch. 15)

**ACRU (Activated Confirmed Registered User)** — eBay's internal metric for a user who
didn't just register but actually bid on or listed an item; contrasted with a raw
Confirmed Registered User (CRU). (Ch. 10)

**Ammunition** — in Keith Rabois's framework, people who do excellent work but need
direction, as opposed to a "barrel" who can drive an idea from concept to shipped
reality on their own. (Ch. 19)

**Anti-dilution provision** — a term sheet clause protecting an investor if the company
later raises money at a lower valuation. See broad-based weighted average and full
ratchet. (Appendix B)

**Balance sheet** — a snapshot, at one moment, of a company's assets minus liabilities,
equal to equity. (Ch. 15)

**Barrel** — see Ammunition; the person who can drive an idea all the way to shipped
reality. (Ch. 19)

**Board seat** — a position on a company's board of directors; Chapter 15 recommends
declining requests for one unless the investor adds genuine, ongoing strategic value.

**Burn rate** — the net amount of cash a company spends per month, beyond what it brings
in. (Ch. 15)

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** — the cost to acquire one paying customer, commonly
calculated as cost per click divided by conversion rate. (Ch. 11)

**Cap table** — the record of who owns what percentage of a company, and how that
changes across financing rounds. (Ch. 15; full worked example in Appendix A)

**Champion** — a customer who actively advocates for a product to others, as
distinguished from a merely satisfied customer. (Ch. 9)

**Churn** — the rate at which existing customers stop using a product or stop paying for
it. (Ch. 8, Ch. 10)

**CLV / LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)** — the total net revenue a customer generates
over a defined period as a customer. (Ch. 11)

**Cofounder vesting** — see Vesting; applies to founders exactly as it does to
employees. (Ch. 4, Ch. 15)

**Cohort** — a group of users who joined or signed up during the same period, tracked
together to see how their behavior (especially retention) changes over time. (Ch. 7, Ch.
10)

**Concentric expansion** — the strategy of dominating a small market completely, then
expanding outward into progressively larger, adjacent markets. Introduced independently
by three separate lectures in this book (Chapters 2, 3, and 16). (Ch. 2)

**Contractor vs. employee** — a legal distinction with different tax treatment (1099 vs.
W-2) and different obligations (workers' compensation, work-hour control). (Ch. 15)

**Convertible note** — a debt instrument that converts into equity at a future priced
round, an alternative to a SAFE. (Ch. 15)

**Drag-along / tag-along rights** — term sheet clauses governing what happens to
minority shareholders when a majority decides to sell the company. (Appendix B)

**Elephant, deer, rabbit** — Christoph Janz's framework for categorizing a business by
customer count and price point (few customers at a high price vs. many customers at a
low price), each requiring a different sales motion. (Ch. 16)

**FF stock** — a special class of founder stock structured so founders can sell some of
it in a later financing round without distorting the common stock valuation. Setting it
up at company formation reads as a bad signal to investors; the safe time is later, once
the business is already working. (Ch. 21)

**Four Horsemen** — John Gottman's four relationship-ending behaviors (criticism,
contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling), applied in this book to customer support.
Stonewalling — simply not responding — is identified as the most damaging to a startup.
(Ch. 8)

**Full ratchet** — the harshest form of anti-dilution provision, repricing an investor's
entire stake to match a lower down-round price regardless of the round's size. (Appendix
B)

**Growth accounting (new / resurrected / churned)** — a framework for breaking down
user growth into new signups, users who return after 30+ days away, and users who
leave — useful because resurrected and churned users often dominate the numbers once a
product has been around for a few years. (Ch. 10)

**Investment thesis** — a written, specific hypothesis for why a company will work,
including what the founder believes they know that skeptics don't; tracked over time by
whether confidence in it is rising or falling. (Ch. 18)

**K-factor** — see Viral coefficient.

**Knowledge gap** — Jared Spool's concept: the distance between what a user already
knows and what they'd need to know to use a product well; closed either by increasing
user knowledge or decreasing what the product requires. (Ch. 8)

**Last mover advantage** — Peter Thiel's argument that durability, not speed to market,
determines whether a company captures most of its eventual value; the goal is to be the
last significant company in a category, not the first. (Ch. 3)

**Liquidation preference** — a term sheet clause determining who gets paid first, and how
much, in an acquisition or liquidation, before remaining proceeds are split by ownership
percentage. (Appendix B)

**Magic moment** — the specific instant a new user genuinely understands what a product
is for (seeing a friend's photo on Facebook; finding the right listing on Airbnb). (Ch.
10)

**Moat** — a term popularized by Warren Buffett for whatever makes a business hard for a
competitor to attack; in this book's terms, the characteristics that make a monopoly
durable (proprietary technology, network effects, economies of scale, branding). (Ch. 3)

**Monopoly (business sense)** — a business with no serious competitor doing what it
does, stable enough to plan years ahead and capture real profit; contrasted with perfect
competition. (Ch. 3)

**MVP (Minimum Viable Product)** — the smallest version of a product that still
genuinely solves the core problem; the emphasis belongs on "viable," not "minimum." (Ch.
7)

**North Star metric** — the single number a company chooses to align every team's
decisions around (monthly active users, nights booked, messages sent), rather than
running a separate, siloed growth team. (Ch. 10)

**Onion theory of risk** — Andy Rachleff's framework, via Marc Andreessen: a startup
carries many layers of risk (team, product, technical, launch, market, revenue, cost of
sales, viral growth), and each funding round should be sized to remove a specific layer,
not simply to raise as much as possible. (Ch. 13)

**Option pool** — shares set aside for future employees, typically created or expanded
at a priced financing round; where it's carved from (pre-money vs. post-money) affects
who bears the dilution. (Ch. 15; Appendix A)

**P&L (Profit and Loss statement)** — revenue minus expenses over a period of time. (Ch.
15)

**Payback period** — how long it takes a customer's revenue to repay the cost of
acquiring them; three months is a conservative benchmark, twelve months is a riskier
upper bound. (Ch. 11)

**Payload, conversion, frequency** — Sean Parker's three factors determining virality:
how many people a viral action reaches, what fraction convert, and how often it happens.
(Ch. 11)

**Perfect competition** — a market with many similar sellers and no meaningful pricing
power, where, per Peter Thiel, almost nobody makes real money; contrasted with monopoly.
(Ch. 3)

**Post-money / pre-money valuation** — pre-money is what a company is considered worth
before new investment; post-money is pre-money plus the new money raised. (Ch. 15;
Appendix A, B)

**Priced round** — a financing round (typically Series A or later) that sets an actual
valuation for the company, as opposed to an unpriced SAFE or convertible note. (Ch. 15)

**Pro-rata rights** — an investor's right to invest again in future rounds to maintain
their existing ownership percentage. (Ch. 15; Appendix B)

**Protective provisions** — a list of major company actions that require investor
approval, written into financing terms. (Appendix B)

**Retention curve** — a plot of what percentage of a user cohort remains active over
time since acquisition; an asymptotic (flattening) curve signals product-market fit, a
curve decaying to zero signals its absence. (Ch. 7, Ch. 10)

**Runway** — how many months of operation remain at the current burn rate before a
company runs out of cash (cash in bank ÷ monthly burn). (Ch. 15)

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)** — an instrument, alongside the convertible
note, that lets an investor provide cash now in exchange for equity later, once a priced
round sets a valuation. (Ch. 15; Appendix A)

**Support-Driven Development (SDD)** — Kevin Hale's practice of having everyone on a
team, including engineers and founders, personally handle customer support, to keep the
product feedback loop intact. (Ch. 8)

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)** — the ceiling on how large a market could eventually
become; useful only alongside the market's growth rate, not on its own. (Ch. 2)

**Task-relevant maturity** — Andy Grove's concept: how experienced a specific person is
at a specific task right now, which should determine how much a manager delegates versus
guides, rather than applying one uniform management style to everyone. (Ch. 19)

**Term sheet** — a short document laying out the proposed terms of a priced financing
round, negotiated before the final legal agreements. (Ch. 15; full walkthrough in
Appendix B)

**Unique insight** — the specific thing a founder understands that skeptics or
competitors don't, required for a contrarian idea to also be a correct one. (Ch. 18)

**Valuation cap** — the upper limit on the valuation used to convert a SAFE or note into
equity, rewarding early investors with a better effective price once a priced round sets
a higher valuation. (Ch. 15; Appendix A)

**Vesting** — the process by which equity is earned over time rather than owned
immediately; the Silicon Valley standard is four years with a one-year cliff. (Ch. 4, Ch.
15)

**Viral coefficient (K-factor)** — roughly, how many new users each existing user brings
in; above 1 compounds into exponential growth, below 1 eventually flatlines. (Ch. 11, Ch.
17)

**Why now** — the discipline, credited to Sequoia, of identifying a specific, dated,
external reason an idea works today and didn't work two years ago. (Ch. 2)

**X and Y (value creation and capture)** — Peter Thiel's framing: a company's value
depends on how much value it creates (X) and what percentage of that value it captures
(Y) — two independent variables, both required for a company to be genuinely valuable.
(Ch. 3)
