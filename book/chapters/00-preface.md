# Preface

In the fall of 2014, Stanford ran a class called CS183B: How to Start a Startup. It had
no textbook. Twenty lectures, most delivered by a founder or investor who had actually
built the thing they were describing, covering everything from how to pick an idea to
how to fire someone with dignity. The transcripts of those lectures have circulated
online for years — the raw material is freely available, unglamorous, occasionally
contradictory, and never organized into anything a reader could sit down with the way
you'd sit down with a real book.

This is that book. Not a summary of the course, and not a transcript with the "umms"
removed. A synthesis — twenty speakers' worth of hard-won, sometimes conflicting
experience, reorganized around the actual arc of building a company instead of the
order in which guest speakers happened to be available on a Tuesday, with the gaps
filled in, the terms defined from scratch, and the places where two speakers said the
same thing without knowing it pointed out explicitly, because that convergence is itself
a signal worth noticing.

## Who This Book Assumes You Are

You can build software. You understand systems, and you're comfortable with the idea
that a complicated thing is usually just several simple things stacked on top of each
other. You do not know what a cap table is. You've never read a term sheet. The words
"Series A" and "SAFE" and "vesting cliff" are either unfamiliar or half-familiar in a way
that's almost worse than not knowing them at all, because you'd nod along in a meeting
rather than admit you needed the basics explained.

This book explains the basics. Every term gets defined the first time it's used, the
same way you'd want a new library's documentation to define a term before using it three
paragraphs later. Nothing is assumed. If a chapter uses a word from a previous chapter,
it either re-explains it briefly or points you back to where it was covered — you should
never have to already know something to follow what you're reading.

## What This Book Is Not About

Chapter 1 draws this line explicitly, but it's worth stating here too, before you've
committed to reading further: this book is about one specific, unusual kind of
company — one built to grow fast, take outside investment, and attempt to become large.
It is not a book about starting a small business, a consultancy, or a lifestyle
company that stays deliberately modest. Both paths are legitimate. They require
different founders, different capital, and different definitions of success, and
conflating them is one of the more common ways people misapply advice meant for one path
to a company actually pursuing the other. If you're not yet sure which path you want,
Chapter 1 is where that question gets addressed directly, before anything else.

## How This Book Was Made

Every chapter in this book traces back to a specific lecture, or combination of
lectures, from the original course — that mapping is documented in this book's planning
materials and referenced in each chapter's "Further Reading" section. Where the
lectures assumed knowledge a reader without a business background wouldn't have — how a
SAFE actually converts, what a valuation cap does to real numbers, what a profit and
loss statement even is — this book adds that missing layer explicitly, clearly marked
as an addition rather than presented as though it came from the original course.

Where lectures given in 2014 made claims later evidence has complicated — that
cofounders should never work remotely, for instance, a claim GitLab and Zapier have
since tested directly — this book says so, in sidebars marked "What's Changed Since
2014," rather than either pretending the original advice is timeless or quietly
updating it without telling you.

Where compressing a lecture's original content risked losing something a reader might
actually need — the full text of a mock pitch, worked through line by line; the full
historical weight of a single management case study — that material was moved into an
appendix rather than cut. Nothing that mattered was thrown away in the name of a tighter
narrative; it was relocated to wherever it could still be found.

## How to Read It

Linearly, ideally, at least the first time through. The chapters build on each other
deliberately: Part One establishes vocabulary the rest of the book uses without
re-explaining it; Part Four assumes the product discipline from Part Three; Part Six
assumes you already have the team, product, and capital covered in the parts before it.
Reading Chapter 15's legal mechanics before Chapter 1's argument for why you'd want to
do any of this at all would be a strange way to spend your time.

After the first pass, treat it as a reference. Each chapter ends with a summary you can
scan without rereading the whole argument, and the glossary and index at the back exist
specifically so you can find the one paragraph you remember reading, months later, once
you actually need it — when a cofounder conversation is getting uncomfortable, when a
term sheet lands in your inbox, when a management decision seems small until you notice
who else will eventually hear about it.

## A Note on What This Book Cannot Do

Every chapter argues, in one form or another, that the tactics inside it only matter in
service of something upstream: a real compulsion, a real insight, a real understanding
of your own users. No book, including this one, can supply that. What it can do is make
sure that once you have it, you're not slowed down by not knowing what a SAFE is, or
losing a cofounder relationship because nobody thought to write down the equity split
early, or misjudging when to fire someone because no one ever explained what "fire fast"
actually looks like in practice. That's the gap this book is trying to close — not the
compulsion itself, which Chapter 1 argues has to come from somewhere else entirely, but
everything downstream of it.
