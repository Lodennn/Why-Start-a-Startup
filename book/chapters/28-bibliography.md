# Bibliography

## Primary Source

Stanford University, CS183B: How to Start a Startup (Fall 2014). Twenty lectures
delivered by founders, investors, and operators, taught by Sam Altman with guest
lectures from Dustin Moskovitz, Paul Graham, Adora Cheung, Peter Thiel, Alex Schultz,
Kevin Hale, Stanley Tang, Walker Williams, Justin Kan, Marc Andreessen, Ron Conway,
Parker Conrad, Alfred Lin, Brian Chesky, Patrick Collison, John Collison, Ben Silbermann,
Aaron Levie, Reid Hoffman, Keith Rabois, Ben Horowitz, Emmett Shear, Hosain Rahman,
Kirsty Nathoo, Carolynn Levy, Tyler Bosmeny, Michael Seibel, and Dalton Caldwell. This
book is a synthesis of all twenty lectures, reorganized and expanded; every chapter's
"Further Reading" section notes which lecture or lectures it draws from.

## Books

Fried, Jason, and David Heinemeier Hansson. *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work.*
Referenced in Chapter 1 as the fullest case for the "calm company" alternative to
hyper-growth culture.

Gottman, John. Referenced in Chapter 8; Gottman's research on marriage and conflict
prediction is the basis for this book's customer-support framework (the "Four
Horsemen," and the money/kids/sex/time categories of conflict).

Grove, Andy. *High Output Management* (1982). Referenced in Chapter 19 as the source of
task-relevant maturity and the "measure output, not motion" framing of management.

Horowitz, Ben. Referenced in Chapter 20; Horowitz's own account of the Toussaint
Louverture case draws on C.L.R. James's historical work on the Haitian Revolution.

Lencioni, Patrick. *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team.* Referenced in Chapter 6 as the
source of the trust-conflict-commitment-accountability-results framework.

Thiel, Peter, with Blake Masters. *Zero to One.* Referenced in Chapters 2 and 3 as the
fuller development of the monopoly-theory argument covered from Thiel's original
lecture.

Treacy, Michael, and Fred Wiersema. *The Discipline of Market Leaders.* Referenced in
Chapter 8 as the source of the best-price / best-product / best-overall-solution
framework.

Walsh, Bill. *The Score Takes Care of Itself.* Referenced in Chapter 19 for the 49ers
turnaround and the argument that operational details, consistently executed, matter more
than any single strategic insight.

## Essays and Articles

Andreessen, Marc. "Why Software Is Eating the World." Referenced in Chapter 2 as the
fuller argument behind the "why now" discussion.

Graham, Paul. "How to Get Startup Ideas." Referenced in Chapter 2 as the source of the
"live in the future" and "don't consciously search for ideas" arguments.

Graham, Paul. "Do Things That Don't Scale." Referenced in Chapter 9 as the origin of
that chapter's title and additional examples.

Holiday, Ryan. *Trust Me, I'm Lying.* Referenced in Chapter 12 as a deliberately cynical
account of how stories spread online, useful for context though not this book's
recommended method.

Janz, Christoph. "Five Ways to Build a $100 Million Business." Referenced in Chapter 16
as the source of the elephants-to-rabbits customer-count/price-point framework.

Kincaid, Jason. Overview of pitching press, referenced in Chapter 12 for additional
tactical depth on media relations.

*The Economist.* "Entrepreneurs Anonymous" (2014). Referenced in Chapter 1 as the source
Moskovitz cites on founder depression as a documented phenomenon.

## Other Referenced Sources

Altman, Sam. Blog post on stock option exercise windows, referenced and critiqued
directly in Chapter 20.

Buffett, Warren. Shareholder letters, referenced in Chapter 3 as the origin of the
"moat" terminology as applied to durable competitive advantage.

Rachleff, Andy. Original writing on the "onion theory of risk," referenced in Chapter 13
via Marc Andreessen.

Y Combinator. Standard startup legal document library (incorporation, financing, and
employment templates), referenced in Chapter 15 and throughout as the practical starting
point for the mechanics that chapter covers.
