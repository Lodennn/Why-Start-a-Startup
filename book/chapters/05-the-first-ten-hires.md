# Chapter 5 — The First Ten Hires

Airbnb spent five months interviewing its first employee. In the company's entire first
year, it hired two people. Before any of that, Brian Chesky and his co-founders wrote
down the culture values they wanted every future Airbnb employee to hold — before there
was a product worth using, before there was any revenue, before there was anyone to
apply the values to yet. One of those values was that you had to "bleed Airbnb." Chesky
used to ask candidates whether they'd take the job if they'd just been told they had one
year left to live. He later decided that was a little too intense and relaxed it to ten
years. As far as anyone at Y Combinator has heard, he still asks it.

This is not how most first-time founders hire. Most first-time founders hire the way
they'd hire for anything else that felt urgent: post a listing, take some interviews,
extend an offer to the strongest candidate within a reasonable amount of time. This
chapter is about why that instinct, applied to a startup's first ten employees, is one
of the more expensive mistakes a founder can make — and what the alternative actually
looks like.

## The Core Idea: Try Not To Hire, Then Hire Completely Differently

The starting stance, before any hiring tactics matter at all, is this: try not to hire.
Sam Altman's observation about the psychology of headcount is worth stating plainly,
because it cuts against a natural instinct — people treat a company's employee count as
a proxy for how real and impressive it is. Say a high number, and people are impressed.
Say a low number, and it can feel like an admission that the company isn't serious yet.
The actual relationship runs the other way. A large headcount usually means a high burn
rate, more complexity, and slower decisions. The startups that stay smallest longest,
for their size and stage, are frequently the ones doing the best — proud of how much they
can get done with how few people, not embarrassed by it.

That said, at some point you do have to hire — and once you're in hiring mode, the rule
changes completely. Altman's answer to how much time hiring should take: zero percent, or
twenty-five percent. There's no comfortable middle. Either you're not hiring at all right
now, or it is close to the single largest block of your time as a founder. Most
management books recommend something like fifty percent, but Altman notes that the
people who give that advice rarely spend even ten percent of their own time on it
themselves. Twenty-five percent, done for real, is already an enormous commitment — and
it's what's actually required to get this right.

The reason this matters so much comes down to one blunt fact: the cost of getting an
early hire wrong is far higher than the cost of getting a later one wrong. A mediocre
hire at a company with a thousand employees causes real but contained damage — that
person is one of a thousand, and the company's culture and standards absorb the mistake.
A mediocre hire among a company's first five or ten people sets the tone for everyone who
joins after them, and Altman has seen it kill companies outright. A friend of his keeps a
sign in his conference room, angled so interview candidates can read it during the
interview: *mediocre engineers do not build great companies.* It's blunt, and it's true,
and it's exactly the standard a big company can survive violating in a way a ten-person
startup can't.

## Why It's True: Culture Before Hiring, Sourcing Beyond LinkedIn, and What to Actually Test For

### Write the culture down before you hire anyone

Airbnb's approach — writing culture values down before making a single hire — sounds like
a strange amount of process for a company with no employees yet. Patrick Collison of
Stripe, teaching later in the same course, offers the underlying logic for why it isn't
overkill: when you hire your first ten people, you're not really hiring ten people. You're
hiring something closer to a hundred, because each of those first ten will go on to bring
in roughly ten more people who resemble them culturally. Deciding, deliberately, what
those first ten should be like — before you're under pressure to just fill a role — is
one of the highest-leverage decisions a founder makes, precisely because its effects
compound for years afterward.

At Airbnb, this showed up concretely: every one of the company's first forty or so
employees reports feeling like they were part of founding the company, not like they
joined after the fact. That feeling doesn't happen by accident. It happens because the
company set an extremely high bar — Airbnb spent five months on its first hire alone —
and everyone who cleared that bar knew, going in, exactly what they were signing up to
believe in.

### There is no wrong place to find people, but there is a best one

Personal referrals — people you already know, and people your existing employees already
know — are, by a wide margin, the best source of early hires. Most founders feel awkward
calling every talented person they've ever met and asking their own employees to do the
same. Do it anyway: at a large company like Facebook or Google, one of the first things
that happens to a new hire is an HR conversation designed specifically to extract every
talented person they know, for exactly this reason. A useful, underused variant: look
outside the immediate geographic area you're hiring in. Competition for engineers in a
place like Silicon Valley is brutal; the person you already know from somewhere else in
the world may be just as good and much easier to bring on.

Ben Silbermann, Pinterest's co-founder, makes a complementary point from the other
direction: there's no wrong *place* to look, even if referrals are the best source on
average. His own early hiring included posting ads on Craigslist, showing up to random
tech talks, and running weekly bring-your-own-food-and-drink barbecues at the office just
to get people talking. His candid observation about where a lot of his best people
actually turned up: any time he went to get coffee at a nearby café, there'd be another
founder there doing exactly the same kind of recruiting. Genuinely good people are almost
always doing something else already — you have to go find them, especially when nobody
has heard of your company yet.

That last point — nobody's heard of you yet — is worth sitting with, because it's the
central obstacle to hiring anyone at all in the first few months. John Collison describes
what this actually feels like: you're two people working on something strange that nobody
recognizes, trying to convince someone to leave a stable situation for it. For Stripe's
second hire, his own friends staged what Collison calls "a full-on assault" the night
before he accepted, trying to talk him out of joining. He joined anyway. One of those
same friends who tried to talk him out of it later joined Stripe himself.

Given that friction, a specific and slightly counterintuitive sourcing insight from
Patrick Collison is worth naming directly: look for people who are early in their careers
or currently undervalued by the broader market, rather than trying to poach someone
already recognized as excellent. Someone the market has already correctly identified as
a "known spectacular quantity" is, by definition, already happy somewhere and hard to
move. Stripe's early team included a designer who was eighteen and still in high school in
Sweden, and a future CTO who was still in college. Neither looked, on paper, like an
obvious hire for a payments company. Both turned out to be exactly the kind of person the
company needed — because the company was betting on who they'd become, not just who they
already were on a resume.

### What to actually test for, and how

Once you have candidates, the traits that matter are more specific than "smart" — smart
is close to a given, and most founders already screen for it without being told to.
Altman's own three-question test, applied consistently across every hire he's ever made
and rarely regretted: **is this person smart? Do they get things done? Do I want to
spend a lot of time around them?** Answer yes to all three, and it's almost always worked
out.

Patrick and John Collison arrived independently at a closely related, slightly more
specific set of three traits after examining what their own first ten hires had in
common: they were **genuine** — people others trusted and wanted to work with; they
**cared a great deal**, to a degree Collison himself now considers a little extreme in
hindsight (every API error at Stripe was, for a period, emailed directly to the founders'
personal inboxes, and early employees would copy each other on outgoing emails just to
flag small grammar mistakes); and they were people who **finished things**, as opposed to
people who were excited about many projects but rarely completed any of them.

The best way to actually assess any of this isn't a standard interview. It's working
together on something real, even briefly, before formally hiring. If you've already
worked with someone, in many cases you don't need a traditional interview at all — you
already have the evidence. If you haven't, a short paid project, ideally spanning a
couple of days, teaches both sides more than any conversation could. Where an interview
is unavoidable, ask about specific past projects in detail rather than reaching for
brainteasers — a pattern Altman notes young technical cofounders fall into surprisingly
often, apparently because it feels more rigorous than it actually is.

References are the step first-time founders skip most often, and the step that matters
most when it isn't skipped. The wrong way to reference-check is to ask "how was
so-and-so?" and accept a pleasant, generic answer. The right way, borrowed from Ben
Silbermann's own practice: force the reference to be specific by asking a version of "if
I asked [a mutual contact] what you're best at, or what you're working to improve, what
would they say?" — which builds in social accountability the reference can't easily
dodge. Then push further: is this person in the top five percent of everyone you've
worked with? The top ten? Would you hire them again? If not, why not? A generic answer
to "were they good?" tells you almost nothing. A forced ranking against everyone else
that person has worked with tells you a great deal.

One more correlated signal worth tracking, per Altman's own observation across many YC
companies: communication skills predict whether a hire works out more reliably than
founders initially expect. Someone who struggles to explain themselves clearly in an
interview tends to struggle the same way once they're actually on the team, under real
pressure. One last practical heuristic, from Mark Zuckerberg by way of Altman: would you
be comfortable hanging out with this person socially, and would you be comfortable
reporting to them if your roles were reversed? You don't need to be friends with
everyone you hire, but if the honest answer to both is no, that's worth taking seriously.

### Give employees real equity — more than feels natural

Founders consistently make an equity mistake in a specific, predictable direction:
generous with investors, stingy with employees. Altman's advice is to reverse that
instinct. His own rough benchmark: aim to give about ten percent of the company, combined,
to your first ten employees. They'll earn it over the standard four-year vesting period,
and if they're genuinely good, they'll go on to create far more value than that ten
percent represents. Investors write a check once and, despite the promises that often
come with a term sheet, frequently contribute less ongoing value than founders expect.
Employees are the ones who build the company, year after year. The YC companies that
have been unusually generous with early equity are, in Altman's account, disproportionately
represented among the ones that went on to be the most successful.

### Keep the people you just spent months hiring

Hiring well and then managing badly wastes the whole effort. A specific, recurring
failure mode among first-time founders: telling people when they're doing something
wrong constantly, because it feels obviously correct to point out mistakes as you see
them, without realizing that a team told they're failing every day will simply leave.
The founders who get this right tend to follow a few unglamorous habits: let the team
take public credit for what goes well, and take the blame yourself for what goes badly.
Don't micromanage — hand people real, if initially small, areas of responsibility, and
let that responsibility grow. Researcher Dan Pink's framework for what actually motivates
people to do their best work — autonomy, mastery, and purpose — is worth taking seriously
even though it's rarely top of mind for a first-time founder buried in product work.
Regular one-on-ones and direct, specific feedback round out the list. None of this is
complicated. All of it is easy to skip when you're moving fast, which is exactly why it
gets skipped.

### Fire fast when it isn't working

The flip side of generous hiring is fast firing, and first-time founders reliably get
this wrong in the same direction: they wait too long, hoping the person will turn it
around. The better rule, painful as it is in the moment, is to fire quickly once it's
clear something isn't working — it's better for the company, and it's usually better for
the person too, who's stuck in a role they're failing at. Two specific patterns are worth
watching for beyond simple underperformance: people who create office politics, and people
who are persistently negative, since both are corrosive to a small team in a way a large
company can better absorb. The test for knowing it's genuinely time to act, rather than
just a rough patch: everyone makes mistakes occasionally, and that's normal — the signal
to actually act is when someone is getting nearly every decision wrong, consistently,
over weeks or a month, to the point where it's obvious to everyone on the team, not just
to you.

## Common Mistakes

- **Hiring to look more legitimate, rather than because the work genuinely requires it.**
  A low headcount is a sign of efficiency, not a sign of being unserious — treat it that
  way.
- **Interviewing with brainteasers instead of asking about real past projects.** Past
  work is a far better predictor of future work than a clever puzzle solved under
  artificial pressure.
- **Accepting a vague, pleasant answer from a reference instead of forcing specificity.**
  "They were great to work with" is not a data point. "Top five percent of everyone
  you've worked with, and here's specifically why" is.
- **Compromising on a hire because of a pressing deadline.** Every founder does this
  eventually as the company scales, and it's regretted almost every time it happens
  among the first five to ten hires specifically.
- **Only sourcing from people who already look impressive on paper.** The most valuable
  early hires are frequently undervalued by the broader market — early in their careers,
  or overlooked for reasons that have nothing to do with how good they actually are.
- **Skipping the culture-values conversation until the company already has employees.**
  By the time you need the values to resolve a real disagreement, it's too late to
  invent them from scratch.
- **Being generous with equity to investors and stingy with employees.** It's usually
  backwards — employees create most of a company's long-term value.
- **Telling people they're doing something wrong constantly, without also giving them
  credit and real ownership.** This is one of the fastest ways to cause otherwise-good
  hires to quit.
- **Waiting too long to fire someone who is clearly, consistently failing.** It's
  painful in the moment and usually better for both the company and the person once it
  finally happens.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Does "try not to hire" ever stop applying?** Yes — this chapter's advice is
specifically about the earliest hires, where the cost of a mistake is highest and the
company has the least infrastructure to absorb one. Later in a company's life, the
opposite skill becomes necessary: hiring fast and scaling a team deliberately. Chapter 21
covers what changes once a company crosses roughly twenty-five employees and needs real
management structure for the first time — the "try not to hire" instinct from this
chapter is explicitly not the right instinct once you're there.

**Does experience matter, or doesn't it?** Both, depending on the role. For a role
running a large, established part of the organization, real experience usually matters.
For most early hires at a very young startup, Altman's own experience is that aptitude
and genuine belief in the mission matter more than having done the exact job before —
some of the best hires he's ever been involved in had never done the specific role
they were hired into. The judgment call is knowing which kind of role you're filling,
and not defaulting to "more experience is always better" without asking that question
first.

**What happens when someone who was a great early hire stops being able to grow with the
company?** This is a real, common problem — someone hired to lead engineering at five
people may not be the right person to lead a team of fifty. Chapter 19 covers a more
precise framework (task-relevant maturity) for reasoning about when someone can grow
into a bigger role versus when a role needs to change around them. The short version for
this chapter: genuinely smart, adaptable people can usually find *some* valuable role as
the company scales, even if it isn't the one they started in — which is a reason to hire
for underlying aptitude in the first place, not just for the specific job description in
front of you today.

## Applying the Test

Take someone you're considering hiring, or someone already on your team whose fit you're
unsure about. Don't ask yourself the vague version of the question — "are they good?" —
because it's too easy to answer yes by default. Ask the forced version instead: if you
called three people who've worked closely with this person, would each of them
independently rank them in the top five percent of everyone they've worked with? Would
each of them tell a specific story about something this person finished, under pressure,
without being managed closely? If you're not confident what the honest answer would be,
that uncertainty is itself the signal — the same way it was in Chapter 4's cofounder
test. The fix isn't to hope it resolves itself. It's to go make the calls before you
decide, not after.

## Chapter Summary

- Try not to hire until you genuinely need to — a small headcount is a sign of
  efficiency, not a sign of being unserious.
- Once you are hiring, it should take zero percent of your time or roughly twenty-five
  percent — there's no comfortable middle, because getting it right requires real,
  sustained attention.
- A bad hire among your first five to ten people can kill the company outright, in a way
  a bad hire at a thousand-person company can't.
- Write your culture values down before you hire anyone, the way Airbnb did — deciding
  this deliberately, rather than under pressure to fill a role, compounds for years.
- Personal referrals are the best source of candidates, but there's no wrong place to
  look — Ben Silbermann hired through Craigslist, tech talks, and office barbecues.
- Test for specific, concrete traits — smart, gets things done, someone you want to
  spend time with; or Stripe's version: genuine, cares a great deal, finishes things —
  rather than generic intelligence alone.
- Work together on something real before formally hiring, whenever possible. When you
  can't, ask about specific past projects instead of brainteasers, and force specificity
  out of every reference check.
- The most valuable early hires are frequently undervalued by the broader market —
  early in their careers, not yet recognized as exceptional by anyone else.
- Be generous with equity toward employees, not just investors — roughly ten percent of
  the company to the first ten hires is a reasonable benchmark.
- Retention is a management habit, not a one-time decision: give credit publicly, take
  blame yourself, avoid micromanaging, and don't tell people they're failing every day.
- Fire fast once it's clearly not working — waiting is worse for the company and usually
  worse for the person, too.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain why the cost of a bad hire is so much higher in a company's first
   ten employees than in its thousandth.
2. **Apply:** Write down your own company's (real or hypothetical) culture values, the
   way Airbnb did before hiring anyone. If you already have employees, would each of
   your first few hires have clearly passed those values, or were the values invented
   after the fact to describe people you'd already hired?
3. **Apply:** Pick someone currently on a team you're part of, or someone you're
   considering hiring. Draft the specific reference-check questions from this chapter
   you'd actually ask about them, and predict honestly what the answers would be.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues aptitude and mission-belief matter
   more than experience for most early hires. But some of the companies covered
   elsewhere in this book (enterprise software, capital-intensive businesses) clearly do
   need specific deep expertise from day one. How would you tell, for your own company,
   which category you're in?
