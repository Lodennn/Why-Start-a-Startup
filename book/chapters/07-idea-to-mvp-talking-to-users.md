# Chapter 7 — From Idea to MVP: Talking to Users

Adora Cheung's first real company wasn't Homejoy. It was Pathjoy, founded with her
brother around 2009, built on a genuinely appealing mission: make people happier. From
that mission, they reasoned their way to a product — a platform for life coaches and
therapists, since those are, on paper, the people whose job is making other people
happier. They spent almost a year building it.

There was one problem, and it only became obvious once the product existed: neither
founder would ever actually use a life coach or a therapist themselves. It wasn't a
problem they had. It was a problem they'd reasoned their way into believing they should
care about. Homejoy — a real company, a real business, eventually a real acquisition
target — came later, and only after this failure taught Cheung something she carried into
every product decision afterward: an idea that sounds meaningful in the abstract is
worthless if you can't picture yourself actually being a customer for it.

This chapter is about the space between "I have an idea" and "I have something worth
building a company around" — the part of the process where a founder turns a hunch into
a real minimum viable product, and learns, through actual conversations with actual
users, whether that hunch was ever true.

## The Core Idea: Storyboard It, Then Build the Smallest Real Version

Before writing any code, Cheung's advice is to storyboard the entire experience a
customer will have — not just the product screen, but everything around it: how they
first hear about you, what the copy says when they land on your site, what happens when
they sign up, what they get from actually using the product, and what happens
afterward — do they leave a review, tell a friend, come back next week. Walking through
that whole arc on paper, before any of it is built, tends to reveal what the smallest
real version of the product actually needs to contain.

That smallest real version is the **MVP** — minimum viable product — and the emphasis
belongs on *viable*, not *minimum*. Cheung's specific warning: a lot of founders build
the "minimum" and quietly drop the "viable," shipping something so stripped down it
can't actually solve anyone's problem, and then wonder why nobody sticks around. An MVP
has to be genuinely small — but it also has to genuinely work for the specific problem
it claims to solve.

Once you have something worth testing, Emmett Shear, who built Twitch, adds a discipline
that's easy to skip: **who** you talk to about it matters as much as **what** you ask
them. Get the wrong people's feedback, and you'll build confidently in the wrong
direction no matter how carefully you listen to them.

## Why It's True: Becoming an Insider, Storyboarding, and Asking the Wrong Question on Purpose

### Become a cog in the industry, even though "disruption" thinking says not to

The popular version of startup wisdom says outsiders disrupt industries precisely
because they aren't weighed down by how insiders already think. Cheung's own experience
argues for something close to the opposite, at least at the very beginning: spend real
time becoming a genuine insider in the industry you're about to build in, before you try
to change it.

When she and her brother started what became Homejoy, they didn't just research the
cleaning industry — they became cleaners themselves, and discovered quickly that they
were bad at it. So one of them went and got an actual job at an existing local cleaning
company. That job taught two different things at once: how to actually clean a house
well, and — more valuable in the long run — exactly why an existing local cleaning
business could never grow into something like Homejoy. The inefficiencies weren't
visible from outside. Booking customers and scheduling cleaners, done the old way, were
so manual and ad hoc that nobody inside the business had any reason to imagine them done
differently. Cheung's analogy: you can read every book about basketball ever written,
but you don't get better at basketball until you actually throw the ball.

This extends to genuine obsessiveness about the competitive landscape: reading every
competitor's public filings, sitting in on earnings calls, working through search
results well past the first page. Most of it won't teach you anything. Occasionally
you'll find a detail that changes how you think about the whole market, and there's no
way to know in advance which search result that will be — which is the actual argument
for doing the tedious version of this research rather than skipping straight to building.

### Positioning in one sentence, users recruited one at a time

Once there's something to show people, the same "one sentence" discipline from Chapter
2 applies to how you describe it to a potential customer. Homejoy's original pitch was
long and abstract — an online platform for home services, with cleaning as the first
category, explained across several sentences. It lost people before they finished
reading. The version that actually worked was blunt and concrete: get your place cleaned
for $20 an hour. Once the offer was that specific, people understood immediately what
they'd get, and signups followed.

Getting the very first users rarely looks dignified. Cheung and her brother tried the
obvious channels first — using it themselves, asking parents (who lived too far away),
asking friends (mostly in the wrong city) — and each one dead-ended. What actually
worked was standing at a street fair on a hot day, handing out free ice-cold water
bottles, and using the goodwill from that to talk people into booking a cleaning on the
spot. Cheung's own description: they'd essentially guilt-tripped strangers into becoming
customers. The important detail isn't the tactic itself — it's that most of those
guilt-tripped customers didn't cancel afterward, which was the real signal that the
underlying product was solving something real, however unglamorous the first ten
customers' path to signing up had been.

### Narrow the customer segment before you try to serve everyone

A related discipline, easy to skip past because it feels like giving up on the eventual
goal: Cheung's advice is to deliberately corner off a specific customer segment early,
rather than building for "everyone" from day one. The eventual product might reasonably
serve nearly anyone, but optimizing for a genuinely broad audience at the start means
optimizing for no one in particular. Whether the chosen slice is teenage girls or soccer
moms, picking one narrow group on purpose lets a founder focus hard enough on that
group's specific needs to actually build something that works for them, rather than a
diffuse compromise that half-works for everyone.

Once real users exist, they need a real way to reach back. Cheung's specific recommendation
is a phone number with voicemail attached, so a founder isn't forced to answer every call
live — but some inbound channel has to exist. That said, an open inbound channel is a
supplement to talking with users directly, not a substitute for it: the real signal comes
from founders going out and initiating those conversations themselves, not waiting for
complaints to arrive. For a faster read on whether things are working than the weeks or
months a full retention curve takes to materialize, Cheung recommends leading indicators
that arrive immediately — star ratings and reviews, or a **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: a
simple 0–10 rating of how likely a user is to recommend the product to a friend.

### Ask what people do, not what they'd like

Emmett Shear's sharpest methodological point, drawn from building Twitch, is about a
mistake that feels reasonable in the moment and quietly ruins most early user research:
asking people directly whether they'd use a specific feature. The honest-sounding answer
you get back is almost worthless, because people are bad at predicting their own future
behavior and good at being polite about ideas that sound plausible. The fix is to ask
about current behavior and existing pain instead — what do you actually do today, what's
frustrating about it, what have you tried — and let the underlying opportunity surface on
its own, rather than asking a user to design your roadmap for you.

This is also why *who* gets interviewed matters so much. When Shear's team first
launched what became Twitch, the obvious move would have been to interview viewers — the
people actually watching game streams. Instead, they focused on **broadcasters**,
because the actual behavior of the market was that people followed specific broadcasters
around, not platforms — content, in Shear's words, brought the viewers with it. Get the
broadcasters right, and the viewers follow. Interviewing the wrong side of a two-sided
market can produce technically honest, carefully collected feedback that still points you
in the wrong direction entirely.

A related discipline: talk to a genuinely varied set of people, not just whoever is
easiest to reach. Six to eight interviews are usually enough to stop learning new
things — but only if those six to eight people are meaningfully different from each
other. Six interviews with people just like you or just like your existing users will
teach you far less than six interviews spanning real extremes — power users and people
who've never tried anything like your product at all, since each of those groups reveals
a different kind of blocker.

Live conversation — in person or over video, not email — matters more than it might
seem. Written exchanges lose the moment where a founder notices something unexpected and
asks "tell me more" on the spot, which is usually where the most valuable insight in the
entire conversation shows up. Shear also recommends recording interviews (with
permission) and playing the recordings back for the rest of the team, rather than just
summarizing findings secondhand — a team hearing a real user struggle in their own words
tends to be far more convinced than a team reading a bullet-point summary of the same
conversation.

### The honesty curve, and why paid feedback is the most valuable kind

Not everyone who gives you feedback is being equally honest, and the pattern is
predictable enough to plan around. For a free product, feedback gets *more* honest the
less the person knows you: your own mother will tell you your product is wonderful
regardless of the truth; friends will be fairly honest, since they care about you but
aren't quite as invested as family; strangers with no relationship to you at all will be
the most bluntly honest of anyone, precisely because they have no reason to spare your
feelings.

Charge money, and the curve changes in an important way: your mother still won't tell
you the truth. But strangers who've paid become dramatically more honest than they were
when the product was free, because now there's money on the line rather than just an
opinion. This is part of why Cheung recommends getting to a paid version of the product
as quickly as is reasonable — not necessarily charging from the very first day, but
getting there fast, because the quality of feedback genuinely changes once real money is
involved.

### Manual before automated, and temporary brokenness over permanent paralysis

At the earliest stage, resist the urge to build automated systems for problems you could
solve manually and learn from instead. Homejoy's early process for accepting cleaning
professionals onto the platform was entirely manual — phone screens, in-person
interviews, trial cleanings, all done by hand, with only a three to five percent
acceptance rate. That manual process is exactly what let the founders learn which
specific questions actually predicted a good or bad cleaner, data that only existed
because a human was paying close attention to every single case. Automating that process
before those lessons were learned would have locked in guesses instead of evidence.

The broader principle behind this: temporary brokenness is far better than permanent
paralysis. Perfection at this stage is close to irrelevant, because whatever you perfect
for ten users won't matter once you have a thousand, and whatever breaks at a thousand
wasn't worth preventing when you only had ten. Build for the size you're at now, not the
size you hope to be at eventually.

One more warning from this same stage: watch for what Cheung calls the Frankenstein
approach — talking to users, collecting a long list of requested features, and building
every one of them in the order they were requested. Most individual feature requests
aren't actually the right fix. They're a proxy for a deeper problem the user is having,
one that a pile of small features tends to paper over rather than solve. The discipline
is to ask why a feature was requested before building it, not to treat every request as
a ready-made spec.

### Knowing when to pivot

Homejoy wasn't Cheung's first idea, or her second. By her own count, it was the
thirteenth concept she and her brother fully built and genuinely tried to make work.
That number is worth sitting with, because most founders quietly assume something is
wrong with them personally when their first idea doesn't take off — not that grinding
through a dozen dead ends might just be a normal part of the process.

Cheung's actual test for when to abandon an idea has three parts: the product genuinely
isn't growing despite real effort, the features you keep shipping don't seem to improve
how many users stick around, or the underlying economics don't work even when everything
else goes right. The hardest of the three to apply honestly is growth, because there are
enough stories of founders who stuck with a struggling idea for years before it suddenly
took off that "just keep going a little longer" always sounds reasonable in the moment.
Cheung's fix for that ambiguity: write down a specific, honest, week-by-week growth plan
before you need one — starting from a single user and doubling from there — so that
"are we on track" becomes a comparison against a number you committed to in advance,
rather than a judgment call made under the emotional pressure of a bad week. Her own rule
of thumb: three or four consecutive weeks of flat or negative growth, while genuinely
executing hard, is a real signal to reconsider — not proof you're a bad founder, and not
something to simply power through by working harder at the same approach.

## Common Mistakes

- **Building in stealth and launching cold, expecting the coverage itself to bring
  loyal users.** Without an early feedback loop, a splashy launch tends to bring a spike
  of visitors who don't stick around, and no data about why.
- **Asking users directly whether they'd use a proposed feature.** The polite,
  honest-sounding "yes" you get back rarely predicts real behavior. Ask about what they
  already do instead.
- **Interviewing whoever is easiest to reach, rather than whoever the business actually
  depends on.** Twitch's own early team nearly optimized for viewers before realizing
  broadcasters were the side of the market that actually mattered most.
- **Automating a process before you've learned enough, by hand, to know what the
  automated version should actually do.**
- **Treating every user feature request as something to build, instead of a clue about
  an underlying problem worth understanding first.**
- **Waiting for a polished, complete product before shipping**, on the theory that a
  competitor might otherwise copy an unfinished idea — when the real risk is spending
  months building the wrong thing with no outside feedback at all.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Doesn't "become a cog in the industry" contradict Chapter 2's point about not needing
startup expertise?** No — they're about different kinds of expertise. Chapter 2's
argument (via Paul Graham) is that you don't need to be an expert in *starting
companies*. This chapter's argument is that you do need to become an expert in the
*specific industry and users* you're building for — which is exactly the "expertise in
your users, not in startups" distinction Chapter 2 already drew. Cheung became an expert
cleaner. She didn't need to become an expert in venture financing to do it.

**What if you can't literally do the job yourself, the way Cheung cleaned houses
herself?** Chapter 2 already covered the relevant exception: a founder who is a genuine
domain expert — someone who spent years actually working inside the industry
professionally, even if not as a hands-on practitioner of the exact task — can close much
of the same gap. The point isn't that founders must physically do the job. It's that
someone on the team needs to have lived close enough to the problem to know what an
outsider's research would miss.

**Does user research work the same way across every market?** Not entirely — Shear is
candid that international and cross-language user research is genuinely harder, and that
even careful efforts to interview users in a market with a different language tend to
oversample people who are already unusually fluent in the founders' language, which
skews the sample in a way that's difficult to fully correct for. It's still worth doing;
it's just worth knowing the sample is imperfect in a specific, predictable direction.

**Is talking to users the same thing as watching how they actually use a shipped
product?** No, and it's worth keeping the two separate. Early discovery interviews — the
subject of most of this chapter — are about deciding what to build in the first place.
Once something is shipped, in-product feedback tools and usage analytics answer a
different question: where does the thing you already built break down for real users.
Both matter. Neither substitutes for the other.

## Applying the Test

Take a feature or product idea you're currently considering, and write down the direct
question you'd be tempted to ask a user about it — something like "would you use a
feature that does X?" Now rewrite it as a behavioral question instead: what do you
currently do to solve this problem, and what's frustrating about that? Notice how much
harder the second question is to answer with a polite, meaningless "yes." That
difficulty is the whole point — it's forcing a real answer instead of a comfortable one.

Then check who you'd actually ask. If your honest answer is "whoever's easiest to
reach," identify the two most different people you could talk to instead — someone deep
inside the exact behavior you're trying to change, and someone who hasn't engaged with
anything like it at all. The gap between what those two people tell you is usually where
the real insight is hiding.

## Chapter Summary

- Storyboard the entire user experience — how someone finds you, signs up, uses the
  product, and comes back — before writing any code.
- MVP means minimum *viable* product. Cutting features is fine; cutting out the thing
  that actually solves the user's problem is not.
- Become a genuine insider in the industry you're building for, even though "disruption"
  culture says outsiders see more clearly — Cheung became a cleaner, then worked at an
  existing cleaning company, before building Homejoy.
- Position the product in one plain sentence ("get your place cleaned for $20 an hour"),
  and recruit your first users manually, however unglamorous that looks.
- Ask about current behavior and existing frustration, not whether someone would use a
  proposed feature — the honest-sounding answer to the second question is usually
  worthless.
- Who you interview matters as much as what you ask — Twitch's early team had to
  interview broadcasters, not viewers, because broadcasters were the side of the market
  that actually drove behavior.
- Feedback gets more honest the less someone knows you, and paid feedback beats free
  feedback, because real money changes what people are willing to say. Do things
  manually before automating, and treat feature requests as clues, not ready-made specs.
- Know your test for abandoning an idea in advance: no real growth despite real effort,
  features that don't improve retention, or economics that don't work — Cheung's own
  Homejoy was the thirteenth idea she tried before one of them met that bar.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain why asking a user "would you use this feature?" tends to produce
   an answer that doesn't predict real behavior, and what question to ask instead.
2. **Apply:** Storyboard the full experience of a product idea you're considering — from
   how someone first hears about it to what happens after their first use. What does the
   smallest *viable* version of that story actually require?
3. **Apply:** Identify two people you could interview about a product idea who are
   genuinely different from each other — not just two people who happen to be easy to
   reach. What would each of them likely teach you that the other couldn't?
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter argues you should become a genuine
   insider in your target industry before building for it. But Chapter 2 also covered
   founders who succeeded by "living in the future" of a technology they understood
   deeply, without necessarily being industry insiders in the market they disrupted (the
   VoIP graduate student, for instance). Reconcile these two pieces of advice — when does
   each one apply?
