# Appendix C — Unit Economics Formula Sheet

A quick-reference sheet for the formulas introduced across this book, gathered in one
place. Each entry names the chapter where the concept was first explained in full — flip
back there for the reasoning and worked examples behind the formula.

## Growth and Retention (Chapters 10–11)

**Retention rate** — the percentage of a cohort still active after a given time period.
Plot it against time since acquisition to get a retention curve; an asymptotic
(flattening) curve signals real product-market fit.

**North Star metric** — not a formula, but a discipline: the single number (monthly
active users, nights booked, messages sent) the whole company optimizes toward.

**Viral coefficient (K-factor)**

> K = (invites sent per user) × (conversion rate of those invites)

K above 1 means a user base grows on its own from virality alone; below 1, it eventually
flatlines without other growth channels.

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**

> CAC = Cost per click ÷ Conversion rate

**CLV / LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)** — the total net revenue a customer generates
over the time they remain a customer, typically calculated over a fixed window (a
"12-month CLV," for instance).

**Unit economics profit per customer**

> Profit = CLV − CAC

A positive number means the channel is worth scaling; a negative one means you're paying
more to acquire customers than they're worth.

**Payback period** — how long it takes a customer's revenue to repay the CAC spent to
acquire them. A 3-month payback is a conservative, safe benchmark; 12 months is riskier
but sometimes reasonable for a well-capitalized company; beyond 12 months moves into
territory where a company is betting its cash position on an unproven prediction.

## Financial Statements and Company Health (Chapter 15)

**Burn rate** — net cash spent per month (expenses minus revenue).

**Runway**

> Runway (in months) = Cash in the bank ÷ Monthly burn rate

Every founder should be able to state this number from memory.

**P&L (Profit and Loss statement)** — revenue minus expenses over a period of time.

**Balance sheet** — a snapshot, at one moment, of assets (what the company owns) minus
liabilities (what it owes), equal to equity (what's left over).

**Cash flow statement** — actual cash moving in and out, distinct from the P&L's
"profit," since revenue is counted when earned, not necessarily when the cash arrives.

## Fundraising Mechanics (Chapters 13–15, Appendices A–B)

**SAFE / note valuation cap conversion (post-money style)**

> Investor's locked-in ownership = Investment ÷ Post-money valuation cap

**Pre-money vs. post-money valuation**

> Post-money valuation = Pre-money valuation + New money raised
>
> New investor's ownership = New money raised ÷ Post-money valuation

**Vesting schedule (standard 4-year, 1-year cliff)** — 0% vested before month 12; 25% at
month 12; the remaining 75% vests monthly over the next 36 months.

## A Reminder Before You Use Any of These

Every formula on this page is only as useful as the segment-level detail behind it.
Chapter 11 makes this point directly about CAC and CLV: a single blended average across
your entire customer base can look healthy while hiding a badly unprofitable segment
sitting right next to a highly profitable one. Calculate these numbers by customer
segment, by channel, and by cohort — never just once, for the whole company, and call it
done.
