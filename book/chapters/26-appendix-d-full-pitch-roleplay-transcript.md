# Appendix D — The Full Pitch Roleplay Transcript

Chapter 14 condensed the lessons from a live mock-pitch exercise run in the original
course, led by YC partner Dalton Caldwell with a founder (referred to as Qasar)
role-playing both a weak pitch and a strong one back to back. The full, unedited
exchange is reproduced here, exactly as it happened, for readers who want to study the
complete back-and-forth rather than the chapter's summary of its lessons.

## The First Pitch (Weak)

**Dalton:** Qasar, I understand you're coming to pitch me today. What can you tell me
about what you do?

**Qasar:** We're building a communication platform that will allow businesses and
consumers to collaborate on one single platform rather than in the fractured state that
they're in right now.

**Dalton:** I don't follow.

**Qasar:** Think about WhatsApp or Snapchat. Those are for consumers. We want to do that
for businesses. What that means is we want to enable consumers to talk to businesses.
That's the goal of our business, or what our startup is.

**Dalton:** Who uses this product? What does the product do?

**Qasar:** It's for consumers and businesses. A messaging product that allows consumers
to send —

**Dalton:** Why would a consumer want to use your product?

**Qasar:** Because they want to message a business.

**Dalton:** What can you tell me about the market and the opportunity? What's the size
of this company?

**Qasar:** Messaging companies are really big, obviously. WhatsApp sold for 19 billion
dollars. Snapchat is really growing very quickly as well. We think the opportunity is
very big.

**Dalton:** Can you tell me a little bit about your traction, your numbers? Have you
given this to people yet?

**Qasar:** We don't want to open the kimono and go into all the details here. At a high
level, we definitely have thousands of users in the Bay Area. Hundreds of businesses.

**Dalton:** Can you tell me who some of those businesses are?

**Qasar:** There's ones that you've been to. We don't really want to get too much into
the details because we're still early — we're trying to stay stealth.

**Dalton:** Okay, well, can you tell me about what you've learned so far? What insights
have you had from customers?

**Qasar:** Yeah, the consumers are sending messages to these businesses. And we think
that's great. And these businesses are responding to the messages, and we think that's —
I don't think it's obvious that would happen.

**Dalton:** So can you tell me about what your business model is and how —

**Qasar:** Yeah, so we charge businesses like a monthly rate. We haven't precisely
figured out what that is. Right now we're free for the few hundred companies we're in
right now, but we're looking to probably do a monthly —

**Dalton:** How much do you think a business would be willing to pay?

**Qasar:** We think certainly ten to fifteen thousand dollars a month...

**Dalton:** Okay. So, can you tell me a little bit about your team and who you have
working on this?

**Qasar:** Yeah, we have five founders. Technically I am the only one who's full-time
right now. We're raising money so we can get the rest of the team on board.

**Dalton:** Can any of the founders program?

**Qasar:** Yeah. One of them has a Bio PhD but he's really picked up coding. I'm a Python
developer — I learned Python the hard way.

**Dalton:** Look at the time. Well, it's been really great meeting you. Please keep me
in the loop. This sounds fantastic.

**Qasar:** I will send you an update.

**Dalton:** Just keep me in the loop as this progresses.

**Qasar:** I'll send you an update. [Breaking character] Great, that was awful.

## The Second Pitch (Strong)

**Dalton:** Qasar, I understand you have a company. Can you tell me a little bit about
what you guys do?

**Qasar:** Yes, we're a messaging product — that's kind of vague. What we allow you to
do is essentially message a location. When you walk into a Crate and Barrel, you can
send the manager a message like, "Hey, there's puke in the hallway." Or if you're in the
airport: "I can't find this specific gate — where's the terminal for Virgin?" Or at
Target: "What aisle is the shampoo in?"

**Dalton:** So is this a mobile app?

**Qasar:** On the consumer side we have an iOS and Android app, but getting consumers to
download apps is obviously very difficult.

**Dalton:** I don't usually download an app just to send a message to Crate and Barrel.

**Qasar:** Most businesses have a call to action that says "text the owner directly." We
tested a bunch of copy, and what works best is small print saying the messages are
anonymous — that lowers the barrier to entry. The most counterintuitive thing we've
learned in the launch we've had — three hundred fifty locations in the Bay Area, about
three months in, roughly 11% weekly growth in participating businesses — is around
whether people would actually send messages at all. We weren't sure that would work.

**Dalton:** Do people send messages?

**Qasar:** They do.

**Dalton:** What's the number one type of message people send?

**Qasar:** We originally thought this would be in-location feedback — that was the
premise. What we found is more than half the messages aren't about feedback at all.
People ask things like — there was a kebab stand in San Jose where messages came in
asking "are you hiring?" Which is strange, because you'd think they'd just ask the owner
directly. But the person walking in doesn't know who the owner is, and we do, so texting
through us is actually the easier path for them.

**Dalton:** Okay, so it's like a suggestion box — a way to message a business?

**Qasar:** That's what we initially thought it was. But what we actually discovered is
that over half the messages are just things like: "When do you open? When do you
close?" — because that's not on Google. "Are you catering? Do you have reservations
tonight?" That kind of thing.

**Dalton:** Okay. In terms of your traction, tell me what you guys have right now.

**Qasar:** Three hundred fifty businesses, San Jose to San Francisco. We sold all of
them ourselves — the three of us are all technical, but we did all the sales, because we
learned a lot about how these businesses actually work that way. We come from a retail
background. We originally built this for large enterprise players — Starbucks, Walmart —
but we realized closing those contracts wasn't realistic on our runway, so we got the
product into users' hands with small businesses instead, and that's when we discovered
this messaging pattern.

**Dalton:** That sounds interesting. It sounds like you have customers. How can this be
big, though?

**Qasar:** In terms of numbers, we see about one and a half messages on average per
location per day. That might not sound like much, but compare it to a Yelp or Google
review — a business might get five or seven of those in its entire lifetime. So this is
a much higher volume of engagement than what they're used to, and it's private, not
public. As for how we actually make money — frankly, we don't have a fully clear answer
yet. There are two paths: the small-business side, or the large-customer side. Large
customers, from our retail experience, pay three to four million dollars a year for
feedback tools like this — think Sears, where we came from. Small businesses, in
testing, are willing to pay around fifty dollars a month. I think this can be a large
business, but there are two genuinely different paths to get there.

**Dalton:** I can see that. Can you tell me about your distribution strategy, and a
little about the team?

**Qasar:** On distribution — what we learned selling to small businesses is that it's
genuinely hard: LTV minus CAC just doesn't work out well in that segment on its own. So
we have two paths. One is going up-market, back toward Starbucks or Walmart-scale
customers. The other is partnering with consumer-facing companies — Yelp, Google,
Facebook.

**Dalton:** Have you talked with them? Are they actually going to do it?

**Qasar:** We've talked to Google and Facebook, and we're meeting with Apple. The idea
is that every time you search for a business, there should be a message button — we want
consumers in the habit of knowing they can text any business directly. That gets us
broad distribution. Our real vision is to become the messaging infrastructure between
consumers and businesses. If those partnerships don't materialize — if Google, Facebook,
and Yelp don't want to give up that surface — we still have a real business simply
selling this as a feedback tool to large players directly.

**Dalton:** Tell me a little about the team — we're running low on time.

**Qasar:** There are three of us, all technical. Mike and I started a company together
before — it failed, so I don't know if that's a point in our favor or not — and Sonny
was an engineer at [a major tech company] before this. We come from retail. We built
everything ourselves, and we sold everything ourselves.

**Dalton:** Okay.

**Qasar:** We've already had a couple of conversations with your firm. We're raising
five hundred thousand dollars on an $8.5 million convertible note. Of that, two hundred
fifty thousand is already committed by [named angel investors], and [a named seed fund]
is willing to fill the rest of the round. We think you and your firm could bring real
value given your retail experience — is this something you'd be interested in?

**Dalton:** Yeah, I think this is really interesting. I'd need to talk to a couple more
people on my side, but I do think this could be pretty big.

**Qasar:** Since we've already had a couple of conversations, we're certainly willing to
meet again — we're closing the round this Friday, so I'll follow up with you before
then. We'd love to have you in the round.

**Dalton:** Okay, sounds good — I've got to run, but thanks for that.

## Reading It Cold

Notice, reading the two side by side, that neither pitch differs in format, length, or
enthusiasm. Every difference traces back to the seven components Chapter 14 named:
a concrete one-sentence description instead of a vague one, real numbers instead of
evasion, a genuine and specific insight instead of a generic claim, an honest answer on
business model instead of a hedge, a real team story instead of a thin one, and — the
thing the first pitch never got to at all — an actual, fully specified ask.
