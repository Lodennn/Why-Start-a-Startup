# Appendix B — Reading a Term Sheet Clause by Clause

A term sheet is a short document — usually two to five pages — that lays out the terms
an investor is proposing for a priced round (Chapter 15 covers the difference between a
priced round and an unpriced SAFE or note). It isn't the final legal agreement, but
everything in the final agreement gets negotiated here first. This appendix walks
through the clauses you'll actually see, in the order they usually appear, so that
reading one for the first time doesn't feel like reading a foreign language.

**Valuation (pre-money and post-money).** Pre-money valuation is what the company is
considered worth *before* the new investment. Post-money is pre-money plus the new
money being raised. If a term sheet proposes a $20 million pre-money valuation on a $5
million raise, the post-money valuation is $25 million, and the new investor is buying
5/25, or 20%, of the company. Every other percentage in the term sheet is anchored to
this one number.

**Liquidation preference.** This determines who gets paid first, and how much, if the
company is ever sold or liquidated — before any remaining proceeds get split by
ownership percentage. The standard, investor-friendly-but-normal version is a **1x
non-participating preference**: in an exit, preferred shareholders get the larger of
either (a) their original investment back, or (b) their pro-rata share of the proceeds
as if they'd converted to common stock — whichever is worth more to them. Watch for two
variations that shift the balance meaningfully toward investors: a preference **higher
than 1x** (meaning investors get back more than they put in before anyone else sees a
dollar), and a **participating** preference (meaning investors get their preference
*and then also* participate in the remaining proceeds pro-rata, effectively double-
dipping). Both are red flags worth pushing back on if they appear in an early-stage term
sheet.

**Option pool.** Appendix A showed exactly how this affects a cap table. The clause to
watch for is *when* the pool gets created relative to the valuation — a pool created
"pre-money" (before the new investor's money, diluting existing shareholders only) is
standard; be alert to language that would size the pool in a way that dilutes only the
founders and not the incoming investor.

**Board composition.** This clause specifies who sits on the board after the round
closes — commonly some mix of founders, investors, and independent members. Chapter 13
already covered why this matters less in practice than founders fear, as long as trust is
high, but it's still worth reading carefully: a board composition that gives investors
control of the majority of seats this early is unusual and worth understanding exactly
why, if it appears.

**Protective provisions.** A list of specific actions the company cannot take without
investor approval — commonly things like selling the company, raising future rounds on
certain terms, taking on debt, or changing the size of the board. These are normal and
expected. What's worth checking is whether the list is a reasonable, standard set of
major decisions, or an unusually long list that would require investor sign-off on
routine operational decisions.

**Anti-dilution provisions.** These protect an investor if the company later raises
money at a *lower* valuation than this round (a "down round"). The standard,
founder-friendlier version is **broad-based weighted average** — a formula that adjusts
the investor's effective price modestly, accounting for how large the down round is
relative to the company's overall size. The much harsher version, **full ratchet**,
would reprice the investor's entire stake to match the new, lower price regardless of
the down round's size — a term that can be devastating to founder and employee
ownership if it's ever triggered, and one worth resisting in early-stage negotiations.

**Pro-rata rights.** Covered in Chapter 15: the right for this investor to invest again
in future rounds to maintain their percentage ownership. Standard and generally
reasonable, though it does mean founders bear more cumulative dilution as investors
exercise it round after round.

**Drag-along and tag-along rights.** A drag-along clause means that if shareholders
holding a specified majority agree to sell the company, remaining shareholders can be
required ("dragged along") to sell too, on the same terms — this prevents a small
minority from blocking an exit the rest of the company wants. A tag-along clause is the
protective mirror image for smaller shareholders: if a majority holder sells their
shares, minority holders get the right to sell alongside them on the same terms, rather
than being left behind as a minority partner to whoever bought in.

**No-shop / exclusivity period.** Once you sign a term sheet, this clause commits you to
not soliciting or negotiating with other investors for a specified window — commonly 30
to 60 days — while the investor completes due diligence and finalizes the legal
documents. This is standard, but signing a term sheet is a real commitment, even though
it isn't the final binding agreement; don't sign one you're not seriously prepared to
close.

**Founder vesting / re-vesting.** Occasionally, an investor's term sheet will ask
founders to restart their vesting schedule (or vest a portion of previously-vested
shares again) as part of the round, specifically to ensure founders stay motivated and
committed for the new round's investment horizon. This is worth understanding clearly
before agreeing to it, since it directly affects what happens to a founder's equity if
they leave the company after this round closes.

## The One Habit Worth Building

Every clause in this appendix answers the same underlying question from a different
angle: *what happens if things go well, and what happens if they don't.* Read every term
sheet clause twice — once assuming the company is a huge success, and once assuming it
struggles or gets sold for less than everyone hoped. A clause that looks harmless in the
success scenario can look very different in the second one, and that's exactly the
scenario a term sheet's protective language is usually written for.
