# Chapter 19 — How to Operate

Bill Walsh took over the San Francisco 49ers in 1979. The team had just finished a
season at 2 wins and 14 losses — about as bad as a professional football team can be.
Within ten years, under Walsh, the 49ers won three Super Bowls. The first concrete thing
Walsh did as head coach wasn't a new offensive scheme or a star recruit. He wrote a
three-page memo teaching the team's receptionist exactly how to answer the phone.

Keith Rabois, who operated at PayPal, LinkedIn, and Square, tells that story to make a
point this whole chapter is built around: building a company is a fundamentally
different, and in his view harder, task than building a product, and it's won or lost in
details most founders would never think to focus on — because building a company means
taking a group of talented, well-intentioned, but genuinely irrational people, and
getting all of them to execute at a consistently excellent level, on purpose, every day.

## The Core Idea: Your Job Is Editing, Not Writing

Rabois's central device for what a founder or executive's actual job becomes, once
there's a team to operate rather than just a product to build, is a metaphor: you are an
**editor**, not a writer. Writers — everyone else in the company — do the actual work.
An editor's job is different and, done well, largely invisible: simplify what gets
produced, ask the questions that matter most, decide where scarce resources and
attention go, and keep the whole organization sounding like it came from one voice.

None of this replaces the earlier, more chaotic phase of a company's life. Early on,
everything should feel like triage — a constant stream of small fires, each one either a
"cold" that resolves itself or something that looks minor and turns out to be serious if
left undiagnosed. That messiness is a normal, healthy sign of real innovation happening.
The editor's job described in this chapter is what replaces pure triage once a company
has grown past the size where a founder can personally touch every decision.

## Why It's True: What an Editor Actually Does, and the Tools for Delegating Without Losing Control

### Simplify, ask questions, allocate, and keep one voice

The first and most important editing skill is **simplification** — taking a
complicated, sprawling set of initiatives and cutting it down to two or three things a
team can repeat without having to think hard about them. Complexity is not a legitimate
excuse for failing to do this: some of history's largest companies are built around
concepts simple enough to describe in a sentence, the same discipline Chapter 2 already
established for evaluating ideas in the first place. If a founder can't simplify their
own priorities down this far, the team underneath them has no real chance of executing
consistently.

The second skill is asking **clarifying questions** — not every possible question, but
the small number of highest-value ones that reveal what actually matters in a given
decision. A useful, checkable measure of whether this is working: the amount of "red
ink" — corrections, simplifications, questions — a leader has to apply should decrease
over time, as the team internalizes the underlying standard for itself rather than
needing it re-explained on every project.

The third is **allocating resources** — sometimes top-down, moving people onto a new
priority because the company's needs have shifted, and sometimes bottom-up, letting
people pitch their own initiatives and deciding which to approve. Andy Grove, whose 1982
book *High Output Management* is a major influence on this entire chapter, defined a
manager's actual job as maximizing the *output* of their organization — not the *motion*
of it, a distinction easy to lose sight of when a team is visibly busy but not
necessarily producing the right things.

The fourth is maintaining a **consistent voice** — making sure a company's website, PR,
recruiting page, and customer support all feel like they came from the same underlying
sensibility, the same way a well-edited publication reads consistently no matter which
individual byline appears on a given piece. Even famously disciplined companies struggle
with this fully — internal tools and recruiting pages inside Apple, even under Steve
Jobs, reportedly never quite matched the polish of the company's public-facing products.
The goal isn't perfection. It's noticing where the voice breaks down and treating that as
a real signal worth fixing, not a cosmetic detail.

### Delegating without abdicating: task-relevant maturity and a simple decision matrix

The hardest part of scaling beyond a founder personally making every decision is
distinguishing real delegation from simply walking away from a decision. Andy Grove's
concept of **task-relevant maturity** is the key variable: how experienced is this
specific person at this specific task, right now — not in general, and not based on
title. A genuinely good manager's style should differ from person to person and from
task to task, rather than being one uniform approach applied to everyone. A useful signal
that this is being done correctly: if a founder runs reference checks on themselves and
some people describe them as a micromanager while others describe them as someone who
hands over enormous freedom, that's not a contradiction to resolve — it's evidence the
calibration is actually working, applied differently where different people warranted
it.

**Figure 19.1 — A simple test for whether to decide yourself or delegate**

```
                 Low confidence in your own judgment   |  High confidence in your own judgment
Low consequence  |  Delegate completely. Let them       |  Delegate, but explain your reasoning —
if wrong         |  make the call, even if it's wrong.  |  don't just assert the answer.
                 |                                      |
High consequence |  Delegate, but explain your          |  Decide yourself. The cost of being
if wrong         |  reasoning — don't just assert.       |  wrong here is too large to hand off.
```

A concrete illustration of the low-confidence, low-consequence quadrant: an early hire
at Square wanted to run a marketing experiment (handing out working payment readers at a
food truck) that Rabois personally doubted would move any meaningful metric. It didn't —
but letting the experiment run cost little, and it taught that employee, directly and
without a lecture, how to evaluate his own future ideas — a cheap price for a lesson that
would otherwise have taken much longer to land. The opposite corner — high confidence,
high consequence — still calls for real explanation rather than simply pulling rank; a
specific signal worth tracking personally is how often a colleague ultimately defers with
some version of "fine, you're the boss" rather than being genuinely persuaded. That
phrase, heard too often, is a sign of spending down trust rather than building it.

### Give people exactly one thing, more often than feels natural

Peter Thiel's practice at PayPal — restricting each person to exactly one clear
priority, and refusing to discuss anything else with them until it was resolved — comes
from a specific insight about human behavior: left with a full list of responsibilities,
most people gravitate toward the problems they already know how to solve (comfortable,
"B+" problems) rather than the harder, highest-impact problem actually in front of them
("A+" problems), because the hard problem doesn't have an obvious first step and is easy
to keep deferring. Rabois's own recommendation is to relax this to two or three
priorities if one feels too rigid, but to keep tracking, explicitly, what would happen if
each person were forced down to exactly one — because that thought experiment reveals
which of the "priorities" on anyone's list is actually load-bearing.

### Barrels and ammunition

A specific, memorable test for who on a team can genuinely drive an idea from concept to
shipped reality — what Rabois calls a **barrel**, as opposed to **ammunition** (people
who do excellent work but need direction rather than generating and driving it
themselves) — is to hand someone a small, almost trivially unimportant task and watch
what happens. Rabois's own example: he wanted cold smoothies delivered to engineers
working late at Square, at a specific time, consistently. Multiple capable people failed
to make this happen reliably. A brand-new intern, on his second day, simply said he'd
handle it — and did, precisely, without further instruction. The task itself didn't
matter. What mattered was learning, cheaply, that this person could be handed a larger
and larger scope over time, expanding it until it eventually broke, which is the actual
process for finding out how much responsibility someone can carry.

The practical ratio worth tracking as a company scales: roughly one barrel for every ten
to twenty pieces of ammunition. Fall too far below that ratio, and a team accumulates
people who all need direction from the same small number of barrels, which caps how much
the company can actually get done regardless of total headcount — a specific,
measurable check against the natural tendency for managers to treat team size itself as
a proxy for importance, rather than measuring actual output relative to team size.

### Transparency, metrics, and the discipline of pairing indicators

Sharing real information broadly — company metrics, board meeting takeaways, notes from
any meeting involving more than two people — helps a growing team stay aligned without
every person needing to be in every room. Chapter 6 already covered how far this can be
pushed and what it costs to sustain (Stripe's own transparency bet, and the real tooling
investment it required once the company grew past its first few dozen people); this
chapter's addition is a specific discipline for the metrics themselves: **pair every
metric with an opposing one**, so that optimizing the first one in isolation can't
quietly damage something else. A fraud-prevention team measured only on lowering the
fraud rate will, predictably, start treating every customer as a suspect — which is why
it needs to be measured on its false-positive rate at the same time. A recruiting team
measured only on hiring volume will lower the bar to hit the number — which is why
interview and hire quality need to be tracked alongside volume, not after the fact.

A related discipline: pay close attention to **anomalies** in your own data, rather than
only the metrics you already expected to matter. PayPal's own core market — power
sellers on eBay — wasn't originally on the company's list of target customers at all. It
was discovered because a small number of eBay sellers had started manually typing "please
pay with PayPal" into their own listings, an anomaly the team almost dismissed before
recognizing it as the actual market they should build for. LinkedIn found something
similar in its own click data: a startlingly large share of all clicks on the site — a
quarter to a third — were people clicking through to view their *own* profile, which
made no obvious sense for a supposedly utilitarian networking tool, until the team
realized users were checking their own profile the way people check a mirror, and that
this vanity-driven behavior correlated with adding more content and endorsements. Neither
insight would have surfaced from a dashboard built only to track the metrics the team
already assumed mattered.

### The unglamorous details that actually decide the outcome

Bill Walsh's own account of turning around the 49ers makes the case that operational
excellence is mostly a matter of a huge number of small details executed consistently,
not one grand strategic insight — his first move, teaching the receptionist how to
answer the phone properly, is the clearest possible illustration of this. Steve Jobs's
insistence that the circuit board inside the original Macintosh be laid out beautifully,
despite the fact that no ordinary customer would ever open the case to see it, is a
similar signal: a company's tolerance for sloppiness in places nobody's watching tends to
show up eventually in places everyone is watching. Food, office space, and the tools a
company gives its own employees fall into this same category — not because any one of
them is individually important, but because a bad lunch or an ugly office becomes
something people quietly gossip about instead of doing focused, creative work, which is a
real and measurable cost even though it never shows up as a line item.

## Common Mistakes

- **Trying to be involved in every decision as a company scales past the size where
  that's actually possible.** The editor metaphor exists specifically because a founder's
  job has to shift from doing the work to shaping how the work gets done.
- **Applying one uniform management style to everyone**, rather than calibrating to each
  person's task-relevant maturity for the specific work in front of them.
- **Measuring a team on a single metric without a paired, opposing one**, which
  predictably produces exactly the kind of narrow optimization (a fraud team that
  alienates every legitimate customer, a recruiting team that lowers its bar) the single
  metric was never meant to reward.
- **Ignoring anomalies in your own data because they don't fit the metrics you already
  expected to track.** PayPal's entire market, and a real piece of LinkedIn's user
  psychology, were both found this way.
- **Treating small operational details (office space, food, internal tools) as beneath a
  founder's attention.** These are exactly the details Bill Walsh's and Steve Jobs's own
  examples suggest actually determine whether a team executes at a high standard
  everywhere else.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Does the "one thing per person" rule feel unreasonably rigid?** It's meant to, and
that's the point — Rabois explicitly allows relaxing it to two or three priorities in
practice. What shouldn't be relaxed is the underlying discipline of asking, for every
person on a team, what would happen if they had only one thing to focus on — because
that question reveals which of their stated priorities is actually driving results and
which is comfortable busywork.

**Isn't measuring "how much red ink you use" a strange way to judge your own
management?** It's counterintuitive precisely because it inverts the instinct to measure
your own value by how much you're personally correcting or directing. The logic is that
a team that has genuinely internalized your standards needs less correction over time —
so declining red ink is a sign of successful teaching, not declining involvement or
relevance.

**How does this chapter's emphasis on systems and delegation relate to Chapter 9's
"doing things that don't scale"?** They describe sequential stages of the same company,
not competing philosophies. Chapter 9 covers the earliest period, before there's enough
signal to know what a good system should even contain. This chapter assumes a company
has already learned those lessons and is building the durable structure — dashboards,
task-relevant delegation, paired metrics — that a founder can't personally staff forever.
Applying this chapter's systems thinking too early, before the hands-on learning Chapter
9 describes has actually happened, tends to produce systems built on guesses rather than
real, hard-won evidence.

## Applying the Test

Pick a task you currently do yourself that a teammate could plausibly take over. Apply
Figure 19.1: how confident are you in your own judgment about it, and how bad would it
actually be if it were done wrong? Use the answer to decide, honestly, whether you should
delegate it completely, delegate it with an explanation of your reasoning, or keep doing
it yourself for now — and if you keep doing it yourself, name the specific thing that
would need to be true before you'd feel comfortable handing it off.

## Chapter Summary

- Building a company is a different, often harder task than building a product — your
  job shifts from writer to editor: simplify, ask the highest-value questions, allocate
  resources, and keep one consistent voice across the whole company.
- Task-relevant maturity, not a uniform management style, should determine how much
  freedom versus guidance each person gets on each specific task.
- Use a simple test — your own confidence, and the consequence of being wrong — to
  decide whether to delegate completely, delegate with explanation, or decide yourself.
- Give people as close to exactly one priority as practical; most people default to
  comfortable, well-understood problems unless forced to focus on the hardest one.
- "Barrels" — people who can drive an idea from concept to shipped reality — are rare;
  test for them with small, low-stakes tasks before handing over anything larger, and
  track your barrel-to-ammunition ratio explicitly.
- Pair every metric with an opposing one, so optimizing one doesn't quietly damage
  something else, and pay close attention to anomalies in your data rather than only the
  metrics you expected to matter.
- Small operational details — how the phone gets answered, what people eat, the quality
  of an internal tool — are not beneath a founder's attention; they set the standard for
  everything else the company does.
