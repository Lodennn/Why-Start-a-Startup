# Chapter 13 — How to Raise Money

About four thousand companies try to raise venture capital in a given year. Roughly two
hundred of those get funded by a top-tier firm. Of those two hundred, about fifteen will
eventually reach $100 million in revenue. Those fifteen companies — out of the original
four thousand — will generate somewhere around 97% of all the returns the entire venture
capital industry makes that year.

Marc Andreessen, co-founder of the venture firm Andreessen Horowitz, shares that number
not to be discouraging, but to explain something that otherwise looks irrational about
how investors behave: venture capital is a business built entirely around finding
extreme outliers, and almost every practice covered in this chapter — what investors
look for, how they price risk, why they sometimes pass on companies that look perfectly
reasonable — makes sense once you understand that a fund's entire return depends on
finding one of those fifteen companies, not on a portfolio of merely solid ones.

## The Core Idea: Fundraising Is About Peeling Away Risk, One Round at a Time

Andreessen borrows a framework from investor Andy Rachleff that reframes what a funding
round is actually for: think of a brand-new startup as carrying an entire onion's worth
of risk, layered one on top of another. Founding-team risk — can these people even work
together. Product risk — can they build the thing at all. Technical risk — does it
require some breakthrough that might not happen. Launch risk. Market-acceptance risk.
Revenue risk. Cost-of-sales risk, if there's a sales force. Viral-growth risk, if the
model depends on one.

Each funding round exists to peel away a specific layer or two of that onion, not to
provide a general cash cushion. A seed round should get you far enough to prove the
founding team can execute and the product can exist. A Series A should get you far
enough to prove real customers want it. Each round's size should be calibrated to the
specific layers of risk it's meant to remove — not simply "as much money as investors
will offer," which is a mistake this chapter returns to directly.

## Why It's True: What Investors Actually Look For, and the Deals That Prove It

### Strength, not the absence of weakness

The default, checkbox way to evaluate a startup goes something like: good founder, check;
good idea, check; good product, check; good initial customers, check. Andreessen's
observation, after years of doing this, is that checkbox deals — reasonable on every
dimension — routinely lack the one thing that actually produces an outlier: an extreme,
specific strength along some dimension that makes the company remarkable rather than
merely solid.

The uncomfortable flip side of this: companies with a genuinely extreme strength
frequently have real, visible flaws elsewhere. If a firm's policy were to avoid every
company with a serious flaw, Andreessen notes, it would have passed on nearly every big
winner in the firm's history. The actual discipline isn't finding flawless companies —
it's finding the specific strength worth betting on, and being willing to tolerate real
weaknesses alongside it, which is a much harder judgment call than working through a
checklist.

Ron Conway, an angel investor who has backed several hundred companies since the 1990s,
adds a specific, practical version of what he's evaluating in the first minute of any
pitch: is this person a natural leader, and are they obsessively focused on the product.
He's hoping the motivation traces back to a personal problem the founder actually had,
not an idea manufactured to look fundable — the exact same instinct Chapter 1's "you
can't not do it" test was built around, arrived at independently by an investor rather
than a founder. Conway's other filter, echoing a principle this book already established
in Chapter 2: a founder should be able to explain what their product does in one
compelling sentence. By his own account, roughly a quarter of the founders he meets still
can't clear that bar after their first sentence — and after enough years doing this, he
simply stops the pitch and asks them to restart.

### Being fundable is easier than almost everything that follows

Andreessen's own comparison, delivered as a direct challenge to the idea that fundraising
is the hardest part of building a company: raising venture capital is, in his assessment,
one of the *easiest* things a founder will ever have to do, compared to recruiting a
twentieth engineer, selling into a large enterprise, or generating real viral growth in a
consumer product. His reference point is a line from comedian Steve Martin's memoir: the
key to success is being so good that people can't ignore you. Applied to fundraising,
that means the highest-leverage use of a founder's time is almost never refining the
pitch deck — it's making the underlying business good enough that raising money becomes
close to a formality.

Parker Conrad's own story, told directly in this same panel, makes the point uncomfortably
concrete. Pitching an earlier company, he and his co-founder were rejected by roughly
sixty different venture firms. At one of those meetings, a partner at Khosla Ventures
told him bluntly: "If you guys were the Twitter guys, you could come in and pitch us
anything and we'd fund you. You're not the Twitter guys, so you need to have all this
analysis ready." Conrad took the opposite lesson from the one the partner intended — not
"prepare better analysis," but "figure out how to actually become the kind of company
that doesn't need the analysis." His next company, Zenefits, was deliberately conceived
as a business that could plausibly grow without ever raising outside money at all — and
that same quality, a business viable on its own, was exactly what made investors want to
fund it eagerly once he did decide to raise.

### Choosing an investor is closer to choosing a spouse than closing a transaction

Both Andreessen and Conway independently describe the investor relationship in the same
terms: a serious startup is potentially a ten-to-twenty year project, which is longer
than the average American marriage, and the investors who join the board for that
stretch of time are people a founder will be relying on through real crises, not just
during the good years. Andreessen's advice: weigh the choice of investor with something
close to the seriousness you'd bring to choosing a life partner — not because the check
size doesn't matter, but because when things go wrong (and on a long enough timeline,
something always does), the difference between an investor with real domain expertise
and trustworthy judgment and one who simply wrote a check becomes the whole story.

Two real investment stories from this same conversation illustrate what that difference
actually looks like in practice. Ron Conway's account of investing in Google in 1999
came entirely through personal trust: a Stanford professor mentioned an early student
project called "BackRub," built around a then-unproven idea — ranking search results by
which pages other people linked to, rather than by keyword matching alone. Conway had to
wait months and prove himself before the founders would even meet with him, and getting
into the deal required bringing in Sequoia Capital first, because the founders wanted
Sequoia's existing relationship with Yahoo secured before they'd accept Conway's money.
Andreessen's account of Airbnb runs the opposite direction: his firm initially passed on
the company entirely, missing it the first time around because — by his own admission —
the idea sounded almost deliberately absurd (a website for staying in strangers' spare
rooms, combined with a website where you could rent out your own spare room to
strangers, which he calls "the second most stupid idea you could possibly have" layered
on top of the actual most stupid one). The firm came back later, at a much higher
valuation, once the numbers had clearly proven the initial read wrong — and the decision
to invest heavily at that point rested as much on directly observing how mature and
grounded the three founders had become under real pressure as on the growth numbers
themselves.

## Common Mistakes

- **Asking investors to sign an NDA before discussing the business.** Both Conway and
  Andreessen treat this as close to a disqualifying signal — professional investors see
  enough deal flow that a request like this reads as distrust rather than caution, and
  it damages the relationship before it starts.
- **Treating a verbal commitment as a real commitment.** Conway's specific fix: as soon
  as an investor commits to something in a conversation, send a follow-up email
  confirming the details in writing immediately — investors, in his experience,
  frequently and genuinely forget specifics later, and a written record avoids relitigating
  it.
- **Raising as much money as investors will offer, rather than exactly enough to peel
  away the next layer of risk.** More capital raised now means more dilution locked in
  permanently, regardless of whether that capital was actually needed for the milestones
  ahead.
- **Choosing an investor primarily for valuation or check size, rather than domain
  expertise and trust.** Andreessen is direct that his own firm will walk away from an
  otherwise-attractive deal if a company's cap table is already too diluted or already
  includes investors who add no real value, because both problems demotivate the founding
  team in ways that outlast any single round.
- **Obsessing over the fundraising process as if it were the actual milestone.** Conway's
  advice: treat it as one of the smallest steps in the company's life and get it over
  with efficiently, rather than treating a successful raise as a personal victory.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Does the onion-theory framework change for capital-intensive businesses (hardware,
biotech, infrastructure)?** The underlying logic stays the same, but the required
precision goes up substantially. Andreessen's advice for these cases: be exact about
what each round of a much longer, more expensive funding path is meant to prove, because
the tolerance for wasted capital or a misjudged milestone is much lower when total
capital needs stretch into the hundreds of millions across many rounds. Venture debt and
lease financing become relevant options in this category in a way they rarely are for a
capital-light software company — but both still require the same operational discipline
this chapter's onion framework describes, arguably more of it.

**What percentage of the company is reasonable to sell in a given round?** There's no
universal formula, but rough norms exist: a Series A commonly sells something in the
twenty-to-thirty percent range; a seed round is often closer to ten to fifteen percent.
Ron Conway's specific warning: think in advance about the point at which your own
remaining ownership would start to feel demotivating, because a founder who's already
diluted down to a very small stake after just one or two early rounds has, in a
practical sense, already undermined their own long-term motivation — a problem no later
success can fully undo.

**Does board composition matter as much as founders often fear?** Less than expected, in
both Andreessen's and Parker Conrad's account, as long as trust is genuinely high. Most
of an investor's real influence comes from protective provisions written into the
financing terms, not from formal board votes — and in Andreessen's own two decades on
startup boards, he reports never once being part of an important vote that actually
came down to a close count; the real decisions get worked out through discussion and
consensus well before any formal vote would matter. The exception, worth noting
honestly: when a company gets into serious trouble, the terms of a prior round often get
renegotiated anyway, regardless of what was originally signed — which is itself another
argument for choosing investors who can be trusted to renegotiate fairly, not just
investors who offered the best terms going in.

## Applying the Test

Take your own company's current or hypothetical funding stage, and list the layers of
Andreessen's onion in order: founding-team risk, product risk, technical risk, launch
risk, market-acceptance risk, revenue risk, cost-of-sales risk, viral-growth risk. Mark
which layers your last round of funding (or your current savings, if you haven't raised
yet) was actually meant to peel away. Then ask honestly: is the amount of capital you're
considering raising next sized to remove the *next* layer specifically, or is it sized to
"raise as much as we can get"? If it's the second, that's worth revisiting before you
start the process.

## Chapter Summary

- Venture capital is a business built around extreme outliers — roughly fifteen out of
  four thousand companies a year generate almost all the industry's returns, which
  explains much of how investors actually behave.
- Each funding round should be sized to remove a specific layer of risk (the "onion
  theory"), not simply to raise as much as investors are willing to offer.
- Investors look for extreme strength, not the absence of weakness — checkbox-perfect
  companies frequently lack the one differentiating quality that produces an outlier.
- Being genuinely fundable is, according to Andreessen, easier than almost everything a
  founder does afterward — the highest-leverage move is making the business better, not
  refining the pitch.
- Choosing an investor is closer to choosing a spouse than closing a transaction — a
  ten-to-twenty year relationship where domain expertise and trust matter more than
  check size or valuation.
- Never ask for an NDA, and always follow up investor commitments in writing
  immediately.
- Board seats and formal votes matter less than expected when trust is genuinely high —
  most real influence runs through informal discussion and the terms written into the
  financing itself.
