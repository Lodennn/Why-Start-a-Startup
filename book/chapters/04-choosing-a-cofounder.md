# Chapter 4 — Choosing a Cofounder

One Y Combinator batch, Sam Altman recalls, had about seventy-five companies. Nine of
them added a new cofounder sometime between their interview and the actual start of the
program — someone who joined the team late, after the selection process was already
done. All nine of those nine companies fell apart within the following year.

Not most of them. All of them.

That number should reframe how you think about this decision. Most first-time founders
treat choosing a cofounder as something close to a social accident — you meet someone
who also wants to start a company, you both seem into it, you start a company together.
Altman's blunt assessment of this pattern: "You would never hire someone like this, and
yet people are willing to choose their business partners this way." This chapter is
about why that casualness is dangerous, and what to actually look for instead.

## The Core Idea: You Can Trust Your Instincts About People

Chapter 1 made a point that's worth restating precisely, because this chapter is about
to say something that sounds like its opposite: you cannot reliably know in advance
whether *you* are capable of building a startup. Confidence going in doesn't predict
outcomes.

Paul Graham makes a related but distinct claim about *other people*, and it points the
opposite direction: your instincts about people are actually trustworthy, more
trustworthy than most founders give them credit for. His observation, from watching
founders make this mistake repeatedly: they meet someone who seems impressive, feel some
private unease about them anyway, and override that unease because the person's
credentials or confidence are hard to argue with. Then, months later, when the
relationship falls apart, they say some version of: "I knew there was something wrong
about that guy, but I ignored it because he seemed so impressive."

Graham's advice is almost embarrassingly simple: pick a cofounder the way you'd pick a
friend. Not a colleague, not a business contact — a friend. Work with someone you would
generally like and respect, and whom you've known long enough to actually be sure of,
because plenty of people are extremely good at seeming likable for a while. The real test
only arrives once your interests are in conflict, which is exactly the situation a
new startup manufactures constantly.

These two claims — don't trust your self-assessment (Chapter 1), do trust your read on
other people (this chapter) — aren't in tension. They're about different kinds of
uncertainty. What you're capable of becoming is genuinely unknowable in advance, because
starting a company changes you in ways you can't simulate ahead of time. Who someone
already *is* — whether they're trustworthy, whether they follow through, whether you'd
actually want to be around them during a crisis — is much more visible, if you're
willing to look honestly instead of being dazzled.

## Why It's True: The Data, the Metaphor, and the Wrong Way to Meet Someone

### The choice matters more than almost any other you'll make

Altman is direct about the stakes: cofounder relationships are among the most important
in the entire company, and cofounder blowups are Y Combinator's single most common cause
of early startup death. That's a strong claim to make about one relationship, in a book
full of chapters about products, growth, and fundraising — but the data backs it up.
Looking across the most valuable companies YC has funded, almost all of them have at
least two founders. Y Combinator funds solo founders at roughly a tenth the rate of
teams. The ranking, in Altman's own words, goes: a cofounder you already know well is
best. A solo founder is acceptable — worse, but survivable. A cofounder you barely know,
chosen the way many students choose one, is the worst option on the list, worse than
having no cofounder at all.

That last point is worth sitting with, because it cuts against instinct. It feels safer
to have a partner than to go alone. The data says a bad partner is more dangerous than no
partner.

Reid Hoffman, who co-founded LinkedIn and has invested in dozens of startups since,
frames the reason teams beat solo founders in a way worth stating directly: it explodes
the myth of the "super founder." Popular imagination pictures a founder as someone who
can do everything — product, fundraising, sales, management, strategy — at a world-class
level, alone. In reality, nobody has that full range of skills, and a company that
depends on one person having it is fragile in a way that isn't obvious until something
goes wrong in the exact area that person is weak. Two or three founders, chosen well,
let you cover each other's gaps and split a genuinely superhuman set of demands across
real, ordinary humans. This is also, not coincidentally, the same reason Chapter 1 spent
so much time debunking the myth of the founder as a lone, glamorous decision-maker —
this chapter's version of that myth is simply the same fantasy applied to a team of one
instead of a team of several.

### The James Bond test

Y Combinator has an internal phrase for the kind of cofounder you want: "relentlessly
resourceful." Paul Graham popularized a more memorable, more specific version of the same
idea at YC's own orientation sessions, and Altman has kept using it since: you're looking
for someone who behaves like James Bond.

Not because you need an expert marksman. Because the archetype captures a specific
cluster of traits better than a checklist would: unflappable under pressure, tough,
knows what to do in almost any situation, decisive, creative, ready for anything. Altman's
framing: you need someone who behaves this way *more* than you need someone who happens
to be a deep expert in one particular domain. Domain expertise can be learned or hired
in. Being unflappable under real pressure mostly can't.

Alongside "relentlessly resourceful," a few more specific traits matter: tough, and calm
— especially if you know you aren't naturally either of those things yourself. Smart is
assumed; almost everyone already screens for that and undervalues the rest. And if
you're not a technical person, you want a technical cofounder, particularly if you're
building a software company. Altman is skeptical of the increasingly popular idea that a
non-technical founding team can just "hire the technical people" and focus on being
great managers instead: "Software people should really be starting software companies."

### How many, and how close

Y Combinator's experience points to two or three cofounders as close to the ideal
number. One founder, working alone, is workable but harder. Five is close to
unworkable — too many voices, too much coordination overhead before the company has any
process to manage it. Four sometimes works. Two or three is the real target.

Getting to know a potential cofounder takes time — ideally years, not weeks. This is
part of why "cofounder dating" events, where strangers meet and agree to start a company
within the same afternoon, are close to the worst possible way to find one. Altman's
advice, matter-of-factly: take advantage of whatever environment already puts you around
smart, ambitious people for an extended period — college is one version of this, working
at a company full of people like Facebook or Google's early employees is another. Being
in either environment for years is, in a very literal sense, cofounder-rich in a way that
a one-off networking event never will be.

A modern illustration of what "already knowing someone" looks like at its most extreme,
outside anything covered in the original lectures: Patrick and John Collison, who founded
Stripe, are brothers. That's obviously not a
formula anyone can copy — you can't manufacture a sibling — but the underlying mechanism
is exactly the one this section has been describing. By the time they started a company
together, there was no discovery process left to do. They already knew, in complete and
tested detail, how the other person behaved under real pressure, what they were actually
good at, and whether they could trust each other completely. Most founders have to build
that knowledge deliberately, over years of working or studying next to someone. The
Collisons simply arrived at the starting line already having it.

### Being in the same place

One of Altman's more absolute claims, delivered without much hedging: don't have
cofounders working from different locations. His reasoning is that in the early days of
a startup, speed and constant, informal communication outweigh nearly everything else,
and video calls — even good ones — don't replicate that. His supporting evidence: look
at a list of the most successful software companies of all time and try to find one
where the founders were based in different cities from day one. He couldn't find a clean
example, and treated the absence as close to conclusive.

> **Sidebar: What's Changed Since 2014**
>
> This is one of the few claims in this book that later evidence has genuinely
> complicated. GitLab was built from the start as an entirely remote company and went
> public in 2021 with no central office at all. Zapier's founders were geographically
> distributed from early on and built a large, durable business that way. Neither
> company disproves Altman's underlying point about the value of fast, informal,
> high-bandwidth communication — both invested heavily and deliberately in written
> culture, asynchronous processes, and tooling specifically to compensate for not
> sharing a room. What's changed since 2014 is less "remote cofounders can work" (a few
> examples always existed) and more that an entire toolkit for making it work — better
> video infrastructure, a cultural expectation of async-first communication, a labor
> market used to it — has matured enough that the deliberate substitution is easier to
> pull off than it was when this lecture was given. The underlying warning still holds:
> if you go remote, it has to be a deliberate design choice with real infrastructure
> behind it, not a default you fell into because it seemed easier to start that way.

Reid Hoffman, teaching a related lecture in the same course, makes a companion point
about location that's easy to conflate with Altman's but is actually distinct: it's not
that Silicon Valley is unconditionally the right place to start every company. It's that
different businesses need different networks, and Silicon Valley is unusually good at
supplying the network some specific kinds of businesses need — software, mobile,
marketplaces — and much less good at supplying what other businesses need. His example is
Groupon, which required an enormous, old-fashioned sales force as a core part of its
early growth strategy. That kind of plan — rent a large office, staff multiple floors
with salespeople — tends to get criticized rather than funded in Silicon Valley, where
investors and talent are oriented around capital efficiency and network effects instead.
Groupon started in Chicago, where that model wasn't a strange or unfundable idea. Hoffman's
broader point: ask what kind of company you're actually building, and go to wherever the
right network for *that* kind of company already exists — which is usually Silicon
Valley for the categories this book focuses on, but isn't automatically true for every
category of business.

### Decide the equity split early, and make it near-equal

There's one conversation founders reliably put off, for no good reason: how to split
the equity. Altman has watched founders delay this for months, sometimes structuring
their own incorporation paperwork in strange ways specifically to avoid having the
conversation. His observation: this isn't a discussion that gets easier with time. It
gets harder, because the longer you wait, the more each person's sense of what they've
already contributed hardens into a position. The right time to have it is near the very
start — ideally within the first few weeks of deciding to work together, before the
company has gotten far enough along for the conversation to feel like it's litigating
the past.

The split itself should be close to equal. This isn't just a fairness norm — it's a
diagnostic. If you find yourself unwilling to give a prospective cofounder a genuinely
equal share, Altman's advice is to treat that reluctance as information: it's a sign you
should think hard about whether you actually want them as a cofounder at all, rather than
a sign you should negotiate them down. A cofounder relationship you're already
structuring to protect yourself from is a relationship with a problem in it from day one.

None of this replaces the legal mechanism that actually protects the company if the
relationship falls apart later — vesting, which Chapter 15 covers in full. This section
is about the decision that vesting later locks in, not the paperwork itself: decide early,
decide close to equal, and treat any resistance to doing either as a signal worth
listening to.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating cofounder selection with less rigor than hiring an employee.** You would
  never hire someone you'd known for an afternoon into your first ten employees. Doing
  the equivalent with a cofounder — someone who will own a much larger share of the
  company and be much harder to remove — is a worse version of the same mistake.
- **Meeting a cofounder through a "cofounder dating" event or a cold introduction and
  starting immediately.** The track record for teams that don't already know each other
  well is poor, and it doesn't improve much even when both people seem genuinely
  talented individually.
- **Ignoring a private sense of unease about someone because they seem impressive.**
  This is exactly the mistake Paul Graham describes founders making again and again —
  and by the time it's confirmed, the equity and the company structure are already
  built around the person.
- **Building a solo-founder company by default, rather than by genuine necessity.** A
  solo founder can succeed, but the data suggests it's a real, measurable handicap —
  worth accepting only if the alternative is a bad cofounder, not as a first choice.
- **Choosing a location based on where you happen to be, rather than where the network
  your specific business needs actually exists.**

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Doesn't this chapter's "trust your instincts about people" contradict Chapter 1's "you
can't tell if you're capable"?** No, but it's worth stating clearly why not, since they
sound similar on the surface. Chapter 1's claim is about self-knowledge under a kind of
change you haven't experienced yet — you can't know who you'll become once a startup
takes over your life, because nothing in most people's prior experience simulates that.
This chapter's claim is about reading someone else's existing character: whether they
follow through, whether they're honest under pressure, whether you'd trust them when it's
hard. That's a different kind of judgment, built from ordinary life experience picking
friends and colleagues, and Graham's argument is that this ordinary judgment transfers
just fine — the mistake founders make isn't lacking the instinct, it's overriding the
instinct they already have.

**Are there real exceptions to "never have remote cofounders"?** Yes, and the sidebar
above covers the clearest ones (GitLab, Zapier). The honest synthesis: the underlying
reason for Altman's advice — fast, high-bandwidth, informal communication matters more in
the first year than almost anything else — hasn't gone away. What's changed is that it's
now possible to build that bandwidth deliberately without sharing a room, if you invest
in it as seriously as those companies did. Choosing to be remote by default because it's
convenient, without that investment, is still exactly the trap Altman is warning against.

**Is a solo founder ever the right call?** Occasionally — some of the highest-valued YC
companies have had solo founders, and Altman doesn't claim it's impossible, only that the
data shows it's harder. The honest framing from this chapter and the last one combined:
a solo founder who is genuinely compelled (Chapter 1's test) and genuinely capable of
covering both the technical and non-technical demands of the business is a real,
survivable path. It's a worse bet than a great cofounder team, and a much better bet than
a mediocre one.

## Applying the Test

Think about a specific person you've considered starting a company with. Run Graham's
test directly: if you strip away their resume, their confidence, and how impressive they
seemed the first time you met them, would you still describe them the way you'd describe
a genuine friend — someone you like, respect, and have actually seen operate under some
kind of pressure? Not "someone impressive I'd enjoy being associated with." Someone whose
character you'd vouch for even if their startup failed and they had nothing to show for
it.

Then run the reference-check version of the same test, the way you would for a serious
hire rather than a friend: ask someone who has actually worked with them, closely, under
stress, whether they were still the same person once things got hard. If the honest
answer to either question is uncertain, the data in this chapter says that uncertainty is
itself the answer — not a detail to resolve after you've already started the company
together.

## Chapter Summary

- Cofounder blowups are Y Combinator's most common cause of early startup death — in one
  batch, all nine companies that added a late, unfamiliar cofounder failed within a year.
- You can't reliably judge your own future capability (Chapter 1), but you can and should
  trust your instincts about another person's character — the mistake is overriding that
  instinct because someone seems impressive.
- The ranking that matters: a cofounder you already know well, then a solo founder, then
  — worst of all — a cofounder you barely know.
- "Relentlessly resourceful," embodied by the James Bond archetype, matters more than
  deep domain expertise in a cofounder; domain expertise can be hired in later.
- Two or three cofounders is the practical target. Getting there usually takes years of
  actually knowing someone, not a single event designed to pair up strangers.
- Being in the same physical location remains the default advice, though remote-first
  companies since 2014 (GitLab, Zapier) show it can work with serious, deliberate
  investment in communication — not as a default.
- The right location for a company depends on which network that specific business
  needs, not just where Silicon Valley happens to be strong.
- Decide the equity split early and keep it near-equal. Reluctance to give a cofounder an
  equal share is itself useful information about whether you actually want them as a
  cofounder.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain, in your own words, why "trust your instincts about people" isn't
   a contradiction of Chapter 1's claim that you can't reliably judge your own future
   capability.
2. **Apply:** Think of someone you've considered as a cofounder. Run the reference-check
   test from this chapter's "Applying the Test" section on them honestly. What did you
   learn that you hadn't consciously admitted to yourself before?
3. **Apply:** If you don't currently have a cofounder, identify one environment in your
   life right now (school, work, a community) that's genuinely cofounder-rich in the way
   this chapter describes. What would it take to actually spend real time there, rather
   than treating cofounder-finding as a one-off event?
4. **Confront a counterexample:** GitLab and Zapier both succeeded as deliberately remote
   companies, directly against Altman's advice in this chapter. What specifically would
   you need to see in your own founding team's plan to know whether you're doing what
   they did — building real infrastructure for remote collaboration — versus just
   defaulting to remote because it's easier to start that way?
