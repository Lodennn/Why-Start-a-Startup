# Chapter 6 — Designing Company Culture

In the fall of 2008, Airbnb had almost no revenue, no real traction, and founders who
were paying for the company on a stack of credit cards. Around that time, Paul Graham
asked Brian Chesky whether people were actually doing this — renting out rooms in their
own homes to strangers over the internet. Chesky said yes. Graham asked what was wrong
with them. It wasn't a promising interview.

That same fall, during the Democratic and Republican National Conventions, the founders
needed money and came up with something almost nobody would have predicted from a
"travel" company: breakfast cereal. Obama-O's — "the breakfast of change" — and Cap'n
McCain's — "a maverick in every bite." General Mills wouldn't return their calls. A local
printer made them a thousand boxes. The story hit national news within a week, and the
founders sold $40,000 worth of cereal — more than the $5,000 the actual Airbnb website
had made that entire year.

Chesky doesn't tell this story because it's funny, though it is. He tells it because it's
the origin of one of Airbnb's actual, permanent core values: **be a cereal
entrepreneur** — meaning, be resourceful and creative under real constraint, because
constraint is what forces creativity in the first place. That's the subject of this
chapter: not culture as a poster on a wall, but culture as the set of values a company's
founding moments actually produce, deliberately written down, and then protected for as
long as the company exists.

## The Core Idea: A Fill-in-the-Blank Definition, and Why It's Not Optional

Alfred Lin, an early investor in Zappos and Airbnb, offers a useful skeleton for a
definition that otherwise tends to stay vague: company culture is the **core values**
and the **actions** of every team member, every day, in pursuit of the company's
**mission**. Fill in those three blanks honestly for any company, and you have a
working definition of its actual culture — not the one printed in the employee handbook,
the one people actually operate by.

Why this matters enough to spend a whole chapter on: culture is what a team falls back
on to make decisions when there's no time to ask the founder directly. It's what tells
people what *not* to do — often the more valuable half of the instruction. It's what
lets you tell, cleanly, who should stay and who shouldn't, when the honest answer would
otherwise be complicated and political. And it's not just a soft, feel-good asset:
looking at the Fortune 100 Best Companies to Work For against the S&P 500 and the
Russell 3000 from 1994 to 2013, the "best places to work" index returned roughly 11.8%
— close to double the other two. Trust and strong culture, in other words, show up in
stock returns, not just in employee satisfaction surveys.

Patrick Collison, teaching a related lecture in the same course, offers the sharpest
explanation of *why* culture becomes so important as a company grows, rather than staying
a nice-to-have: culture is a solution to a bandwidth problem. In an ideally small
company, a founder could be involved in every decision. That stops being physically
possible almost immediately — by somewhere around 150 people, in Collison's estimate, it's
completely hopeless. Culture is the thing you deliberately design to stay constant as the
fraction of decisions you can personally touch keeps shrinking toward zero. Get it right
early, and the company keeps making decisions the way you would have, long after you've
stopped being in the room.

## Why It's True: How Real Companies Actually Wrote Theirs Down

### Zappos: a year, thirty-seven values, and a whittling process

When Zappos went through the exercise of defining its own values, employees proposed
thirty-seven candidates. The company spent roughly a year narrowing that list down to
about ten. That length of time is itself a lesson: a value like "honesty" sounds
appealingly simple, but it's nearly meaningless as written, because no company would
ever claim the opposite. Nobody sets out to build a culture of dishonesty. A real value
has to say something that could plausibly *not* be true of some other company — which is
why it takes real time and real specificity to get right.

Zappos landed on specifics like "deliver wow through service" — not just "great customer
service," but a claim about the *degree* and *quality* of service, tied directly to the
company's actual mission. Alongside it: "be humble," which came from the opposite
direction Alfred Lin recommends founders use when defining values — think about the
people you've disliked working with in the past, and write down the opposite of what
made them difficult. Zappos's founders didn't like working with arrogant people, so
humility became one of their core values, credible precisely because it was a specific
reaction to something real.

One more Zappos habit worth naming directly: prioritizing company, then department, then
team, then self, when smart people disagree. The point isn't hierarchy for its own sake —
it's steering disagreement toward "how do we make this idea better together" rather than
toward "who's right," which matters because a room full of talented people arguing to win
is a very different room from a room full of talented people building on each other's
ideas.

That last point connects to a specific, checkable model of what actually breaks inside
teams, borrowed from Patrick Lencioni's *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team*. The
dysfunctions stack on top of each other, each one causing the next:

1. **No trust** among team members leads to —
2. **No real conflict or debate.** Without trust, people avoid disagreement rather than
   working through it, which means —
3. **No genuine commitment.** If ideas were never actually debated, nobody is sure the
   final decision is right, so nobody fully commits to it, which means —
4. **No accountability.** People who never truly committed don't hold each other, or
   themselves, to the outcome, which means —
5. **No results.**

The practical use of this stack: if a team isn't producing results, the fix usually
isn't "try harder" or "communicate more." It's diagnosing which layer actually broke
first, since accountability and results are downstream problems that can't be fixed
directly — they're symptoms of a trust or conflict problem further up the stack.

### Airbnb: writing the values before hiring anyone at all

Zappos wrote its values down at around a hundred employees — successful, but later than
its own founders wished in hindsight. Airbnb's founders, after visiting Zappos and
learning this, decided to do it earlier: before hiring a single person. As far as Sam
Altman has been able to tell, Airbnb may be the only company in Y Combinator's history to
have written its core values down before making any hire at all.

Chesky's framing for why this mattered so much: your first engineering hire isn't really
one hire. It's closer to a DNA sample for the entire company, because if the company
succeeds, there will eventually be a hundred thousand more people like that first one.
The real question isn't "can this person do the job." It's "do I want to build an
organization out of a hundred thousand people who think and behave the way this person
does." Airbnb's first engineer took four to five months to hire — a number that echoes
Chapter 5's account of Airbnb's five-month search for its very first employee overall,
because it's describing the same hire.

Chesky distinguishes two layers inside "culture," and the distinction matters: there are
**behaviors** — the specific rituals and practices a company follows — and there are
**core values** underneath them. Behaviors can and should evolve over decades. Core
values are supposed to be the three to six things that never change, the things that
would still describe the company fifty years from now even if almost everything else
about how it operates looks completely different. Steve Jobs's own version of this
distinction, quoted by Chesky: "Our products change but our values never have." Chesky's
broader observation is that this isn't unique to companies — nations do the same thing
with founding declarations, precisely so they can endure past any single leader.

### Missionaries versus mercenaries

The clearest test of whether a company's stated values are real or decorative is what
happens when following them costs something. Airbnb's version of this test arrived in
2011: a company founded by the Samwer brothers, known for aggressively cloning successful
American startups and building them out at extreme speed, copied Airbnb across Europe.
Within thirty days, the clone had hired four hundred people. The pragmatic case for
acquiring them was strong — Airbnb, still only forty employees and seven million dollars
raised at the time, had no real path to matching that speed on its own, and a travel
company that isn't in Europe is missing a critical piece of the map.

Chesky chose not to buy them. His own explanation: "I felt like we were missionaries and
they were mercenaries," and he believed — as a bet, not a certainty — that missionaries
outlast mercenaries in the long run, and that the best response to being cloned was
simply to keep building the real thing better than the clone ever could. This was, by his
own account, a genuinely controversial call inside the company at the time. It's worth
being honest that it wasn't an obviously correct decision in the moment — reasonable
people on Airbnb's own team argued for buying the clone. What makes it a useful example
for this chapter isn't that it was risk-free. It's that Airbnb had actually written down,
in advance, what kind of company it wanted to be — which meant that when a real, costly
decision arrived, there was already a values-based answer to reach for instead of
improvising one under pressure. More than half of Airbnb's revenue eventually came from
Europe anyway.

### Culture and brand are the same coin, seen from two sides

A recurring theme across the founders in this chapter: culture (what you believe
internally) and brand (what customers believe about you) aren't separate projects.
Zappos's own framing is that they're two sides of one coin — whatever a company actually
believes, internally, eventually leaks out into how customers experience it, whether or
not that was intended. Google's internal hiring shorthand — asking whether a candidate
"is Google" — captures the same idea from the inside. There's no such thing, in this
framing, as a good culture or a bad culture in the abstract. There's only a strong
culture or a weak one, and a strong culture that fits one company might be entirely wrong
for another.

Airbnb learned this the hard way with its own external messaging. Its early tagline —
"forget hotels, save money with Airbnb" — described the company as a cheaper substitute
for something else, which is a fundamentally weak position: a company selling on price
alone is, in Chesky's words, "a utility," and utilities get commoditized and priced down
over time. The company's later language — "travel like a human," built around belonging
rather than savings — matched what Airbnb's founders actually believed the company was
for. Steve Jobs made the identical pivot at Apple with the 1997 "Think Different"
campaign: not a word about processor speed or specifications, entirely about what the
company believed. Chesky's summary of a founder's actual communications job: you
articulate the vision, then keep repeating it — in recruiting conversations, investor
meetings, press interviews, customer emails — until it's said itself so many times that
it's simply what people believe about the company, largely because it's true.

### Transparency scales, but not automatically

Culture at ten people and culture at two hundred people require different tools, even if
the underlying values stay fixed. Stripe's founders made an unusually aggressive bet on
one specific value — internal transparency — as the company grew, and their experience
shows both the upside and the real cost of taking a value that seriously. Early on,
nearly every email at Stripe went to every employee by default, on the theory that
shared context eliminates a large share of the meetings and misunderstandings that
otherwise accumulate as a company grows. Ben Silbermann of Pinterest, comparing notes
with Stripe's founders, describes the underlying design philosophy well: culture is more
like gardening than architecture. You plant values early, and then you spend ongoing
effort pulling weeds and helping the right things grow — it's not a structure you build
once and leave alone.

The costs of Stripe's transparency bet were real, not hypothetical. At roughly fifty
employees, new hires reported being overwhelmed rather than informed — too much email to
find what actually mattered to them. At one point, internal email volume grew large
enough to break Gmail itself. The fix wasn't abandoning the value; it was building
purpose-made tooling (mailing-list structures, a company-wide shared-documents system)
and establishing social norms about what should and shouldn't be shared as openly, once
open sharing itself was happening in front of a much larger and more anonymous audience
than the first ten people in one room. The lesson generalizes past transparency
specifically: a value that worked effortlessly at ten people usually needs real,
deliberate infrastructure to keep working at two hundred — it doesn't scale for free just
because it was true from day one.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating culture as a one-time document instead of a daily habit.** Alfred Lin's
  comparison: culture is like fitness. You can't do a single hard session and then coast
  — skip it for a while, and it deteriorates the same way physical fitness does.
- **Writing values so generic that no company would ever claim the opposite.** "Honesty"
  and "integrity" sound safe, but they don't tell anyone how to actually behave
  differently from a competitor. A real value should be specific enough to be
  falsifiable.
- **Interviewing only for skill, not for whether someone actually believes in the
  mission.** A brilliant hire who doesn't believe in what the company is doing tends not
  to put in the sustained effort the mission actually requires.
- **Assuming a value that worked at ten people will keep working at two hundred without
  any new investment.** Stripe's own transparency bet needed real tooling and new norms
  once it stopped being ten people in one room.
- **Only discovering what the company's values mean under pressure, instead of before
  it.** Airbnb had already decided, before the Samwer brothers ever appeared, that it
  wanted to be a company of "missionaries" — which is what made a genuinely hard decision
  resolvable instead of just another argument.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Was the missionaries-versus-mercenaries decision actually correct?** Worth being
honest here: it's not possible to prove, even in hindsight, that acquiring the Samwer
brothers' clone would have gone worse for Airbnb. The pragmatic case for buying speed
in a new, unfamiliar continent was real, and reasonable people inside the company argued
for it. What this chapter is actually claiming is narrower and more defensible: having a
written, real set of values in advance turned an agonizing, ambiguous decision into one
Airbnb's founders could reason through quickly and stand behind, rather than making it
up under pressure with no anchor at all. A company without that anchor doesn't
necessarily make the wrong call — it just has no faster way to make the right one.

**Isn't a company this focused on mission and values really just a marketing exercise?**
This is a real question people asked Chesky directly, and it's worth taking seriously
rather than dismissing. Sequoia's Doug Leone once told Chesky, half-joking, that his job
was the worst in his entire portfolio: Airbnb is a technology company facing every
challenge a normal software startup faces, plus operating in effectively every country
in the world, plus being a payments company handling billions of dollars with real fraud
and regulatory exposure, plus a trust-and-safety operation on the scale of governing a
mid-size city (Leone's comparison: imagine being responsible, every single night, for
everything that happens among the roughly 425,000 people staying in strangers' homes on
a typical peak night, across 190 countries), plus navigating distinct regulations in
thirty thousand different cities, plus a genuinely hard search-and-matching problem,
since there's no single "best"
listing in a city the way there might be a single best search result for a query. None of
that is marketing. The point of this chapter isn't that culture *replaces* solving those
problems — it's that a real, specific culture is what makes an organization capable of
solving all of them at once, coherently, without a founder personally supervising every
decision.

**Does writing values down early guarantee anything?** No — Zappos wrote its values at a
hundred employees and still built one of the most-cited culture examples in business.
Earlier is better, mainly because it means fewer bad habits have already calcified by
the time you do it. But "we haven't done this yet and we already have fifty employees" is
a reason to do it now, not a reason to conclude it's too late to matter.

## Applying the Test

Try Alfred Lin's actual exercise, the one Zappos used to generate its own list. First,
write down the people you've most enjoyed working with, and name specifically what made
them good to work with — not vague traits, but things you could point to. Then write
down people you've actively disliked working with, and name the opposite. Somewhere in
that contrast is the beginning of a real values list — not one copied from another
company's careers page, but one built from your own specific, lived experience of what
good and bad collaboration actually look like.

Then apply Chesky's harder test to it: if you had to describe your company in three to
six words that would still be true in fifty years, regardless of what the product looks
like by then, what would they be? If the honest answer is closer to "we're not sure yet,"
that's useful information — it means the exercise above is worth doing now, before the
company is large enough to make doing it much harder.

## Chapter Summary

- Culture is the core values and daily actions of every team member, in pursuit of the
  company's mission — not a poster, a real, checkable pattern of behavior.
- Culture matters because it's how decisions get made when a founder can't be involved
  in every one — Patrick Collison's framing: culture is a solution to a bandwidth
  problem that gets worse as the company grows.
- The best-places-to-work index outperformed the S&P 500 and Russell 3000 by nearly 2x
  from 1994–2013 — strong culture shows up in financial returns, not just morale.
- Write values from lived experience — who you loved and hated working with — not from
  generic, unfalsifiable words like "honesty" that no company would ever claim the
  opposite of.
- Separate behaviors (which can evolve for decades) from core values (which shouldn't
  change) — Airbnb wrote its values before hiring anyone; Zappos wrote its own at a
  hundred employees and still succeeded.
- The real test of a stated value is what it costs you to follow it under pressure —
  Airbnb's choice not to acquire a well-funded clone is the clearest example, and it's
  worth being honest that it wasn't a risk-free, obviously correct call.
- Culture and brand are two sides of the same coin — what you actually believe
  internally eventually shows up in how customers experience the company, intentionally
  or not.
- A value that works at ten people doesn't automatically keep working at two hundred —
  Stripe's transparency bet needed real tooling and new norms as the company grew.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain, in your own words, why "honesty" is a weak core value even
   though it sounds important, and what would make a value strong instead.
2. **Apply:** Run Alfred Lin's exercise from this chapter's "Applying the Test" section:
   list people you've loved and hated working with, and derive three to six specific
   values from the contrast.
3. **Apply:** Describe a real or hypothetical situation where following your own stated
   values would cost your company something concrete — money, speed, or a deal. If you
   can't think of one, what does that suggest about whether the values are specific
   enough yet?
4. **Confront a counterexample:** Airbnb's decision not to acquire its European clone is
   presented as a values-driven success story, but this chapter admits it wasn't provably
   the correct call. Under what circumstances would a values-driven decision actually be
   the *wrong* one for a company to make, and how would you tell the difference in the
   moment rather than only in hindsight?
