# Chapter 21 — Scaling the Company

Sam Altman describes a test he runs whenever he visits a company that's starting to
struggle with growth. He asks the founder: if I walked around and asked ten random
employees what the company's top three priorities are right now, would they all say the
same thing? The founder, every single time, says yes — of course they would, we
announced it at the all-hands three months ago. Altman then actually does it. Every
single time, no two employees list the same three priorities, let alone in the same
order.

The founder isn't lying, and the employees aren't being difficult. The gap is simply
what happens by default once a company grows past the size where everyone naturally
absorbs what matters just by being in the room. This chapter is about that transition —
the specific, structural changes a company needs once it outgrows the informal,
everyone-knows-everything stage that worked fine at ten or twenty people.

## The Core Idea: The Founder's Job Changes, and Flat Structure Breaks All at Once

Before a company has found real product-market fit, a founder's job is building a great
product. Past roughly twenty-five employees, the job changes, and it stays changed for
the rest of the company's life: the founder's primary job becomes building a great
*company* — the systems, structure, and culture that let the product keep getting built
well without the founder personally touching every decision.

A specific trap makes this transition harder than it needs to be: a completely flat
structure, with everyone reporting to the founder, works remarkably well up to a point —
and then it doesn't, suddenly, rather than gradually. What worked fine at twenty
employees becomes unworkable at thirty, seemingly overnight, because the failure is one
of coordination overhead compounding past a threshold, not a gradual decline anyone
notices coming. The fix isn't complicated: every employee needs exactly one manager, and
every manager needs to know who their direct reports are. Companies that try to look
innovative by avoiding any real structure, or that overcomplicate it with matrixed,
multiple-reporting-line arrangements to make people feel important, both make the same
underlying mistake in opposite directions.

## Why It's True: Four Ways Founders Fail as Managers, and What Actually Needs Building

### Four common failure modes, in order of how often Altman has seen them

**Being afraid to hire senior people.** Early on, this instinct is correct — aptitude and
belief in the mission matter more than experience for a company's first hires. Once a
company is scaling, the same instinct becomes a liability: an experienced executive who's
run a team like this one before can take over an entire function and make it work, and
nearly every founder who finally hires one says the same thing afterward — they wish
they'd done it sooner.

**"Hero mode."** A manager, often running support or a similarly ticket-driven function,
responds to growing demand by working harder personally rather than hiring more people —
leading by extreme, unsustainable example instead of accepting the short-term pain of
building out a team properly. The fix requires a real, uncomfortable tradeoff: falling
behind for a few weeks while hiring, rather than compensating personally forever.

**Bad delegation.** The common, wrong version looks like this: "go research this and
come back with the tradeoffs, and I'll decide." The better version: "you're smart, that's
why I hired you — here's context, here's what I think, but you decide, and tell me what
you chose." The first approach doesn't scale and doesn't build trust. The second does,
even though it feels riskier in the moment.

**No personal organization system.** Building product doesn't require much personal
organization. Managing a growing team does — tracking what each person is working on,
what needs following up, and what you told them last time you spoke — and founders who
skip building this system for themselves find it catching up with them exactly when the
company can least afford the confusion.

### Write down the how and the why, or someone else will write it for you

Chapter 6 already covered writing down a company's core *values*. This chapter adds a
related, more operational discipline: writing down *how* the company actually does
things — how code gets shipped, how customer support gets handled, how decisions get
made — the practices that used to spread naturally over shared lunches when the company
was small enough for everyone to just absorb them by proximity. Skip this, and the
company doesn't end up with no culture — it ends up with an *accidental* one, transmitted
randomly by whichever manager or peer happens to onboard each new hire. Write it down,
and a founder gets to be the one who actually sets the standard, rather than discovering
years later that it drifted somewhere they never chose.

### HR is not the TV-sitcom version, and it's worth taking seriously

Most founders resist formal HR because of a specific, inaccurate mental picture — office
politics, box-checking, bureaucracy for its own sake. Done properly, HR is closer to the
opposite: a clear structure, a real path for career growth, and frequent, honest
performance feedback that actually speeds a company up rather than slowing it down.
Concretely, this means: **compensation bands** instead of ad hoc, individually negotiated
pay that inevitably becomes visible and inevitably feels unfair once it does; **continuing
equity grants**, not just generous grants to the first ten hires (Chapter 5) — a
healthy, growing company should expect to distribute something like 3 to 5% of its
equity to employees every year, indefinitely, and needs a real system (not a shared
spreadsheet) to track it once mistakes in that tracking can cost real money; **a formal
hiring process**, including a full-time recruiter once the company can actually absorb
hires at volume — brought on too early, before there's real product-market fit, this
tends to produce hiring for its own sake rather than hiring for a real need; **announcing
every offer internally before it goes out**, since someone already at the company
frequently knows something useful about a candidate that the hiring manager doesn't; and
active attention to **diversity**, especially on early engineering teams, where a
homogeneous first fifteen or twenty hires tends to calcify into a monoculture that's
difficult and slow to correct later. New compliance requirements — sexual harassment and
diversity training among them — also kick in at the 50-employee mark in many
jurisdictions, worth knowing about before it becomes an unpleasant surprise.

One more HR-adjacent responsibility easy to neglect: actively watching for **burnout**
across the team, not just in yourself. What worked as an all-consuming sprint for the
company's first year or two becomes unsustainable as a permanent operating mode, and a
team that burns out all at once, rather than staggered, can be close to company-ending —
worth deliberately building in real rest, not just tolerating it when someone happens to
take it.

### Productivity degrades on its own unless you actively fight it

Left alone, a growing company's productivity doesn't just fail to increase with
headcount — it can decline, because the number of possible communication pairs between
employees grows roughly with the *square* of the total headcount, not linearly with it.
The single most effective countermeasure is **alignment**: making sure everyone in the
company can name the same handful of current priorities, which is exactly the test that
opened this chapter. Most founders wildly overestimate how well their own team already
does this, because a goal stated once, months ago, doesn't function as a live priority —
it has to be repeated far more often than feels necessary, reinforced in every all-hands,
every one-on-one, every planning document, to actually stay top of mind across a growing
team.

Process itself should serve the product, not become an end unto itself — a useful,
lightweight forcing function some companies use is a simple commitment to ship something,
however small, every single day, which keeps the whole organization oriented toward
delivery rather than internal process for its own sake. Underneath that, a basic
operating rhythm matters more than most founders expect: a weekly meeting with direct
reports, a monthly all-hands reviewing results and the roadmap, quarterly planning tied
to the company's yearly goals, and — genuinely underused, per Altman's own account —
periodic offsites that pull the company's best people out of daily operations
specifically to think about what's not getting done that should be.

### The unglamorous mechanics that become urgent around month 18

A handful of operational tasks tend to get ignored during a company's earliest phase,
reasonably, since they're not writing code or talking to users — and then become urgent
once things are actually working, typically somewhere around month 18. Proper accounting
and annual audits should start around this point, alongside a systematic effort to
collect and organize every legal document the company has ever signed (Chapter 15
already covered why this matters, and why it's much easier to do this before a
high-stakes fundraise or acquisition than during one). Provisional patents are worth
filing within roughly eleven months of first publicly discussing a product — patent law
typically gives a twelve-month window after public disclosure, and a provisional filing
is inexpensive and simply holds a company's place in line while it decides whether a full
patent is worth pursuing. Trademarks, in the U.S. and in major international markets,
are worth locking down around the same time, along with any domains a company hasn't
already claimed. A dedicated FP&A (financial planning and analysis) hire, building a real
financial model of the business, tends to get hired far later than it should — some of
the most useful models built for fast-growing companies run to well over a thousand
lines of detail, and the insight that level of modeling produces is available much
earlier than most founders assume to look for it. The same logic applies to hiring a
dedicated, full-time fundraiser once a company is past its Series B — a specialist
focused entirely on the next round tends to produce a meaningfully better outcome, at
lower cost, than treating each future raise as an ad hoc process layered on top of
everything else a founder is already doing.

### Founder psychology gets worse, not better, and that's the actual reason companies die

A specific, honest warning worth stating plainly: the emotional experience of running a
company does not get easier as it grows and succeeds. The highs get better. The lows get
worse, and the swings between them tend to widen rather than narrow. Alongside that,
success changes how the outside world treats a founder in a way nobody fully prepares
for: an early-stage founder is generally an underdog people root for; a visibly
successful one increasingly becomes a target for online criticism and, eventually,
skeptical press coverage — a transition worth making peace with in advance rather than
being blindsided by later.

Two specific, avoidable ways founders lose their footing once things start working: first,
losing focus — accepting invitations to advise other companies, speak at conferences, and
generally enjoy the trappings of success that follow a company doing well, at the exact
moment sustained focus on the actual business matters as much as ever. Second, getting
distracted by acquisition conversations — informal interest from potential acquirers is
genuinely flattering, and engaging with it too early or too often is a recognizable
company-killer: it's a real distraction, it's demoralizing when nothing comes of it, and
if a real offer does arrive, a team that's already mentally moved toward selling tends to
undervalue itself and accept less than the company was actually worth. The practical
rule: never start a real acquisition conversation unless you're genuinely willing to sell
at whatever number is actually on the table, not the number you're hoping to hear.

The single most important claim in this section, worth stating directly: when startups
fail, the actual proximate cause, more often than competition or a bad market or a
technical failure, is that the founders themselves gave up. Managing your own psychology
well enough to keep going — not blindly, but honestly — is one of the highest-leverage
skills a founder can build, precisely because it's the thing most likely to determine
whether the company gets the chance to succeed at all.

### Marketing, PR, and business development at scale

Chapter 12 already covered why press mostly doesn't matter early on. Once a company is
working, that changes, and two specific responsibilities shift back onto the founder
personally rather than being fully delegated: defining the company's actual key
messaging (never fully outsourced to a marketing hire or a PR firm), and maintaining
direct, personal relationships with a small number of journalists, for exactly the
reasons Chapter 12 already laid out about referrals and trust. Business development,
similarly, only becomes relevant once a real product exists — Chapter 16's persistence
and warm-introduction lessons apply directly here too — and Altman's own compressed
version of what actually makes deals happen adds two further points worth naming: a
genuine personal connection with the other side of any significant deal, rather than
treating them as a purely transactional counterparty; and real **competitive
dynamics** — having, or credibly appearing to have, another option on the table, since
that alternative is usually what actually moves a negotiation, more than any argument
made in the room. And, simply: ask directly for what you actually want in a deal, even
when it feels aggressive to say it plainly. Most of the time, nobody laughs a founder out
of the room for asking — they just don't get what they never asked for.

## Common Mistakes

- **Keeping a fully flat structure past the point where it works**, or replacing it with
  an overcomplicated, multi-manager structure designed to make people feel important
  rather than to actually clarify who reports to whom.
- **Staying in "hero mode" instead of hiring**, absorbing unsustainable personal strain
  rather than accepting a short-term backlog while a real team gets built.
- **Assuming a goal stated once, months ago, is still a live priority** — Altman's own
  "ask ten random employees" test reveals how rarely this assumption holds up.
- **Treating HR as bureaucratic overhead rather than genuine infrastructure** — real
  compensation bands, ongoing equity grants, and a formal hiring process all speed a
  company up, not slow it down.
- **Engaging seriously with acquisition conversations without being genuinely willing to
  sell at the actual number on the table.**
- **Assuming running a company gets emotionally easier as it succeeds.** It doesn't —
  the highs improve, but the lows get worse, and founders who expect otherwise are
  caught off guard by exactly the swings that end up determining whether they keep
  going.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**Does every company really need all of this scaling infrastructure by month 18?** The
timing is a rough guide, not a strict deadline — the underlying principle is that these
are the categories of work worth having on your radar *before* they become urgent, not
that every company needs to execute on all of them at exactly eighteen months regardless
of its actual size or trajectory. A company still well short of product-market fit at
that point has bigger, more immediate problems; a company scaling unusually fast might
need several of these earlier.

**Isn't "founders give up" an oversimplified explanation for startup failure?** It's
worth taking seriously rather than dismissing, precisely because it's an uncomfortable
claim: most founders, and most outside observers, prefer to attribute failure to external
causes (competition, market timing, funding) because those explanations don't require
examining a founder's own choices. Altman's claim isn't that external factors don't
matter — it's that, holding those factors constant, the proximate decision to stop
trying is where most failures actually complete themselves, which is a claim about where
to place attention (a founder's own psychology and sustained willingness to continue),
not a claim that outside factors are irrelevant.

**How does this chapter's advice on structure relate to Chapter 19's editor metaphor?**
Directly — this chapter covers *when* and *why* a company needs real management
structure in the first place; Chapter 19 covers *how* to actually operate once that
structure exists. Read together, they describe a single continuous transition: from a
founder who does everything personally, to a founder who edits, delegates, and sets the
standard for a company that no longer needs them in every room.

## Applying the Test

Run Altman's own test on your own company, or a hypothetical one: if you asked ten
random employees what the top three priorities are right now, would they give you the
same three, in roughly the same order? If you're not confident they would, that's not a
hypothetical problem — it's the specific, checkable symptom of a misalignment that's
already costing you productivity, whether or not it's visible yet.

## Chapter Summary

- A founder's job shifts from building a great product to building a great company
  around twenty-five employees — flat structure works until it suddenly doesn't.
- The four common failure modes as founders become managers: fear of hiring senior
  people, "hero mode," bad delegation, and a missing personal organization system.
- Write down not just your values (Chapter 6) but your actual practices — how things get
  done — or the company will develop an accidental culture instead of a chosen one.
- Real HR — compensation bands, continuing equity grants, a formal hiring process,
  attention to diversity — speeds a company up rather than slowing it down.
- Productivity degrades on its own as headcount grows; alignment around a small,
  constantly-repeated set of priorities is the main countermeasure.
- A cluster of unglamorous mechanical tasks (accounting, patents, trademarks, financial
  modeling, a dedicated fundraiser) become urgent around month 18 — worth having on your
  radar well before then.
- Founder psychology gets harder, not easier, as a company succeeds, and the actual
  proximate cause of most startup failures is founders giving up — managing your own
  psychology is one of the highest-leverage skills available.
- Marketing, PR, and business development all shift back toward direct founder
  involvement once a company is working, rather than being fully delegated away.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** Explain why flat organizational structure is described as failing "all at
   once" rather than gradually, and what the minimum viable fix actually requires.
2. **Apply:** Run Altman's alignment test on a real team you're part of (or imagine
   running it): would ten random people name the same three priorities? If you're
   unsure, what would you need to do to find out honestly?
3. **Apply:** Pick one of the "month 18" mechanical tasks (patents, FP&A, a dedicated
   fundraiser) and describe what getting ahead of it, rather than reacting to it late,
   would concretely look like for your own company.
4. **Confront a counterexample:** This chapter claims founders giving up is the actual
   proximate cause of most startup failures, more than competition or market timing. Can
   you think of a real, well-documented startup failure that seems to have been caused
   primarily by external factors rather than founder psychology? Does it genuinely
   contradict this chapter's claim, or does a closer look complicate that reading?
