# Chapter 1 — Why Start a Startup

There is a scene near the start of the movie *The Social Network*. Mark Zuckerberg's
Harvard dorm room fills up with laptops, cheap liquor, and a coder pouring champagne
over someone's keyboard while a crowd cheers. It is a good scene. It is also, according
to Dustin Moskovitz, almost entirely fiction. Moskovitz co-founded Facebook with
Zuckerberg, so he was actually there. The real version of that photo — the one Moskovitz
showed a Stanford class in 2014 — is a picture of Zuckerberg sitting alone at a desk,
head down, typing. There are two photos of him in that exact same position, taken on
different days, wearing different clothes. That is the whole scene. A guy at a desk, for
years.

Moskovitz was not being modest. He was making a point, and this book starts from the
same point: almost everything people believe about why they want to start a company is
wrong. Believing the wrong reasons is not a small mistake. It is the main reason most
people who start companies for those reasons quit, burn out, or build something nobody
needed. Before this book teaches you anything about ideas, products, fundraising, or
growth, it needs to talk you out of the reasons you probably think you have.

## This Book Is About One Kind of Company

First, a boundary. Sam Altman opened the Stanford class this book is built from with a
line that the whole rest of the book depends on:

> All of the advice in this class is geared towards people starting a business where the
> goal is hyper growth and eventually building a very large company... if you try to do
> these things in a lot of big companies or non-startups, it won't work.

There is a good, respectable way to build a small business: a consulting firm, a local
service company, a shop, a small business that supports you and a few employees forever
without ever trying to get big. Nothing in this book argues against that path. But
nothing in this book is *for* it either. This book is about a different, less common kind
of company — one built to grow fast, take money from investors, and try to become large.
The psychology, the tactics, and the mechanics in this book are built for that goal only.

A fast-growing, investor-backed company and a steady small business are not the same
thing wearing different clothes. They need different founders, different money, and
different definitions of success. If you are not sure which one you want, it is worth
figuring that out before you read Chapter 2. The two paths call for different kinds of
people.

With that boundary set, assume you *do* want the fast-growth kind. Why would you do it?

## The Core Idea: Four Reasons That Sound Right, and One That Isn't Really a Reason

Dustin Moskovitz, in that same lecture, listed four reasons people commonly give for
wanting to start a company:

1. It's glamorous.
2. You get to be the boss.
3. You get flexibility over your own schedule.
4. You'll make more money and have more impact than you would at an existing company.

All four are wrong, or at least wrong enough to be dangerous if they are your *main*
reason. Each one falls apart after about ten minutes of contact with an actual startup.
What replaces them is not a fifth item on the list. It's a different kind of test.
Moskovitz summed it up as: *you can't not do it.*

The rest of this chapter does three things. First, it shows why each of the four common
reasons fails. Second, it explains what "you can't not do it" actually means. Third, it
looks at where the edges of this advice are — because "should I do this" has real answers
that depend on your situation. It isn't a slogan.

## Why the Four Reasons Fail

### "It's glamorous"

The glamour comes from movies and culture, not from real data. *The Social Network* cut
together parties and big "eureka" moments like a highlight reel, and the highlight reel
is what people remember. What doesn't make it into the movie is what Moskovitz actually
lived through while running Facebook and later Asana: sitting at a desk, answering
support emails, doing sales, working through hard engineering problems, for years, with
no editing.

*The Economist* ran a piece in 2014 called "Entrepreneurs anonymous." It described
founders hiding under their desks and talking openly — maybe for the first time in the
business press — about founder depression. That is not a made-up story to sell
magazines. It describes a real risk of the job, and it comes from a simple fact: you
carry the fear of failure not just for yourself, but for everyone who left a stable job
to follow you. Sometimes people depend on you financially. Even when they don't, they've
given you the best years of their working life, and you are responsible for what that
turns out to be worth. Moskovitz said it plainly, describing his own early years at
Facebook: "I got really unhealthy, I wasn't exercising, I had a lot of anxiety actually
throughout my back, like almost every six months, when I was twenty-one or twenty-two."
That is not glamour. That is a 22-year-old with a repeating stress injury.

There's also a problem that's hard to undo, and the glamour story never mentions it. An
unhappy employee can quit. An unhappy founder mostly can't — not without real cost to the
people who joined because of them. Moskovitz's rough estimate: if a startup is going
well, you're committed for around ten years. If it's going badly, more like five — three
years to realize it isn't working, then two more years finding a soft landing for the
company and its people. Leaving earlier than that doesn't just cost you. It costs
everyone who trusted the path you were selling them.

None of this means startups are miserable. It means the glamour is something sold to you
from the outside, and if it's your reason for starting a company, the first real year
will remove that reason completely. You'll be left needing a better one, and you won't
have it.

> **Sidebar: What's Changed Since 2014**
>
> When this course was taught, talking openly about founder depression was still new
> enough that a major magazine covering it counted as news. That has changed a lot
> since. Y Combinator itself now runs mental-health-focused support for founders, and it
> is common today for accelerators, investors, and founder communities to treat burnout
> and depression as a normal, expected part of the job — not a shameful secret. The
> underlying reality this chapter describes has not changed. What has changed is that
> founders now have more permission to talk about it, and more support built
> specifically to help them, than they did in 2014.

### "You get to be the boss"

This is probably the most tempting of the four reasons, especially if you've ever worked
for a manager whose decisions seemed obviously bad. The fantasy goes: I'll start my own
thing, and I'll be the one making the decisions, and they'll be right, because I'm the
one making them.

Moskovitz quoted Phil Libin, who was CEO of Evernote at the time, on what the job
actually is:

> People have this vision of being the CEO of a company they started and being on top of
> the pyramid. Some people are motivated by that, but that's not at all what it's like.
> What it's really like: everyone else is your boss — all of your employees, customers,
> partners, users, media are your boss. I've never had more bosses and needed to account
> for more people today. The life of most CEOs is reporting to everyone else, at least
> that's what it feels like to me and most CEOs I know. If you want to exercise power and
> authority over people, join the military or go into politics. Don't be an entrepreneur.

There's a second point here that's easy to miss. The managers you thought were making
obviously bad calls at your old job probably weren't idiots. They were making hard
tradeoffs with imperfect information, while several people pulled them in different
directions — the exact spot you'll be in within your first month as a founder. Most of a
CEO's actual time, Moskovitz found, goes to settling conflicts other people bring to
them: two customers who want opposite things, two employees who disagree about
direction. You don't get to just decide what matters and go do it. You get to decide who
you're going to disappoint the least, over and over, all day.

### "You get flexibility"

This is really a smaller version of the "be the boss" fantasy — control over your own
schedule — and it fails for a related reason. Libin again, on this point:

> If you're going to be an entrepreneur, you will actually get some flex time to be
> honest. You'll be able to work any 24 hours a day you want!

You are always on call, whether you meant to be or not, because you don't get to choose
which part of the day something important happens. You are also, like it or not, the
emotional thermostat of your own company: your employees copy your energy level. Slow
down for a week because you're tired, and your team notices, and they slow down too. And
if you truly care about the problem you're solving, that passion pulls you toward the
work anyway, no matter what your calendar says about weekends.

There is one honest exception, and it matters enough to get its own section below: the
small "lifestyle business" path, where a four-day week or something close to a Tim
Ferriss-style light schedule can genuinely work — but only if you deliberately keep the
company small. The moment more than two or three people depend on you, that model stops
working, and you're back to full-time commitment whether you planned for it or not.

### "You'll make more money and have more impact"

This reason sounds the most rational, so it deserves the closest look. The numbers are
less clearly in the founder's favor than most people assume.

Moskovitz built a comparison, using 2014 figures, between joining an already-successful
company as an early employee and starting your own company from nothing:

| Path | Rough outcome (2014 figures) |
|---|---|
| Employee #100 at Dropbox (joined a couple of years in) | About $10M in upside already locked in, with more growth ahead |
| Employee #100 at Facebook (joined a couple of years in) | About $200M |
| Employee #1,000 at Facebook (joined around 2009) | About $20M |
| Founder of a made-up "Uber for Pet Sitting," building a $100M company, ending up owning roughly 10% of it (after selling shares to investors and giving some to early employees along the way) | About $10M |
| Founder of a made-up "Uber for Space Travel," building a $2B company | Much larger, but this requires a truly huge idea that doesn't exist yet |

This table is not an argument against starting a company. It's an argument against
starting one *only for the expected payoff*. The math only favors founding if you are
extremely confident you can build something in the $100M–$2B range. And you should have
more confidence in a company that already exists and is growing fast, like Facebook in
2009 or Dropbox in 2014, than in an idea you haven't even started yet.

More importantly, the "impact" half of this reason doesn't require founding anything at
all. Bret Taylor joined Google as roughly its 1,500th employee and built Google Maps
there — a product used by hundreds of millions of people, with no company of his own
needed. Justin Rosenstein joined Google a bit later, built an early prototype of what
became Gchat as a side project, then moved to Facebook as roughly employee 250, where he
led the hackathon project that became the Like button — one of the most-used pieces of
interface on the internet. That project likely could not have existed as its own
startup: it needed Facebook's huge user base to work at all. Both of these are enormous-
impact careers, and neither one required starting a company, raising money, or giving up
any ownership to get there. If impact is really your goal, joining a company that's
already moving fast can get you there faster than starting one from zero.

None of this means money and impact don't matter. It means: if they are your *reason*,
an honest look at the numbers usually points somewhere other than founding a company. If
you still want to found one after doing that math, your real reason is something else.
That "something else" is what the next section is about.

## The Test That Actually Works: "You Can't Not Do It"

Moskovitz's own answer, once he gets to it, is short: you start a company when you can't
not do it. He means this in two ways, and both need to be true at once.

**You're compelled.** Not "interested in" — compelled. This matters because you will
need that level of pull to survive the real difficulties described above. It also
matters because you can't fake it well enough for it to work. Moskovitz's claim: strong
job candidates can sense an unconvincing founder, and there are enough founders out there
with the real thing that talented people don't need to take a chance on someone who's
only somewhat interested. Your own subconscious, he adds, also tends to know the
difference — and that tends to show up in ways that hurt you before you even notice.

**The world needs it.** This checks that your compulsion isn't just a personal itch. If
the problem genuinely doesn't need solving, or doesn't need *you* specifically to solve
it, that's a sign your time is better spent somewhere else — there's no shortage of real
problems, so there's no reason to invent one. And if it's a real problem but you're not
the right person for it, Moskovitz's honest worst case is this: you go do it anyway, you
beat the team that actually should have built it, and the world ends up with a worse
version of the thing it needed. That's not a hypothetical to feel bad about later. It's a
reason to be honest with yourself about fit before you start.

Moskovitz's own example is Asana. He and his co-founder Justin Rosenstein were, in his
words, "reluctant entrepreneurs" — they were already working at Facebook on something
genuinely important. At night, after a full day at their real jobs, they kept building an
internal task-management tool, because they could see it solving something real inside
the company, and no one else seemed to be solving it well anywhere. Eventually they had
what he called "the hard conversation": what happens if we don't start this company? They
had seen the impact inside Facebook. They believed it would matter to the world. They
were convinced no one else was about to build the right version of it. That combination —
*I can't stop working on this, and if I don't, something valuable doesn't happen* — is
what Moskovitz means when he says the idea was "beating itself out of our chests." It's a
specific, checkable feeling, not just a stand-in for enthusiasm.

The same pattern shows up outside this course's original 2014 lecture hall, too. Melanie
Perkins, co-founder of the design tool Canva, spent about three years pitching investors
before one finally said yes — by her own account, more than a hundred said no first. What
kept her going wasn't stubbornness for its own sake. Years earlier, teaching university
students how to use a clunky, hard-to-learn design program, she'd watched them struggle
with software that should have been simple. She built her first product — a much simpler
tool for designing school yearbooks — to fix that one specific problem, long before Canva
existed. By the time a hundred investors were telling her no, she wasn't chasing an
idea that merely sounded good. She had already been living inside the problem for years.
Canva later being worth billions of dollars doesn't prove the test right on its own.
What proves it is that she kept building through a hundred rejections in a row — which
only makes sense if the "can't not do it" half of the test was already true, well before
anyone else agreed with her.

## Common Mistakes

Founders talk themselves into starting for the wrong reasons in a few common ways:

- **Treating this as a two-to-three-year plan.** A common pattern, especially with
  student founders, is assuming they'll do the startup thing for a couple of years and
  then move on to what they're "really" passionate about. Moskovitz and Altman both call
  this close to a universal mistake. Real companies — the ones that work — usually take
  a decade. If you don't love what you're building, you're likely to give up around year
  three or four, right when the real payoff would have started arriving.
- **Wanting the identity, not the problem.** Wanting to *be a founder* is different from
  being pulled toward a specific problem. Someone motivated by the identity tends to pick
  whatever idea is available, rather than the one they can't stop thinking about. That
  produces exactly the kind of copycat, uninspired company that struggles to hire and
  struggles to survive year three.
- **Mistaking flexibility for freedom.** Believing the "control your own schedule" pitch,
  then being surprised — and often ashamed, since people don't talk about this much —
  when the job turns out to be always-on. That shame makes things worse: founders who
  feel like they're the only ones struggling this way are less likely to build the
  support network the burnout answer below says you need.
- **Doing the money math and stopping there.** Running the comparison in the table above
  and treating the dollar number as the whole decision, instead of using it as a filter.
  If you're not confident you're building something in the Facebook/Dropbox class, the
  money argument for founding doesn't hold up on its own — you need a different reason if
  you're going to go ahead anyway.
- **Ignoring how hard this is to reverse.** Underestimating how committed you become the
  moment you take money and hire people. Quitting a startup a year in doesn't just cost
  you — in Moskovitz's words, it hurts your reputation "for the rest of your career," and
  it costs everyone who joined because of the path you presented to them.

## Tradeoffs and Edge Cases

**The lifestyle-business exception is real, and it's not a lesser choice — it's a
different project.** If you want a lifestyle business — something that supports two or
three people forever, without needing to grow fast or take outside money — a flexible,
Tim Ferriss-style schedule can genuinely work, as long as you deliberately keep it small.
That's a real, legitimate choice. But it comes with a different set of tradeoffs than
everything else in this book assumes, which is why this chapter opened by drawing the
line clearly: this book is written for the fast-growth path. If you're building the
other kind of company, treat every "you must give everything to this" claim in the
chapters ahead as conditional on the goal you've chosen.

> **Sidebar: The Rise of the "Calm Company"**
>
> This lecture was given in 2014, near the peak of an era when "hyper-growth or
> nothing" was close to the only story Silicon Valley told about startups. In the years
> since, a real counter-movement has grown up around exactly the path this chapter
> treats as a footnote. Basecamp's founders wrote a whole book, *It Doesn't Have to Be
> Crazy at Work*, arguing against always-on hustle culture. The "indie hacker" movement —
> solo or small-team founders building profitable, modest-growth software companies
> without ever raising outside money — now has its own communities, conferences, and
> well-known success stories. Investor Aileen Lee coined the term "unicorn" in 2013 for
> a $1B+ startup; a group of founders later coined a deliberate counter-term, "zebra,"
> for companies built for sustainable profit instead of hypergrowth, arguing those
> deserve just as much respect. None of this contradicts what this book teaches — a
> fast-growing, investor-backed company is still a real and valuable thing to build. But
> it is worth knowing, before you commit to the path this book describes, that
> "hyper-growth or you're not a real founder" is itself a choice this book is making on
> purpose. It is not the only option that exists.

**Should you do this in college?** Paul Graham, teaching the same course, said no —
firmly. His reasoning: starting a company "changes your life irrevocably" (in a way you
can't take back), the same way having kids does. There are things you can only do easily
before that happens: travel with no deadline, follow a project that seems to have no
payoff, meet dozens of potential future co-founders in a place built exactly for that.
Once a startup is working, those options close. Graham's view: you're not a student who
happens to also run a company. If the startup succeeds, "you're not a student anymore,"
no matter what your enrollment status says. His advice, aimed at an audience of college
students: "Be a real student and not start a startup or start a real startup and not be a
student." He argues twenty usually isn't the best age to do this — not because young
founders can't succeed (some clearly do), but because the serendipity you give up by
succeeding early is a real cost. And it's a cost you never get to see, because if you
succeed at twenty, you never find out what the other path would have given you. Larry
Page, in Graham's telling, started running at twenty-five and hasn't stopped since: he
can't backpack anywhere without it turning into either a state visit or a security
operation. Graham's line about YC's own successful founders: "it never gets any easier."
The problems change — construction delays on a new London office instead of a broken air
conditioner in a studio apartment — but the total amount of worry doesn't go down. It may
go up.

**You genuinely can't tell in advance whether you're capable of this.** Graham makes
this point directly, and it's worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as false
modesty: your life so far has probably given you a decent read on whether you could
become, say, a professional athlete or a mathematician. It has probably given you almost
no read on whether you could become a startup founder — because starting a company
changes you. What you're really trying to guess isn't what you are now, but what you
could become, and that's close to unknowable from the outside, and often from the inside
too. Graham's report, after nine years of picking founders at Y Combinator: confidence
going in doesn't predict outcomes. The confident applicants who expected to do great were
no more likely to succeed than the ones who arrived wondering if Y Combinator had made a
mistake letting them in. His practical rule: if you are *completely* terrified of the
idea, you probably shouldn't do it, unless you're someone who's specifically energized by
doing scary things. If you're merely unsure — which is far more common — the only way to
really find out is to try it. Graham's addition, aimed at the college-age reader
specifically: try it, "just not now."

**Burnout is not something you can plan your way around. It's a recurring cost you
manage, not solve.** A student in the same course asked how to handle burnout while
staying productive. Sam Altman's answer was deliberately unglamorous: "it sucks and you
keep going." The advice that works for employees — take a vacation, recharge, come back —
mostly doesn't work for founders, because a founder's problems don't pause while they're
away. They pile up. What actually helps: a support network you build *before* you need
it, and a willingness to deal with the real problem directly instead of waiting for the
feeling to pass on its own. Founder depression, Altman is direct about this, is serious
and real, not a minor inconvenience — which is exactly why "it's glamorous" and "you'll
have flexibility" are such dangerous reasons to start a company. They set you up to be
blindsided by a cost that nobody advertises.

## Applying the Test

Think about two people, both with an idea for the same kind of product.

The first has spent two years frustrated by one specific, narrow problem in her own
work. She has already built three rough versions of a fix for herself, because she
couldn't stop thinking about it. She describes the market not in terms of how big it
could eventually get, but in terms of who is underserved right now. Ask her why she'd do
this instead of joining an existing company working on something similar, and she
doesn't really have an answer beyond: *I'm already doing this, at night and on weekends,
and I don't seem to be able to stop.*

The second has read about the funding rounds and the acquisition news. He likes the idea
of being a founder. He picked this particular idea three weeks ago because it sounded
like a defensible space with a large potential market. Ask him why this instead of
joining a fast-growing company already working nearby, and the honest answer is closer
to: *because I want to be the one who gets the credit.*

You don't need to read anyone's mind to tell these two apart. The first person passes
"you can't not do it" on both halves: she is compelled, and she already has evidence the
problem is real, because she has been living inside it. The second person hasn't shown
either half — he's justifying a decision he made for reasons this chapter just spent
several thousand words explaining don't hold up. Neither movie-style glamour, nor the
fantasy of being the boss, nor the promise of flexibility, nor the raw money math got the
first founder to her decision. All four of them are exactly what got the second founder
to his. That's the test, and you can run it on yourself before you ever run it on anyone
else.

## Chapter Summary

- This book is about venture-backed, fast-growth-oriented companies specifically — not
  lifestyle businesses, which are a legitimate but different path with different rules.
- Four common reasons for starting a company — it's glamorous, you get to be the boss,
  you get flexibility, you'll make more money and have more impact — all fall apart under
  a closer look, and are dangerous as your main motivation.
- The glamour story hides a real cost: founder depression is well documented, and
  leaving a struggling company early has lasting professional and financial costs for you
  and everyone who joined you.
- "Being the boss" flips in practice — you end up reporting to everyone: employees,
  customers, partners, media.
- "Flexibility" flips the same way — you are on call at all hours, and your energy sets
  the tone for the whole team.
- The money-and-impact argument, done honestly, often favors joining an already-
  successful company over founding a new one. Bret Taylor and Justin Rosenstein built
  huge-impact products without ever starting a company.
- The real test is "you can't not do it": you're personally compelled, and the world
  (not just you) needs the thing built.
- You cannot reliably know in advance whether you're capable of this — confidence going
  in does not predict outcomes. So the honest answer to "am I ready" is usually: try it,
  if you're not terrified, and not right now if you're still in college.

## Exercises

1. **Recall:** In your own words, state the two conditions in Moskovitz's "you can't not
   do it" test, and explain why both are needed. Why would satisfying only one of them
   not be enough?
2. **Apply:** Take an idea you've thought about building a company around. Write down,
   as honestly as you can, which of the four tempting-but-wrong reasons (glamour, being
   the boss, flexibility, money/impact) is actually driving your motivation right now.
   Then write down what would have to be true for you to pass the "you can't not do it"
   test instead.
3. **Apply:** Using the comparison table in this chapter as a model, make your own rough
   guess: what would you likely earn, and what impact would you likely have, by joining
   the fastest-growing company you could realistically get hired at today — compared to
   founding the company you're considering? Where does the honest math point?
4. **Confront a counterexample:** Mark Zuckerberg started Facebook at nineteen, in
   college — directly against Paul Graham's advice in this chapter. Graham would likely
   say Zuckerberg is exactly the "extremely unlikely" case he mentioned: a side project
   that took off before its founder ever decided to run a company. Does that answer fully
   resolve the tension, or does it just relabel the exception? What would you need to see
   in your own situation to know whether you're a real outlier, versus someone
   rationalizing an exception that doesn't apply to you?

- Dustin Moskovitz's original "Why to Start a Startup" talk, delivered as part of
  Stanford's CS183B, is the main source for most of this chapter and is worth reading or
  watching directly.
- Paul Graham's essays on startup timing and founder psychology, mentioned throughout
  his CS183B lecture, go deeper into the "you can't tell" argument than the lecture
  format allowed.

  the piece Moskovitz points to on founder depression as a documented, not anecdotal,
  problem.

  the fullest case for the "calm company" alternative mentioned in this chapter's
  sidebar — useful if you suspect hyper-growth is not actually the goal you want.
