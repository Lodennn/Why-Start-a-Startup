# Book Architecture — How to Start a Startup: The Book

Status: Approved (Phase 1). Standing rule from approval: compression must never lose
information — cut/compressed material is restructured (main text, sidebar, or appendix),
never deleted.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this book, a reader with strong technical skills and zero business
background should be able to:

1. Evaluate a startup idea using the same mental models VCs and YC partners actually use
   (monopoly theory, market growth vs. market size, "why now").
2. Choose and structure a founding team, understand equity and vesting well enough to
   negotiate their own cap table without a lawyer translating for them.
3. Run a product-discovery process (user interviews, MVP scoping, retention/cohort
   analysis) without hand-waving.
4. Read and reason about a term sheet, a cap table, and basic financial statements.
5. Understand growth as an engineering discipline (retention curves, K-factor, CAC/LTV)
   rather than as "marketing magic."
6. Recognize the operating and management patterns that separate companies that scale
   from companies that stall at 20-50 employees.
7. Situate any specific piece of tactical advice inside the larger arc of a 10-year
   company-building project, not treat any one lecture's opinion as gospel.

## Reader Journey

The reader arrives knowing how to build software and nothing about why some software
companies become durable businesses and others don't. The book takes them through the
actual chronological arc of building a company - idea, team, product, growth, money,
scale - but at each stage stops to build the conceptual machinery (monopoly theory, unit
economics, cap table math) that the original lectures assumed or gestured at without
fully deriving. By the final chapter, the reader should be able to sit in a YC partner
meeting or a board meeting and understand every term being used.

## Dependency Graph

- Part One is load-bearing for the entire book - it introduces vocabulary (market, moat,
  monopoly, TAM) used everywhere after. Read first, always.
- Part Two (People) depends only loosely on Part One; could theoretically be read second
  or third.
- Part Three (Product) depends on Part One's idea-framing.
- Part Four (Growth) has a hard dependency on Part Three - you cannot discuss retention
  curves before a product exists to retain users in.
- Part Five (Money) depends on Parts One, Three, and Four for narrative context. Ch. 15
  (Legal Mechanics) is comparatively standalone/reference-like.
- Part Six is the capstone - every chapter assumes the company already has a team, a
  product, growth, and money, and is now hitting the 20-50 employee wall.

## What Gets Merged

- Growth: L4 + L6 -> two unified chapters instead of two disconnected lectures.
- Culture: L10 + L11 -> one arc from "define your values" to "culture at 150 people."
- Fundraising: L9 + L18 + L19 -> strategy, pitching mechanics, and legal mechanics
  separated into three chapters instead of interleaved.
- Execution/Management: L2 + L14 + L15 + L20 -> kept as separate chapters (different
  skills) but sequenced to build on each other rather than repeat.

## What Gets Expanded Beyond the Lectures

1. Financial statement literacy primer (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, burn/runway).
2. Cap table & dilution worked example across seed -> A -> B.
3. SAFE vs. convertible note vs. priced round, updated for post-2014 industry standard.
4. Vesting math with an actual cliff/schedule table.
5. Unit economics formulas (CAC, LTV, payback period) as a reusable worked example.
6. Negotiation fundamentals (BATNA, anchoring).
7. What a board actually does - voting vs. protective provisions.
8. "What's changed since 2014" sidebars (e.g., remote cofounders, SAFEs, PLG vs.
   sales-led growth).
9. Founder's Toolkit appendix - annotated bibliography of every essay/book referenced
   across the 20 lectures.

## What Gets Compressed (Never Deleted)

Per the standing rule: the following get restructured out of the main narrative flow but
every fact/number/quote they contain must still appear somewhere in the book (sidebar or
appendix).

- The verbatim roleplay transcript in L19 (Dalton/Qasar mock pitches) -> structured
  "good pitch / bad pitch" comparison in the main text; full transcript preserved as an
  appendix.
- The extended Toussaint Louverture narrative in L15 -> condensed case study in the main
  text preserving the substantive facts and the management lesson; fuller historical
  detail preserved in a sidebar.
- Dated tactical specifics (Clerky, inDinero, "Google AdWords Keyword Planner") ->
  retained as explicitly time-stamped historical illustrations, not current-day
  instructions.
